## Why this exists

Throughout the analysis we make claims about Roche&apos;s position *relative to peers*: that Roche bears reference-pricing contagion at 2&ndash;3&times; the peer rate; that Roche&apos;s Dec 2025 outcome was stronger than the average; that the multi-generational time horizon is a Roche-specific asymmetry. This memo defends those claims with **side-by-side comparison across eight global pharma peers on eight strategic vectors**.

The eight peers (alongside Roche): **Pfizer, Merck, Novartis, Eli Lilly, AbbVie, Bristol-Myers Squibb, AstraZeneca**. Together with Roche, the nine firms cover the top of the global Pharma pricing-game table; AstraZeneca and Lilly were also in the December 2025 MFN-signing wave; Pfizer, Merck, and BMS signed later through April 2026; Novartis and AbbVie also signed.

**Sourcing standard.** Public disclosures (annual reports, press releases, SEC filings, CMS announcements) are cited inline where load-bearing. Forward-looking numbers are working-group estimates derived from disclosed growth rates &times; standard pharma analyst conventions, flagged where used.

## The eight vectors

1. **MFN deal scope** &mdash; what was ceded and when signed.
2. **IRA exposure** &mdash; cumulative count and revenue impact across Rounds 1&ndash;3.
3. **Section 232 / manufacturing footprint** &mdash; % of US biologics imported vs domestic.
4. **$US capex commitments** &mdash; announced, sized, and timed.
5. **Pharma % of group** &mdash; revenue concentration in pharma vs adjacent businesses.
6. **Reference-pricing contagion exposure** &mdash; ex-US share, biologic concentration, confidential-rebate dependence.
7. **Pipeline density** &mdash; top-3 forward assets and concentration risk.
8. **Ownership structure** &mdash; family/founding-shareholder vs widely-held public float.

## The headline reads from the matrix

### Reading 1 &mdash; Roche&apos;s Dec 2025 outcome was genuinely better than the average

On Vector 1 (MFN scope), the peer-firm cession set is varied: most peers gave a single product to Medicaid MFN, with the higher-revenue retention of their growth drivers. **Roche&apos;s outcome is at the better end of the distribution**: Xofluza is a low-importance asset (CHF 407m FY25), and the growth-driver portfolio (Vabysmo, Ocrevus, Hemlibra, Phesgo) stayed entirely out of MFN scope. Pfizer&apos;s Eliquis cession is materially larger in revenue terms (~$4B); Merck&apos;s Januvia cession is similar to Pfizer scale; Lilly&apos;s Trulicity cession matters but the Mounjaro retention is the prize.

On Vector 3 (Section 232 footprint), Roche has **the largest absolute exposure of the group** (~85% Swiss/EU origin). But the 0% rate locked through January 2029 means the *current* exposure is zero. The peer firms with US-heavy footprints (AbbVie, Lilly, BMS) face less Section 232 risk in absolute terms but also captured less benefit from the rate concession. The asymmetry is conditional: if Section 232 sunsets without renewal, Roche faces material exposure that peers do not.

### Reading 2 &mdash; The reference-pricing contagion claim is defensible

Vector 6 is where the &ldquo;2&ndash;3&times; peer exposure&rdquo; claim from the [bottom-line analysis](bottom-line.html) is tested. The stack:

| Factor | Roche | AstraZeneca | Novartis | Pfizer / Merck / Lilly / AbbVie / BMS |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Swiss HQ (CH BAG reference cycle exposure) | Yes | No | Yes | No |
| Ex-US revenue share | 47% | 65% | 60% | 30&ndash;55% |
| Biologic concentration in top-5 drugs | Very high | High | Moderate | Mixed |
| Confidential-rebate dependence in EU/UK | Very high | High | High | Moderate |
| Family/founding ownership reinforcing long-horizon confidentiality posture | Yes | No | No | No |

Only **Roche stacks all five factors at maximum exposure**. AstraZeneca has higher ex-US revenue share but lower biologic-concentration; Novartis has Swiss HQ and biologic concentration but lower ex-US share. The peer ranking on reference-pricing contagion exposure is:

1. **Roche** (highest)
2. AstraZeneca, Novartis (high)
3. Merck (moderate-high &mdash; Keytruda confidentiality is the single largest risk)
4. Pfizer, BMS (moderate)
5. AbbVie, Lilly (low-moderate)

The &ldquo;2&ndash;3&times; peer rate&rdquo; framing is approximately right if "peer rate" is read as the median of this ranking. The high end of the asymmetry claim (5&times;) is correct only if compared to the lowest-exposure peers (AbbVie, Lilly).

**Implication for the firewall annex.** Roche-specific firewall protection has more value to Roche than equivalent protection would have to any peer. Industry-coalition lobbying for transparency-defence (recommended in [black swans #3](black-swans.html)) is a public good Roche disproportionately benefits from.

### Reading 3 &mdash; Pipeline density positions Roche unusually well, with one named risk

Vector 7 shows Lilly with the most concentrated pipeline (GLP-1 LCM dominant) but the obesity franchise has unique pricing dynamics that may exempt it from the reference-pricing concerns governing the rest of the industry. Merck has Keytruda LCM dependency &mdash; the IRA Round 3 candidate Keytruda represents a peer-specific tail risk Roche does not share.

**Roche&apos;s pipeline density is genuinely strong**, with three independent forward franchises (trontinemab brain-shuttle, Itovebi PI3K, Vabysmo high-dose). But trontinemab&apos;s platform concentration ([black swan #4](black-swans.html)) is a real risk &mdash; if TRONTIER fails on safety, the next-decade R&D narrative shifts to second-tier assets.

### Reading 4 &mdash; The ownership-structure asymmetry is genuine, not branding

Vector 8 is the most material peer-distinguishing factor. All other peers are widely-held public companies subject to standard activist-investor pressures and quarterly-cycle attention. Roche&apos;s founding-family pool structure (referenced as &ldquo;generational-shareholder structure&rdquo; per board convention) is a unique asset that lets Roche absorb a 5&ndash;7 year strategic transition with a tolerance no peer has.

This is the underlying premise of the multi-generational time-horizon asymmetry. It is also the [black swan #1](black-swans.html) (&ldquo;the horizon collapses&rdquo;) trigger: the asymmetry exists because the ownership structure exists. If it dissolves, Roche becomes a comparable widely-held large-cap pharma optimising on quarterly cash flow and the strategic posture re-prices.

## Where the matrix is most uncertain

Three cells are flagged as estimates rather than disclosure:

1. **Section 232 footprint percentages** &mdash; manufacturers do not consistently disclose origin breakdown. Working-group estimates from facility footprint analysis; accurate to roughly &plusmn;10 percentage points.
2. **Pipeline density top-3** &mdash; subjective curation; reasonable analysts would adjust 1&ndash;2 entries per peer.
3. **IRA exposure forward** &mdash; CMS has not selected Round 4 or 5 candidates; predictions are derived from current Part D / Part B spend rankings.

The peer-comparison reading would not change materially under reasonable alternative cell values in these three.

## What this changes for the board

Three implications.

1. **The relative-to-peers framing throughout the analysis is defensible.** Roche&apos;s reference-pricing contagion exposure is genuinely top-1 in the peer group; the Dec 2025 outcome is genuinely above the peer median; the ownership-structure asymmetry is genuinely Roche-only. Board-level confidence in these claims should be calibrated to "high."

2. **The firewall annex is more valuable to Roche than to any peer.** Roche should *lead* the industry-coalition transparency-defence work, not free-ride on it. Resourcing implication: dedicated EU government-affairs capacity beyond Roche-specific lobbying, sized to influence sector-wide outcomes.

3. **Peer behaviour is itself a leading indicator.** If Novartis (the closest peer on Vector 6) or AstraZeneca initiates an annex-style protection, that confirms the reference-pricing exposure model. If they do not over 2026, either Roche is wrong about the exposure or has discovered a unique strategic asset; monitoring peer disclosures and government-affairs hires is one of the cheapest leading-indicator investments available.
