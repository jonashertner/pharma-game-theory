# Item 1 unpacked

> The board memo asks for endorsement of "the firewall-annex approach as the primary 2026 negotiating priority — explicitly above MFN-coverage expansion or TrumpRx SKU growth." This page unpacks that sentence into precise actionable content, names every load-bearing assumption, and provides a fallback plan if assumptions fail.

**Snapshot:** 20 May 2026 · companion to [board recommendation](recommendation.html) and [red team](adversarial.html)

---

## What is being asked

The slogan: *"Endorse the firewall-annex approach as the primary 2026 negotiating priority — explicitly above MFN-coverage expansion or TrumpRx SKU growth."*

The precise content the board is endorsing — written so it can be voted on:

### 1.1 What is the firewall annex

A textual addition (an annex, an exchange of letters, or a side-agreement) to the **US–Switzerland–Liechtenstein Joint Statement of 14 November 2025**, agreed bilaterally between USTR and SECO, containing the following five operative provisions:

**Provision A — Confidential-net-price recognition.** The Parties recognise that confidential net prices negotiated between manufacturers and payers in the Parties' jurisdictions (including but not limited to AMNOG-style Erstattungsbeträge in Germany, Managed Entry Agreements in Italy, Patient Access Schemes in the UK, and equivalents in Switzerland) are commercially-sensitive information that shall not constitute the basis for MFN benchmark calculation under US Executive Order 14297 or successor instruments.

**Provision B — Symmetric scope.** This annex applies to all manufacturers signed onto the US MFN regime as of the annex's effective date, without distinction by manufacturer headquarters jurisdiction or by share of US sales.

**Provision C — Audit-rights preservation.** Both Parties retain audit rights over commercial agreements relevant to this annex through their respective competent authorities (USTR Office of Pharmaceuticals, Swiss FOPH, BAG), subject to standard commercial confidentiality protections.

**Provision D — Sunset and renewal.** This annex enters into force on signature and remains in force for five years, renewable by mutual consent for successive five-year periods.

**Provision E — Compliance reciprocity.** Both Parties commit to incorporate this annex's framework into their respective domestic pharma-pricing regimes — the US through interpretive guidance on EO 14297 implementation, Switzerland through corresponding FOPH guidance on LAMal Article 52a determinations.

The annex is approximately three to five pages of operative text plus a preamble and definitions.

### 1.2 What "primary 2026 negotiating priority" means operationally

For Roche's executive team and government-affairs organisation:

- *Resource allocation.* Government-affairs and trade-team time and budget allocated to securing the annex takes precedence over time spent on MFN-coverage-expansion discussions, TrumpRx SKU-onboarding negotiations, or Section-232 milestone-renegotiation work.
- *Public posture.* In public statements through Q3 2026, Roche emphasises the framework's stability and the importance of confidential-net-pricing for global pharmaceutical innovation. Roche does *not* publicly push for MFN coverage reduction, TrumpRx SKU reduction, or other contestable positions that could distract from the annex.
- *Internal negotiating posture.* In bilateral discussions with USTR, HHS, and CMS, Roche treats the annex as the bid. Other items (capex acceleration, OOP commitments, additional TrumpRx SKUs) are *trades* offered in exchange for annex movement.

### 1.3 What "explicitly above MFN-coverage expansion or TrumpRx SKU growth" means

Practically:

- If the US Executive proposes adding 5+ Roche/Genentech drugs to MFN coverage in exchange for confidentiality protection elsewhere, Roche declines and counter-proposes the firewall annex as the primary subject.
- If TrumpRx requests additional Genentech SKUs as a condition of continued framework stability, Roche offers minimal additional SKUs (1–3, not 5+) and ties any expansion to annex progress.
- If the Administration explicitly asks for the trade "MFN coverage expansion in exchange for confidentiality language," Roche resists this framing — the annex must stand or fall on its own merits, not as a trade.

### 1.4 What "endorse" means as a board action

Endorsement is approval of:

(a) The negotiating posture defined above.
(b) Allocation of approximately CHF 8–12M of additional government-affairs / trade-team / external-counsel budget for the 2026 Q3–Q4 window to pursue the annex.
(c) Authorisation for CEO Schinecker plus General Counsel plus Head of Government Affairs to engage personally with USTR leadership, the Swiss Federal Council, and the German pharma-industry establishment in support of the annex.
(d) Acceptance of the fallback plan (Section 5 below) if the annex is unachievable.

It is *not* approval of any specific concession Roche might be required to make in exchange for the annex — those concessions return for board approval if material.

## Twelve assumptions — explicitly named and questioned

The endorsement rests on twelve assumptions. Each has been challenged. The board should know which it is signing onto.

### Assumption 1: The US Administration would accept confidentiality language at all

**The case for.** The Administration's MFN program is structurally about *visible US-channel prices*. Confidentiality language about ex-US prices does not weaken the MFN program's core narrative; it merely clarifies that MFN benchmarking applies to *visible OECD prices*, not to *confidential commercial arrangements*. The Administration has no observable interest in ex-US confidential pricing collapsing.

**The case against.** The Administration's narrative is "American patients should pay no more than other countries." Confidentiality language could be characterised by political opponents as "Roche hides ex-US prices from US patients." The Administration's communications team may resist any annex that appears to *protect* manufacturer-private pricing information.

**What evidence would settle it.** A back-channel sounding by Swiss SECO to USTR leadership. Pre-negotiation acceptance of the *concept* of confidential-net-price recognition before tabling the annex.

**Current status.** Untested. The board endorsement should be *conditional* on a 60-day back-channel sounding confirming USTR receptivity.

### Assumption 2: The German MRA mechanism is the right template

**The case for.** The German Medizinforschungsgesetz (MFG, in force 1 January 2025) is the most recent successful precedent for confidential pricing in a major OECD economy. It is operational, has counsel-vetted text, and has not been challenged in court.

**The case against.** The MRA is a *domestic* German law for the German market only. It contains a 9% additional discount in exchange for confidentiality and an R&D-in-Germany requirement. Both elements may not translate to a US–Swiss bilateral context. Specifically: would Roche commit to an additional 9% discount on US-channel prices in exchange for the firewall? Would Roche commit to R&D-in-US obligations beyond the existing $50B capex pledge?

**What evidence would settle it.** Counsel review of MRA text and identification of which elements transfer to the US–Swiss annex and which do not. Likely conclusion: the *structural mechanic* of confidential net pricing transfers; the *quid-pro-quo elements* (9% discount + R&D-in-Germany) do not transfer one-for-one and must be re-negotiated.

**Current status.** The recommendation underspecifies which MRA elements transfer. The board should ask General Counsel to produce a specific transfer-and-translation memo before the negotiating posture is locked.

### Assumption 3: "Symmetric (not Roche-specific) language" is easier to negotiate

**The case for.** Symmetric language allows the Administration to sell the annex as cross-cutting policy rather than as Roche/Swiss-firm-specific carve-out. Politically the optics are dramatically better.

**The case against.** Other MFN signatories may have different ex-US exposures and may not want identical confidentiality protections. Pfizer's ex-US exposure is different from Roche's; AstraZeneca's is different from Lilly's. Symmetric language may be either (a) the lowest common denominator that is too weak to protect Roche's specific position, or (b) too strong for some signatories who actively want US-channel transparency.

**What evidence would settle it.** Quiet bilateral conversations between Roche and other MFN signatories (especially Novartis, AstraZeneca, GSK as Swiss/UK peers with similar exposure profiles) to understand whether symmetric language is achievable across the cohort.

**Current status.** Untested. The recommendation assumes symmetric language is achievable; the board should require evidence from peer-firm sounding within 60 days.

### Assumption 4: Q3 2026 timing is correct

**The case for.** The Section 232 effective date is 31 July 2026. Closing the annex before that date means it can be characterised as *clarification* of existing framework rather than *renegotiation*. After 31 July 2026, any addition is renegotiation, which is structurally harder.

**The case against.** Q3 2026 is 6–10 weeks away as of this writing (20 May 2026). The negotiation pace required to close in that window is unusually fast for international annexes. Pushing the timing creates pressure to accept suboptimal text. Q4 2026 or Q1 2027 may produce better text even at the cost of "renegotiation" framing.

**What evidence would settle it.** Diplomatic readiness assessment by SECO and USTR. If both teams confirm they can close by 31 July 2026 with full text and counsel review, proceed; if either signals they cannot, target Q1 2027 with renegotiation framing.

**Current status.** The recommendation locks Q3 2026 without verification that the timing is achievable. The board should soften this to "target Q3 2026, accept Q1 2027 if necessary for text quality."

### Assumption 5: The firewall annex is highest-value among Roche's available moves

**The case for.** The model identifies international-reference-pricing protection as Roche's highest-weight unused lever (10/10 weight on Roche, currently 3/10 floor, achievable 7/10).

**The case against.** The model's weights are derived from sourced public positions in the actor profiles. Public positions may understate or overstate Roche's actual private valuation of each issue. Specifically: the patent-exclusivity issue (preserving the IRA biologics 13-year exclusivity vs the IRA pill-penalty 9-year exclusivity for small molecules) may be more strategically important than the firewall over a 5–10 year horizon, particularly given the upcoming Ocrevus patent cliff (March 2029).

**What evidence would settle it.** Internal Roche valuation exercise comparing five-year revenue impact under (a) firewall annex secured but no exclusivity protection, vs (b) exclusivity protection secured but no firewall. If (b) > (a), the priority is wrong.

**Current status.** The recommendation underspecifies the comparative valuation. The board should commission the internal exercise before locking the priority.

### Assumption 6: The Administration's political cost of accepting the annex is low

**The case for.** Confidentiality language about ex-US prices is technically far from the political optics of MFN. The Administration's communications team will not be asked to defend it publicly; it appears only in framework annex text.

**The case against.** Political opponents will read the annex if it becomes public (and trade-deal annexes typically become public within 12–24 months). A political opponent characterising the annex as "Roche-protective confidentiality language Trump quietly approved" could create cost the Administration is not prepared to absorb.

**What evidence would settle it.** Communications-strategy game-out by Roche's external counsel and PR agencies, identifying the worst-case political characterisation and the Administration's likely response to it.

**Current status.** Untested. The board should require a defensive communications-strategy review before authorising material concessions.

### Assumption 7: Roche should lead the push

**The case for.** Roche has the most direct material interest. Roche's General Counsel and Government Affairs team have the operational depth to design the annex text and pursue it through USTR.

**The case against.** The Swiss Federal Council, not Roche, is the bilateral counterparty to USTR under the November 2025 framework. The natural posture is for SECO to lead the negotiation and for Roche to provide technical input. If Roche is publicly identified as the driving force, the annex risks the "Swiss-firm sweetheart deal" characterisation.

**What evidence would settle it.** Conversation between Schinecker and Federal Council President Parmelin (or Cassis at FDFA) on who leads. If SECO has capacity and willingness, SECO leads with Roche as technical advisor. If SECO defers, Roche leads with the awareness of the optics cost.

**Current status.** The recommendation implicitly assumes Roche leads. This should be reversed: SECO leads as default, Roche leads only if SECO declines.

### Assumption 8: The annex's confidentiality framework does not conflict with EU Critical Medicines Act

**The case for.** The CMA's manufacturing-resilience provisions are about *supply continuity*, not about pricing transparency. There is no obvious direct conflict.

**The case against.** The CMA (provisional deal 12 May 2026) includes provisions on transparency in pharmaceutical supply chains and may include disclosure requirements for cross-border drug pricing arrangements. If the EU formally adopts CMA with such provisions, a US–Swiss firewall annex protecting confidential pricing could be characterised as undermining EU transparency standards.

**What evidence would settle it.** EU Commission engagement on whether the firewall annex is compatible with CMA principles. Roche's EU government-affairs organisation should run a parallel track checking compatibility.

**Current status.** Untested. The board should require an EU compatibility check before annex signature.

### Assumption 9: The financial calculation that intl-ref-pricing-protection at 7/10 vs 3/10 is materially valuable

**The case for.** The model treats issue values as ordinal indicators. 7/10 represents "strong confidentiality protection across the OECD basket"; 3/10 represents "current state." The qualitative difference is large.

**The case against.** Ordinal scales are not financial. The board needs to know: in CHF, how much does moving from 3/10 to 7/10 actually protect over the 5-year annex life? The recommendation does not state this.

**What evidence would settle it.** Internal financial modelling: scenarios with and without annex protection, modelled across Roche's top 10 drugs in Europe over 2027–2031, with explicit assumptions about MFN-basket-erosion risk pathways.

**Current status.** Underspecified. The board should require financial modelling before signing.

### Assumption 10: The successor 2029 administration will respect the annex

**The case for.** Treaty form (or treaty-adjacent bilateral instruments) typically survives administration turnover. The 2025 framework itself is a bilateral instrument signed by both governments.

**The case against.** The November 2025 framework was signed under unusual political conditions and could be repudiated by a successor US administration. A subsequent administration's withdrawal from the annex during its 5-year life is a real risk. The annex needs to survive this.

**What evidence would settle it.** Constitutional law analysis on whether the annex has the legal status that requires Senate consent for repudiation. If it does, the 2029 administration cannot unilaterally exit. If it does not, the risk is real.

**Current status.** Underspecified. The recommendation should require treaty-adjacent legal form, not executive-agreement form.

### Assumption 11: 5-year sunset is the right horizon

**The case for.** The German MRA has a 3-year sunset (June 2028). A 5-year horizon for the US–Swiss annex gives Roche stability through the next political cycle without committing too long.

**The case against.** A 5-year horizon could be perceived by the Administration as too long (locking in concessions before the next political cycle has even started) or by Roche as too short (forcing renewal during what may be unfavourable political conditions). 10-year horizon with structured 5-year reviews may be better.

**What evidence would settle it.** Game-theoretic analysis of when Roche and the Administration's joint optimum lies in horizon length. The model can answer this if asked.

**Current status.** Underspecified. The board should explore both 5-year and 10-year horizons.

### Assumption 12: "Explicitly above MFN-coverage expansion or TrumpRx SKU growth" is the right way to frame priority

**The case for.** Public framing helps. Naming what Roche is NOT pursuing helps focus the negotiation.

**The case against.** Naming what Roche is NOT pursuing telegraphs Roche's priority structure to the Administration, which can extract more value from the firewall by demanding less ostensible movement elsewhere. *Strategic ambiguity* about Roche's priority structure may extract better terms.

**What evidence would settle it.** Negotiation-theory consultation. Standard practice in multi-issue negotiation is to *not telegraph* which issues are highest-priority unless doing so creates focal-point benefit.

**Current status.** The recommendation telegraphs. This is probably wrong. The board should approve the *internal* prioritisation but require Roche to preserve external strategic ambiguity about which issues are most valued.

## Refined precise actionable version

Replacing the original sentence with assumption-stripped, precise text:

> **Item 1 — Decision required.** The board endorses Roche pursuing a confidentiality-recognition annex to the November 2025 US–Switzerland–Liechtenstein framework, modelled on the German Medizinforschungsgesetz mechanism, targeting Q3 2026 closure (acceptable Q1 2027), and authorises CHF 8–12M of additional government-affairs and external-counsel budget for the 2026 H2 window in support.
>
> The endorsement is **conditional** on three pre-conditions established within 60 days:
>
> (i) a back-channel sounding by SECO to USTR confirming receptivity to confidentiality-framework discussion;
>
> (ii) peer-firm sounding (Novartis, AstraZeneca, GSK) confirming that symmetric language is achievable across the MFN signatory cohort;
>
> (iii) General Counsel memo specifying which elements of the German MRA template transfer to the US–Swiss context and which do not, with proposed substitutes for the non-transferable elements.
>
> If all three pre-conditions are met, Roche proceeds with the firewall annex as the lead 2026 negotiating priority. SECO leads the bilateral negotiation with USTR; Roche provides technical input but is not publicly identified as the driving force.
>
> If pre-conditions (i) or (ii) fail, the fallback plan in Section 5 below applies.
>
> Internal prioritisation is the firewall annex over MFN-coverage expansion or TrumpRx SKU growth. *External* communication preserves strategic ambiguity about Roche's priority structure; the Administration is not told which item Roche values most.

This refinement does five things the original did not:

1. Names the specific instrument (annex form, MRA template).
2. Names the specific budget commitment.
3. Adds three pre-conditions that test load-bearing assumptions before commitment.
4. Names SECO as lead, not Roche.
5. Adds strategic ambiguity in external communications.

## Fallback plan

If Pre-condition (i) — USTR back-channel sounding — comes back negative, the firewall annex is unachievable in 2026 in the current administration's MFN architecture. Fallbacks in order of preference:

**Fallback A — Congressional vehicle.** The firewall framework moves to a congressional instrument. The EPIC Act (S. 832, softening IRA small-molecule pill penalty) or a successor bill in 2027 incorporates the confidential-net-price recognition as a statutory element. This survives administration turnover.

**Fallback B — EU-side firewall.** Roche pursues an enhanced version of the German MRA in additional EU member states (France via CEPS, UK via NICE commercial-access agreements). This does not protect ex-US prices from US MFN-basketization but it does prevent ex-EU price disclosures from cascading.

**Fallback C — Commercial-contract confidentiality.** Roche tightens commercial-contract confidentiality clauses with payers across all jurisdictions, particularly clauses about commercial-confidentiality-protected disclosure to government counterparties. This is operational-level not policy-level but provides incremental protection.

**Fallback D — Wait.** Defer the firewall question to a 2027 successor administration if 2026 is unfavourable. Maintain the current 3/10 protection floor. Pursue other moves (Move 2 — capex flexibility, Move 3 — tail-risk hedge) on their independent timelines.

## Resource commitments

Approved by board endorsement of Item 1 as refined:

- *Budget.* CHF 8–12M additional government-affairs / trade-team / external-counsel budget for 2026 H2.
- *Personnel.* CEO Schinecker engaged personally; General Counsel leads operational work; Head of Government Affairs manages USTR engagement; Roche EU government affairs runs parallel CMA-compatibility check.
- *External.* Sullivan & Cromwell or Skadden as US trade counsel; Lenz & Staehelin as Swiss trade counsel; Sidley or Hogan Lovells as EU regulatory counsel.
- *Timeline.* 60-day pre-condition window (signed off by end June 2026). If pre-conditions pass: target Q3 2026 closure, acceptable Q1 2027. If pre-conditions fail: pivot to fallback plan within 30 days of pre-condition failure.

## Measurable success criteria

The board can hold the executive team accountable to the following measurable outcomes:

- **By 30 June 2026:** all three pre-conditions resolved (pass or fail). If pass, proceed; if fail, fallback initiated.
- **By 30 September 2026:** firewall annex text drafted by Roche–SECO joint team. USTR has received the draft and provided initial response.
- **By 31 December 2026:** firewall annex signed by USTR and SECO, OR formal acknowledgement that 2026 closure is not achievable and Q1 2027 timeline confirmed.
- **By 30 June 2027:** firewall annex in operational force, with at least one demonstrable application (a specific commercial pricing arrangement protected under the annex).
- **By 30 June 2031** (annex 5-year review): renewal decision based on observable protection delivered.

---

## What the board is *not* endorsing

For clarity, the endorsement is not:

- Approval of any specific concession Roche may be required to make in exchange for the annex (those return for board approval if material).
- Public commitment to the annex framework (until signed, the annex is internal-only).
- Continued pursuit of the annex if pre-conditions fail (the fallback plan triggers).
- Subordination of other strategic moves (Move 2 — capex flexibility — proceeds in parallel on its own timeline).
- Commitment to the 5-year horizon if the negotiation surfaces a better duration.
