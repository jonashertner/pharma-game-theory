# Global pricing-mechanism map

> A comparative map of US, EU, and Chinese drug-pricing-negotiation regimes — what mechanisms exist, what Roche / Genentech and direct competitors have agreed to in each, and where the structural differences create strategic asymmetries.

**Snapshot:** May 19, 2026

---

## 1. Why this map matters

The phrase "MFN deal" has, in the Trump-administration sense, become a load-bearing piece of US pharmaceutical-policy vocabulary in 2025–2026 — but it is a US-only construct. A "Trump MFN deal" is a *voluntary, manufacturer-specific* commitment to peg US Medicaid and direct-to-consumer prices to the lowest price observed in an OECD reference basket (members with per-capita GDP ≥ 60% of US per-capita GDP), in exchange for a three-year Section 232 tariff exemption. As of April 23, 2026, all 17 large originators targeted by the July 31, 2025 HHS letter — including Roche/Genentech — have signed.

The European Union has no MFN regime, has no central pricing authority, and is not architecturally capable of cloning the US model. Pricing is a Member-State competence under Article 168(7) TFEU; the EU layer is regulatory (EMA), HTA-methodological (the Joint Clinical Assessment that went live January 12, 2025), and supply-resilience (Critical Medicines Act, May 12, 2026). Each country runs its own reference-pricing and HTA system. Germany's January 2025 Medical Research Act introduced *confidential* reimbursement amounts — the direct opposite of US transparency-by-design.

China runs a state-monopsony architecture: the National Healthcare Security Administration (NHSA, est. 2018) runs the annual NRDL negotiation for innovative products and a separate Volume-Based Procurement (VBP) tender for generics and biosimilars. Roche's exposure differs by region in kind, not just degree: the US is a tariff-coupled list-price problem; the EU is a contagion-and-confidentiality problem; China is a market-access-versus-volume trade-off. Building a comparative map — not a parallel list — is the only way to see where the structural asymmetries become strategic levers.

## 2. United States — voluntary manufacturer MFN deals

*(Reference-only summary; the full corpus is in [`/memo/disclosures-register.md`](./disclosures-register.md). This section gives the comparison-ready slice.)*

### 2.1 The five MFN-deal-defining structural features

1. **Voluntary.** The legal core (Executive Order 14297, May 12, 2025, 90 FR 20749) does not compel pricing — it directs HHS to *seek* manufacturer agreements; the 2020 interim final rule was enjoined on APA grounds, and the 2025 EO deliberately avoids rulemaking. ([Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/05/15/2025-08876/delivering-most-favored-nation-prescription-drug-pricing-to-american-patients))
2. **Manufacturer-specific.** Each agreement is bilateral between HHS and one manufacturer. Agreement texts are confidential; the only public disclosures are White House fact sheets and Annex II of Proclamation 11020.
3. **OECD ≥ 60%-GDP reference basket.** HHS-communicated basket (per CRS LSB11319) excludes most lower-priced EU peripheral states, anchoring the effective MFN floor at Germany / UK / France / Japan / Canada / Australia rather than Greece or Portugal. ([CRS LSB11319](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11319))
4. **Effected via TrumpRx + Medicaid MFN.** Two enforcement rails: (a) Medicaid Drug Rebate Program MFN access for all 50 state programs; (b) direct-to-consumer cash pricing on the federal portal [TrumpRx.gov](https://trumprx.gov) (launched February 5, 2026).
5. **Conditional on Section 232 exemption track.** Proclamation 11020 (April 2, 2026; 91 FR 18183) imposes a 100% ad valorem duty on patented pharmaceuticals from non-signatory manufacturers; Annex II lists the MFN signatories as duty-exempt; the 20% "onshoring discount" tier steps back up to 100% on April 2, 2030. ([Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/09/2026-06956/adjusting-imports-of-pharmaceuticals-and-pharmaceutical-ingredients-into-the-united-states))

## 3. European Union — reference-pricing mosaic

There is no EU drug-pricing authority. Pricing and reimbursement are a Member-State competence (TFEU Art. 168(7)). The EU layer comprises (i) centralized marketing authorization via EMA, (ii) the new EU Joint Clinical Assessment under Regulation (EU) 2021/2282, (iii) the Pharma Package revision (provisional trilogue Dec 11, 2025), and (iv) the Critical Medicines Act (provisional Council/Parliament deal May 12, 2026). Pricing remains per-country.

### 3.1 Germany — G-BA / IQWiG / AMNOG / Erstattungsbetrag

- **Mechanism:** AMNOG (Arzneimittelmarktneuordnungsgesetz, 2010). G-BA dossier review + IQWiG benefit assessment within 6 months of launch; GKV-Spitzenverband then negotiates the *Erstattungsbetrag* (reimbursement amount) — the binding net price — under §130b SGB V. Free pricing during the launch year; the negotiated price applies retroactively from month 7 (since the GKV-Finanzstabilisierungsgesetz, Nov 12, 2022).
- **Medical Research Act (Medizinforschungsgesetz, MFG):** Entered into force Oct 30, 2024; the *confidential Erstattungsbetrag* path became operational **January 1, 2025**. Manufacturers with qualifying German R&D operations may opt for a confidential negotiated price *not* published in Lauer-Taxe — in exchange for an additional **9% discount** on the agreed price. Sunset clause June 30, 2028 (initial evaluation window). ([Inside EU Life Sciences, Covington](https://www.insideeulifesciences.com/2024/07/12/germany-amends-drug-pricing-and-reimbursement-laws-with-medical-research-act-drug-pricing-becomes-intertwined-with-local-clinical-research-expectations/)) ([BfArM](https://www.bfarm.de/EN/Medical-research-act/_node.html)) ([Value & Dossier](https://value-dossier.com/en/confidential-reimbursement-amount-only-for-pharmaceutical-companies-with-rd-in-germany/))
- **Strategic significance:** The MFG is the *legal template* Roche's board memo Move-1 firewall annex is modeled on (see [`/docs/recommendation.html`](../docs/recommendation.html) — "symmetric language modeled on Germany's January 2025 Medical Research Act confidential Erstattungsbetrag mechanism"). The German innovation is that *transparency* and *deep discount* are explicitly traded against each other: you get the better net price but the published price stays high, so other countries that reference-price off Germany (Austria, Czechia, Slovakia, parts of Italy, Greece's basket) cannot lift the lower number into their own systems.
- **Roche drugs in / recently completed Erstattungsbetrag negotiation:** Polivy (polatuzumab vedotin, D-507 first benefit assessment August 20, 2020; ongoing follow-up assessments through 2024–2025 — [G-BA file](https://www.amnog-monitor.com/de/verfahren/g-ba/polatuzumab-vedotin/), [GKV-SV §130b entry](https://www.gkv-spitzenverband.de/krankenversicherung/arzneimittel/verhandlungen_nach_amnog/ebv_130b/wirkstoff_1219267.jsp)); Vabysmo and Phesgo have completed assessments; the published Erstattungsbeträge are visible in the GKV-Spitzenverband §130b directory. **Verification gap:** I could not extract specific Erstattungsbetrag numerical values for individual Roche SKUs within the research window — and post-MFG, some of those numbers will not become public going forward.

### 3.2 France — HAS / CEPS

- **Mechanism:** Commission de la Transparence (CT) at HAS issues the *Service Médical Rendu* (SMR — clinical relevance) and the *Amélioration du Service Médical Rendu* (ASMR — added clinical value, rated I–V). The ASMR rating then drives the *Comité Économique des Produits de Santé* (CEPS) price negotiation; an ASMR V ("no improvement") prevents premium pricing vs. comparator.
- **Roche / Vabysmo:** ASMR V vs. EYLEA (aflibercept) for both wet AMD (CT opinion Jan 18, 2023) and macular edema secondary to BRVO/CRVO (CT opinion Dec 18, 2024). Translation: France paid no premium for Vabysmo over Eylea — material because Vabysmo's global commercial thesis was differentiation against Eylea on dosing interval. ([HAS / Roche-archived avis Dec 18, 2024](https://assets.roche.com/f/173873/x/d3a1b17def/vabysmo-ovr-ins-avis-ct-20241218.pdf)) ([HAS / Roche-archived avis Jan 18, 2023](https://assets.roche.com/f/173873/x/f655e54d95/vabysmo-dmla-pic-ins-avisdef-ct20010-20230302.pdf))
- **Implication:** France illustrates the *contagion risk* on which the firewall annex rests. The French CEPS net price is confidential (commercial agreements); the public ASMR rating is the signal that drives other countries' reference baskets. So the *rating* matters more than the *price* for cross-border contagion.

### 3.3 United Kingdom — NICE / NHS England

- **Mechanism:** NICE Technology Appraisals (TAs) — yes/no cost-effectiveness decisions at ~£20–30k/QALY thresholds; commercial-access agreements (CAAs) with confidential discounts via the NHS Commercial Medicines Unit's CAP Portal; Cancer Drugs Fund (CDF) for conditional access.
- **Roche / Vabysmo:** Positive TA800 (wet AMD) and TA1004 (macular oedema after retinal vein occlusion); list price £857/vial with a confidential simple-discount Patient Access Scheme. ([NICE TA800](https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ta800)) ([NICE TA1004](https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ta1004))
- **Roche / Ocrevus subcutaneous:** MHRA approved July 2024; NHS England implementation began July 2024 — 10-minute SC injection replaces ~4-hour IV. ([NHS England, July 15, 2024](https://www.england.nhs.uk/2024/07/nhs-rolls-out-new-multiple-sclerosis-jab-that-cuts-hospital-treatment-time-by-ninety-per-cent/))
- **Roche / Polivy:** NICE initially declined; later recommended for regular NHS funding in 2L DLBCL not eligible for stem-cell transplant, on the basis of an updated commercial-arrangement and survival analyses ([pharmaphorum](https://pharmaphorum.com/news/nice-recommends-regular-nhs-funding-for-roches-lymphoma-drug-polivy)). UK is a model of confidential-net + visible-list pricing — closer to the German MFG model than to the US MFN model.

### 3.4 Italy — AIFA

- **Mechanism:** AIFA runs national pricing and reimbursement, with the *registri di monitoraggio* (managed-entry-agreement registries) the principal post-launch tool. Outcomes-based agreements (OBAs), payment-by-result, financial risk-sharing, and price-volume agreements all coexist. Strong tradition of confidential discounts and clawback (*payback*) mechanisms.
- **Roche exposure:** Polivy, Lunsumio, Tecentriq and Phesgo are all in the AIFA-managed-entry registry orbit. **Verification gap:** I could not surface specific 2025 OBA terms for Polivy or Lunsumio within the research window — Italy is where confidentiality is most thorough in practice. ([Frontiers, AIFA registries review](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fphar.2021.699466/full))

### 3.5 Netherlands — Zorginstituut

- **Mechanism:** Zorginstituut Nederland runs horizon-scanning (horizonscangeneesmiddelen.nl) and assessment for the *sluis* (lock) — products that exceed cost or volume triggers enter the *sluis* and require national price negotiation before broad reimbursement.
- **Roche exposure:** Polivy was placed in the *sluis* on conditional-reimbursement; Lunsumio status not confirmed in the research window. ([Tandfonline, Reimbursement routes for ATMPs in NL](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17460751.2025.2594314))

## 4. China — NRDL negotiations + Volume-Based Procurement

### 4.1 Structural overview

The National Healthcare Security Administration (NHSA, 国家医疗保障局) was created in 2018 and consolidated drug pricing, reimbursement listing, and procurement under one body. Three principal instruments:

1. **NRDL (国家基本医疗保险药品目录, National Reimbursement Drug List)** — annual negotiation round; manufacturer applies, NHSA committee reviews, price negotiation in late autumn, results published Nov–Dec, listing effective Jan 1 of the following year. Single-payer leverage at the national level.
2. **Volume-Based Procurement (VBP, 国家组织药品集中采购)** — the "4+7" pilot launched in 2018 covered 11 cities; the national program now runs annual rounds and applies primarily to off-patent generics and (increasingly) biosimilars. Awards are volume-guaranteed tender contracts.
3. **Commercial Health Insurance (CHI) Drug List ("C-list")** — newly formalized in 2025 as a parallel listing track for products that NHSA does not place in the NRDL but commercial insurers can co-fund. The 2025 NRDL release was *the first* to include a commercial drug list alongside the basic list. ([Pharmaceutical Technology](https://www.pharmaceutical-technology.com/analyst-comment/china-2025-nrdl-first-commercial-insurance-drug-list/))

### 4.2 NRDL 2025 round — published late 2025, effective Jan 1, 2026

- 114 drugs added; 88% final-negotiation success rate (a seven-year peak); shortlist pass rate dropped to a record-low 41%, indicating tighter gatekeeping upstream. ([Greenberg Traurig](https://www.gtlaw.com/en/insights/2026/2/china-on-the-move-key-takeaways-from-the-2025-nrdl-negotiations-and-adjustment)) ([Simon-Kucher](https://www.simon-kucher.com/en/insights/2025-nrdl-readout-new-listings-renewals-and-re-negotiations))
- Renegotiation price cuts averaged **14%** (vs. 12% in 2024) — i.e., the *erosion-on-renewal* is steepening for existing NRDL products.
- Oncology remained the largest single category: 36 of 60 shortlisted oncology drugs (60% success).

### 4.4 Volume-Based Procurement — 11th round, July–October 2025

- 55 products across 14 therapeutic areas, all awarded contracts by end-October 2025. ([Pacific Bridge Medical](https://www.pacificbridgemedical.com/news-brief/china-adds-55-drugs-to-the-new-11th-vbp-round-tightens-rules-on-quality-and-supply-stability/)) ([Simon-Kucher](https://www.simon-kucher.com/en/insights/what-latest-volume-based-procurement-changes-china-mean-pharma))
- **Structural shift in the 11th round:** explicit move away from price-only awards toward quality and supply-stability scoring — a response to the post-VBP shortages that surfaced earlier rounds and to international scrutiny of Chinese API supply.
- **Biosimilar VBP signal:** biosimilars have not yet been swept into the national VBP regime at scale, but several provincial-level biosimilar tenders ran in 2024–2025, and a national biosimilar-specific framework is in active drafting per industry trackers. ([NRDL Plus](https://www.nrdlplus.com/chinas-biosimilar-vbp-emerging-rules-pricing-dynamics-and-what-early-signals-suggest/)) — bevacizumab biosimilars (Innovent Byvasda, Henlius Hanbeitai, Qilu Ankeda) are the obvious near-term VBP candidates given multiple licensed suppliers and material public-payer spend.

### 4.5 Tariff and trade-war overlay

- Trump-administration tariffs on Chinese imports (separate from §232 pharma proclamation) affect Roche's *competitive* landscape rather than direct Roche cost: bevacizumab biosimilar manufacturers Innovent and BeiGene face higher US-market frictions, marginally reducing the long-run threat of low-cost biosimilar exports into US markets and indirectly supporting Roche's US Avastin franchise.
- Biosimilars are *explicitly carved out* of §232 (Annex of Proclamation 11020), so cross-Pacific biosimilar trade flows are not directly tariffed — the effect is via investment and market-entry costs.

### 4.6 Pluvicto in China — Novartis comparator

Pluvicto (lutetium-177 vipivotide tetraxetan) received NMPA approval in **November 2025**, becoming the only PSMA-targeted radioligand therapy licensed in China. ([Yicai Global](https://www.yicaiglobal.com/news/novartis-nears-first-radioligand-therapy-approval-in-china/)) ([Roche/Novartis trade press]). NRDL inclusion of Pluvicto is the natural next decision point and likely a 2026 NRDL agenda item — relevant comparator for Roche radioligand pipeline thinking.

## 6. Cross-region structural comparison

The three regimes look superficially similar — "drug pricing pressure" — but their *mechanism design* is fundamentally different on at least five axes.

**(i) Voluntary vs. mandatory.** The US MFN deals are voluntary (executive-order-driven, manufacturer-specific contracts). The EU per-country and EU-level frameworks are mandatory regulations binding on all market participants. China's NRDL is voluntary-to-apply but effectively mandatory because non-listing typically collapses commercial viability for innovative drugs in public hospitals — the choice is between "negotiate hard with NHSA" and "give up the largest oncology market." Voluntary in the US is a *real* lever — Pfizer and Genentech could in principle have refused, accepted the 100% tariff, and litigated; Roche's December 2025 calculation was that 15% Swiss tariff cap + 3-year exemption + Medicaid market access > the cost of MFN concessions. Voluntary in China is essentially Hobson's choice.

**(ii) Manufacturer-specific vs. broad-policy.** The US MFN deals are bilateral and confidential. Every other major regime — German Erstattungsbetrag (despite the new confidential option), French ASMR, NICE TA, AIFA OBA, NRDL — operates on a per-product (not per-manufacturer) basis and produces a determination that *generalizes* across the manufacturer's portfolio in that geography. So a single AIFA OBA touches one Polivy indication, not the manufacturer book. The US construct gives administrations leverage on the *entire* portfolio (and on cross-product behavior like investment commitments) that no other system has.

**(iii) Confidential net price vs. visible list price.** This is the axis on which the Roche firewall annex's strategic logic hinges. Germany (post-MFG, January 2025), UK (CAA Portal), Italy (managed-entry registers), Netherlands (sluis), and France (CEPS net) all operate confidential-net + visible-list pricing. China NRDL is *visible*-list-price publication of negotiated prices — the NHSA explicitly publishes the negotiated NRDL prices. The US MFN regime is a *hybrid*: TrumpRx prices are explicitly visible (the whole platform's signaling function depends on visibility), Medicaid MFP is visible, but the *agreement text* with each manufacturer is confidential. So the US is generating visible MFN benchmark numbers that other countries' reference systems *can* read off, while the rest of the world has been moving *toward* confidentiality.

**(iv) Single mechanism vs. layered.** US: one mechanism (MFN deal + IRA + §232 tariff stick), enforced by one administration. China: one institutional actor (NHSA), two mechanisms (NRDL + VBP) plus the new C-list. EU: 27 Member-State systems + 4 EU-layer frameworks (HTAR/JCA, Pharma Package, Critical Medicines Act, EMA centralized authorization) = ~31 interacting layers. The EU is uniquely *un*-actionable as a single negotiating counterparty; the US is uniquely *concentrable* as one.

**(v) Strategic asymmetry for Roche.** *Most strategically asymmetric* region: the EU — because German MFG confidentiality is what protects Roche's Swiss / German / EU net-price book from contagion if US visible-list MFN benchmarks try to walk into European reference baskets. *Most stable*: China — paradoxically. NHSA's mechanism is the most coercive, but it is also the most *predictable*, with annual cycles, transparent process, and known erosion rates (10–14% on renewal). Roche's China revenue is more forecastable than its US Medicaid book is, post-MFN.

### Three non-obvious structural insights

1. **The US MFN regime is the only one in the world that systematically *generates* visible benchmark prices that other countries' systems can reference-price off.** That is a structural inversion of the historical pattern, where the US was the reference *for* European systems (via published US net prices in 340B disclosures, ASP). The MFN list prices on TrumpRx are, by design, visible — Praluent $225, Xofluza $50, Otarmeni free. Every other major system has moved toward confidentiality precisely because reference-pricing contagion is the problem. The US has just become the *source* of contagion rather than its target. This is the central insight the Roche board memo's firewall annex is structured around.

2. **The 2024–2025 NRDL inclusions of Phesgo and Vabysmo are arguably more economically consequential for Roche's near-term China P&L than the MFN deal is for its US P&L.** Phesgo China growth in 2025 was specifically attributed by Roche to NRDL access; Vabysmo NRDL listing opened the largest single-market ophthalmology opportunity. Meanwhile, MFN's direct US revenue hit is mediated by Medicaid (a minority of US Roche revenue) and TrumpRx (Xofluza is small). The MFN deal's economic weight is tariff-exemption-driven, not price-driven, at the margin.

3. **The EU Pharma Package's RDP cut (8+2+1 → 8+1+1+1) and the Critical Medicines Act's strategic-project regime are an explicit *trade*.** The EU is saying: shorter exclusivity = lower prices and more competition for patients; strategic-project status = more EU manufacturing investment. Roche's strategic choice is whether to *participate* in CMA strategic projects (and bank the permitting/funding benefits) in exchange for accepting the shorter-RDP baseline, or to *opt out* and rely on patent terms. Most originator commentary frames the Pharma Package as bad news. Read together with the CMA, it is closer to a quid-pro-quo — the same architecture as the US MFN-for-§232-exemption swap, just slower-moving.

## 7. What this means for the firewall annex

The board recommendation in [`/docs/recommendation.html`](../docs/recommendation.html) (Move 1) calls for a Swiss-bilateral firewall annex "modeled on Germany's January 2025 Medical Research Act confidential Erstattungsbetrag mechanism." The comparative map sharpens that recommendation in three ways.

**The German MFG mechanic, verified.** The Medical Research Act introduced an *opt-in* confidential reimbursement amount in §130b SGB V from January 1, 2025, conditional on a qualifying German R&D footprint and a 9% additional discount on the negotiated price. The list price stays public; the negotiated net price does not appear in Lauer-Taxe; reference-pricing systems in other jurisdictions cannot lift the lower number across borders. ([BfArM](https://www.bfarm.de/EN/Medical-research-act/_node.html)) ([Inside EU Life Sciences](https://www.insideeulifesciences.com/2024/07/12/germany-amends-drug-pricing-and-reimbursement-laws-with-medical-research-act-drug-pricing-becomes-intertwined-with-local-clinical-research-expectations/)) The board memo's symmetric-language template aligns with this mechanic: a Swiss-bilateral instrument that says "MFN-benchmark prices arising under the December 2025 deal are not referenceable into Swiss, EU, or other OECD reimbursement baskets" replicates the *MFG firewall* at the trade-treaty layer rather than the German federal-law layer.

**Why neither the EU nor China would adopt a US-style voluntary MFN.** The EU cannot: pricing is reserved to Member States, and any cross-Member-State price-setting initiative would require a unanimous Council vote and a TFEU Art. 168(7) revisitation. The Commission has neither the legal base nor the political mandate. China would not: NHSA already extracts deeper net-price concessions through the annual NRDL cycle than any voluntary US arrangement does — NHSA negotiating power compounded with state-monopsony status produces price cuts that the US MFN regime can only approximate. China sees the US MFN regime more as a *prestige adoption* of Chinese-style pricing leverage than as a model to import.

**What the EU might do if pushed too hard on transparency.** If the US MFN regime produces a sustained visible benchmark that EU reference baskets can read into, the structural response will *not* be parallel adoption — it will be acceleration of confidentiality. The German MFG sunset clause is June 30, 2028; an early renewal and extension to all comers (without the R&D-footprint conditionality) is the most likely path. France will quietly continue confidential CEPS net prices. The UK CAP Portal will deepen. The risk for Roche is not that the EU becomes more transparent; it is that Member-State confidentiality drifts apart from the US visible-list architecture, making the firewall annex *more* necessary, not less.

**What China is likely to do regardless of US action.** Continue the annual NRDL cycle, deepen biosimilar VBP, expand the C-list, and treat Roche on a case-by-case product basis. China is the most stable region for Roche's planning horizon precisely because it is the most coercive — NHSA's playbook is known. The firewall annex does not address China; nothing in the Swiss-bilateral architecture would.

## 9. Summary and gaps

### Summary (200 words)

The three regional regimes converge on a single outcome — drug-price moderation — through structurally incompatible mechanisms. The US has built a *voluntary, manufacturer-specific, tariff-coupled* MFN architecture: 17 deals (Pfizer Sept 30, 2025 → Regeneron Apr 23, 2026), all confidential as to text, all visible as to TrumpRx and Medicaid prices, all gated by Section 232 tariff exemption under Proclamation 11020. The EU has a *27-Member-State pricing mosaic plus four EU-layer regulations*; the January 12, 2025 JCA central-clinical-assessment and the Dec 11, 2025 Pharma Package revision (8+1+1+1 RDP, 11-year cap) reshape the methodology and the IP-protection baseline, while the May 12, 2026 Critical Medicines Act introduces a manufacturing-resilience quid pro quo. Germany's January 2025 Medical Research Act introduced *confidential Erstattungsbeträge* (9% additional discount in exchange) — the explicit legal template for the firewall annex Roche's board memo recommends. China's NHSA runs annual NRDL rounds with 14% average renewal cuts and the 11th VBP round (55 products awarded Oct 2025) — Roche's Phesgo and Vabysmo entered NRDL effective Jan 2025, Itovebi entered effective Jan 2026, and Hemlibra is deliberately C-list-only. The most strategically asymmetric region for Roche is the EU (because confidentiality is the firewall); the most stable is China (because the playbook is known).
