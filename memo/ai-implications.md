# AI implications

> The mid-2026 pharma-pricing negotiation has been analysed so far without explicit treatment of AI. This page corrects that. AI capabilities over 2026–2030 will reshape every layer of the negotiation: drug discovery, regulatory review, pricing decisions, and the structure of the negotiation itself. This is an attempt to think through what AI changes, what it doesn't, and what the board should know.

**Snapshot:** 20 May 2026 · companion to [board recommendation](recommendation.html), [strategic dynamics](dynamics.html), and [cross-domain strategy](cross-domain.html)

---

## Why this page exists

Every actor profile, every issue weight, every cross-domain move in the supporting analysis takes AI as if it were *not happening*. That is an analytical hole. AI is not a side issue in pharmaceutical innovation — it is reshaping the cost curves of drug discovery, the regulatory throughput of FDA/CMS/EMA, the optimization functions of pricing decisions, and the very actors at the negotiation table.

The board memo's recommended 5–10 year moves are built on assumptions about the pharma industry's structure. Several of those assumptions will not survive AI's 2026–2030 trajectory. This page identifies which ones and what to do about it.

The page is organised as eight layers of implication, followed by AI-specific strategic moves and the meta-question of how AI changes the negotiation's character.

## Layer 1 — AI in drug discovery

**State of the field (May 2026).** AlphaFold (2021) and AlphaFold-Multimer (2023) effectively solved protein structure prediction. AlphaProteo (2024) extends to de novo protein design. Isomorphic Labs (DeepMind spin-out), Recursion, Insilico Medicine, Generate Biomedicines, Genesis Therapeutics, Iambic Therapeutics, and a dozen others now operate AI-first drug-discovery platforms. By mid-2026, multiple AI-discovered candidates are in Phase 2 clinical trials; the first AI-discovered drug to reach FDA approval is expected 2027–2028.

**The cost curve shift.** Traditional pharma drug discovery costs $1B–$2B and 10–15 years per drug. AI-first platforms claim 10× cost and time reductions for the discovery and early-development phases (target identification through lead optimization). The savings concentrate in *the first 3 years* of the development pipeline; later phases (clinical trials) remain expensive and slow.

**Implications for Roche.**

1. *Pipeline economics.* Roche's current pipeline economics assume traditional cost structures. If AI-first competitors can produce twice as many viable candidates at half the cost, Roche's pricing-power-via-scarcity model weakens over a 10-year horizon. The firewall annex's value is partly to preserve pricing power that may be eroding from a different direction.

2. *Acquisition strategy.* Roche's M&A targets are increasingly AI-first biotechs (Genentech's history of Genentech-OncoStartups acquisitions is a model). The strategic question: build AI capability internally, acquire AI-first biotechs, or partner. Roche's existing biotech acquisitions (Spark, Inflazome, etc.) suggest a hybrid model.

3. *Investor narrative.* Sell-side analysts increasingly evaluate pharma companies on AI-pipeline-strategy alongside traditional pipeline-strength metrics. Roche needs an explicit AI-discovery narrative.

**What this means for the negotiation.** The firewall annex's value calculation should incorporate the expected pricing-power erosion from AI-driven competitor entry. If AI-discovered drugs reach FDA approval at lower cost, even confidentiality-protected pricing in EU/CH faces downward pressure from competitive substitutes. The annex protects against MFN-basket-erosion but does not protect against AI-driven supply increase.

## Layer 2 — AI in regulatory review

**State of the field.** FDA has been incrementally integrating AI into review processes since 2023 — initially in safety signal detection, then in real-world-evidence assessment, increasingly in early-stage scientific review. CMS uses AI for Medicare fraud detection at scale and is exploring AI in Medicare Advantage risk-adjustment audits. EMA has been more cautious but is implementing AI-assisted scientific advice. The PDUFA VIII reauthorisation discussions (Q4 2026) are expected to include explicit AI-review provisions.

**The throughput shift.** AI-assisted regulatory review could double FDA scientific-review throughput within 5 years. This sounds like good news for pharma; in practice it changes the bargaining position.

**Implications for Roche.**

1. *Faster approvals — for everyone.* If FDA throughput doubles, competing drugs reach market faster. Roche's first-mover advantage windows shrink.

2. *Sharper regulatory analysis.* AI-augmented FDA reviewers can find weaknesses in submissions faster, more reliably, and more systematically. The era of "regulatory affairs as relationship management" is ending; rigour becomes more decisive.

3. *CMS pricing decisions become AI-informed.* IRA Maximum Fair Price negotiations under the IRA's 2022 architecture rely partly on CMS's view of comparative effectiveness. AI-assisted CMS analysis will produce sharper, more aggressive pricing positions on Roche's drugs in IRA Round 4 and beyond.

**What this means for the negotiation.** The 2027–2028 IRA Round 4 selection criteria will be informed by significantly better CMS analytical capacity than the 2023–2025 rounds were. Roche's price-defence arguments will need to be technically rigorous, not just commercially-framed. The firewall annex protects ex-US prices but the IRA pricing pressure on US-channel prices may intensify regardless.

**Strategic move.** Roche should invest in AI-augmented regulatory affairs and pricing-defence analytics — at parity with FDA's investment in AI-augmented review. The information asymmetry that has historically favoured manufacturers (more analytical capacity than regulators) is closing rapidly.

## Layer 3 — AI in pricing decisions

**State of the field.** AI-augmented pricing decisions are emerging across all three major regions:

- *US.* CMS used predictive modelling in IRA Round 1 and Round 2 selections; expected to formalise AI-augmented selection criteria in Round 4 (announced ~Q1 2027). The Trump administration's MFN program could similarly adopt AI-augmented OECD-basket calculations.

- *EU.* G-BA (Germany), HAS (France), and NICE (UK) increasingly use AI for evidence-synthesis. The JCA process under HTA Regulation 2021/2282 explicitly includes AI-augmented methodology.

- *China.* NHSA's NRDL negotiation process has incorporated AI-augmented comparative-effectiveness analysis since 2024. The 2025 round (114 drugs, 88% inclusion) was visibly informed by faster, more rigorous AI-supported analysis than prior rounds.

**Implications for Roche.**

1. *Negotiation asymmetry inverts.* Historically, pharma manufacturers had more analytical capacity than payers. AI closes that gap and may invert it: payers, with access to broader datasets (claims, outcomes, real-world evidence), may have *more* AI-augmented analytical capacity than manufacturers within 3–5 years.

2. *Negotiation tactics must adapt.* The Vickrey-mechanism move (Move 3 in the cross-domain analysis) becomes more plausible when both sides have AI-augmented analytical capacity — the mechanism design benefits from both sides being able to compute their reservation values accurately.

3. *Confidential net pricing under pressure.* AI-augmented analysis by payers can *infer* confidential net prices from public list prices, formulary positioning, and claims data. The firewall annex's confidential-net-price recognition becomes more important precisely because AI makes confidentiality harder to maintain technically.

**What this means for the negotiation.** The firewall annex's value increases over time as AI inference threats grow. But the annex's enforceability depends on the political/legal commitment to confidentiality, which AI does not strengthen and may erode.

## Layer 4 — AI in the negotiation process itself

**The frame.** The pharma-pricing negotiation is, at its core, a multi-party multi-issue bargaining game with incomplete information. AI is uniquely well-suited to this domain: it can model multiple parties' preferences, simulate counterparty responses, propose mechanism designs, and provide negotiation analytics in real time.

**The site you are reading.** This site is a working example. Twelve actor profiles with sourced positions, a 13-issue payoff model with red-line constraints, an 18-event simulation transcript, and seven cross-domain strategic moves — all generated and maintained by an AI system. The administrative cost of producing this analysis was a fraction of what a McKinsey or BCG team would have charged. The implication is not "AI replaces strategy consultants" — it is that *every actor at the table will have analysis of comparable or superior depth at AI-affordable cost*.

**Implications.**

1. *Information equilibration.* Within 2–3 years, every major pharma firm, every major payer, every major regulator, and every major patient advocacy group will have AI-augmented strategic analysis. The current information asymmetries (Roche knows more than CMS knows more than AARP knows more than patient X) will compress.

2. *Negotiation cycle compression.* AI-augmented analysis is real-time. Negotiations that took weeks of preparation may compress to days. The Antarctic-Treaty freeze move's 24-month negotiation window assumes 2024-era diplomatic pace; with AI augmentation, this could shrink to 9–12 months.

3. *Possibility of AI agents as negotiators.* By 2028–2030, some elements of pharma negotiations may involve AI agents communicating directly with each other under human supervision. The Vickrey mechanism move becomes especially apt: AI agents can be designed for incentive-compatible bidding more reliably than humans can.

4. *Game-theoretic literacy spreads.* This site has surfaced concepts like BATNA, Nash bargaining, mechanism design, and Pareto efficiency to a board audience. Every other actor's board will get similar treatment from their own AI. The intellectual scaffolding of negotiation literacy will be common.

**What this means for the negotiation.** Roche's competitive advantage from sophisticated analysis is *temporary*. The window in which this site's analytical depth is unique to Roche is perhaps 12–24 months. By 2027–2028, everyone at the table has comparable analysis.

The implication: speed matters more than depth in the next phase. The board should authorise faster decision cycles to capture the temporary advantage.

## Layer 5 — AI in the broader pharmaceutical industry structure

**Beyond drug discovery and pricing.** AI is reshaping:

- *Clinical trials.* AI-designed trial protocols, synthetic control arms (Unlearn.ai), real-world evidence at scale. The cost and time of Phase 3 trials may compress 30–50% over 5 years.
- *Manufacturing.* AI-optimised continuous-manufacturing processes (especially for biologics) reduce production cost.
- *Patient outcomes.* AI-augmented diagnostics increase the *addressable* patient population for specific drugs by identifying responders better.
- *Real-world evidence generation.* AI can generate RWE from claims data, EHR data, and wearables data at scale.
- *Pharmacovigilance.* AI safety-signal detection is operational at FDA, EMA, and Roche internally.

**Implications.** The pharma industry's cost structure, time horizons, and competitive dynamics all shift. Some companies (those that adopt AI well) gain durable advantage; others (those that resist or move slowly) lose. Roche's strategic position over a 10-year horizon depends on how AI-savvy its discovery, development, and commercial organisations become.

**What this means for the negotiation.** The financial cushion that has historically allowed Roche to absorb price compression in some markets while preserving margins in others is shrinking. AI-driven competitive pressure may force Roche to choose between markets earlier than the current negotiation framework anticipates.

## Layer 6 — Geopolitical AI considerations

**State of the field.** AI capabilities are concentrated in the US (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, Meta) and China (DeepSeek, Qwen team, Baidu, Tencent). Europe lags. Switzerland has notable AI capacity (ETH Zurich, EPFL, ELSI initiative) but is small-scale.

**Implications for the negotiation.**

1. *US-China AI competition reshapes pharma policy.* The CHIPS Act precedent has been extended to biopharma (the Bioeconomy Executive Order, 2022; various successor instruments). AI-augmented bio-design tools are increasingly export-controlled. This affects which discovery platforms Roche can use, where they can be hosted, and how cross-border R&D collaboration is structured.

2. *EU AI Act and pharma.* The EU AI Act (in force August 2026) classifies high-risk AI systems including those used in medical-device approval and patient-care decisions. Roche's AI-augmented R&D tools face compliance obligations under the Act.

3. *Switzerland's AI sovereignty.* Switzerland's data-residency and AI-regulation frameworks are likely to diverge from both US and EU positions over 2026–2028. This creates Swiss-specific compliance complexity for Roche's AI-augmented operations.

4. *China's AI-pharma strategy.* China's 2024–2025 push to build domestic AI-augmented drug discovery (BeiGene's AI-platform, Innovent's biosimilar AI optimisation) reshapes the competitive landscape for Roche in the Asian market.

**What this means for the cross-domain moves.**

- *The Pharma Hansa move* gains relevance: cluster-level AI cooperation across Basel-Boston-Cambridge-Heidelberg-Singapore can hedge against fragmented national AI-pharma policies.
- *The Coal and Steel Community move* could focus on AI-augmented rare-disease horizon-scanning as a pilot for sectoral integration.
- *The lex mercatoria move* could include AI-tooling-portability provisions in the Pharma Trade Code.

## Layer 7 — AI implications for each cross-domain move

The seven cross-domain moves (and three additional moves in the depth companion) each interact with AI in specific ways:

**Mandate of Heaven move.** AI generates ceremonial content cheaply; the cost of the ceremonial pipeline drops by 80–90% with AI-augmented communications. The administration's ability to generate visible-deliverable narratives also improves; the ceremony arms race accelerates. *Strategic implication:* the ceremony pipeline is more affordable and more politically necessary.

**Pharma Hansa move.** AI enables real-time cluster coordination across cities. Member cities can share supply-chain data, talent-pool analytics, and regulatory intelligence at near-zero marginal cost. *Strategic implication:* the Hansa secretariat's value-add increases; the institutional argument strengthens.

**Vickrey mechanism move.** AI agents as bidders. The mechanism's incentive-compatibility properties are more reliably achieved with AI-agent bidders than with human bidders. *Strategic implication:* the Vickrey move becomes more technically feasible and more politically novel.

**Antarctic-Treaty freeze move.** AI-mediated verification could enable freezes that were previously unverifiable. The Treaty's compliance regime can use AI to detect regulatory-expansion attempts more reliably. *Strategic implication:* the Treaty's enforcement becomes more credible.

**Maori utu move.** AI-tracked register makes the closure protocol machine-readable. Disputes about "what counts as the same item" can be resolved by AI-augmented semantic analysis. *Strategic implication:* the utu register becomes operationally smoother.

**Mycorrhizal compact move.** AI-enabled operational coordination across firms. Supply-chain resilience benefits from AI-augmented forecasting and inventory management. *Strategic implication:* the compact's operational value-add increases.

**Wabi-sabi voluntary excess move.** AI-augmented social-listening detects whether the voluntary excess is generating goodwill capital. The effectiveness measurement becomes precise. *Strategic implication:* the move becomes more measurable and therefore more defensible.

**Federalist Papers move.** AI-assisted essay writing reduces the cost; AI-augmented public-reasoning campaigns can be conducted at higher frequency. But also: AI-generated counter-campaigns can dilute the effect. *Strategic implication:* the move is more affordable but in a noisier information environment.

**Coal and Steel Community move.** AI-augmented horizon-scanning makes the pediatric-oncology-orphan-drug sectoral coordination more rigorous. The cooperation produces better outcomes. *Strategic implication:* the spillover bet becomes more credible.

**Lex mercatoria move.** AI-augmented contract drafting accelerates the Pharma Trade Code adoption. AI-augmented dispute resolution under the Code becomes more efficient. *Strategic implication:* the Code's operational viability improves.

## Layer 8 — AI-specific strategic moves

Three moves that emerge specifically from AI considerations:

### Move 11 — AI compliance harmonisation

The EU AI Act (in force August 2026), the US AI Executive Order updates, China's AI governance framework, Switzerland's emerging AI regulation, and Japan's AI Promotion Bill all impose different compliance obligations on pharma AI systems. Roche operates AI-augmented R&D in all five jurisdictions.

**The move.** Roche advocates for AI-pharma compliance harmonisation through industry coordination (Pharma Hansa + IFPMA channels) and direct regulatory engagement. The harmonisation focus is *technical specifications* (model validation, data governance, audit logs) rather than substantive policy. The technical-specification approach borrows from medical-device regulation harmonisation (ICH guidelines).

**Counterparty.** IFPMA, ICH, national regulators (FDA, EMA, MHLW, Swissmedic, NMPA).

**Timeline.** 2026–2028 negotiation; first harmonised technical specification 2029.

**Why this is important.** Without harmonisation, AI-augmented R&D becomes more expensive and slower at exactly the time when AI is the primary lever for industry productivity gains.

### Move 12 — AI sovereignty contingency

**The move.** Roche develops a "AI sovereignty stack" — the capability to operate AI-augmented R&D and pricing analytics under multiple jurisdictions' divergent regulations, with data-residency, model-residency, and computational-residency flexibility built into the architecture. This is industrial infrastructure investment, not regulatory advocacy.

**Counterparty.** Internal Roche IT/engineering organisation; cloud vendors (AWS, Azure, GCP); sovereign-cloud providers (Swisscom Sovereign Cloud, Switzerland; STACKIT, Germany; Bleu, France).

**Timeline.** 2026–2029 build-out; first sovereign-cloud-resilient deployment 2027.

**Why this is important.** AI export controls and data-residency requirements are tightening. Roche's AI capabilities will be more durable if they are jurisdiction-portable. This is the AI equivalent of the manufacturing-footprint flexibility Move 2 in the board recommendation addresses.

### Move 13 — AI-augmented negotiation infrastructure

**The move.** Roche establishes a permanent AI-augmented strategic-negotiation function — not as a consultancy engagement but as a standing internal capability. The function maintains models of every counterparty's positions, runs ongoing simulations, generates briefings for executive decisions, and provides real-time analytical support during active negotiations.

**Counterparty.** Internal Roche strategy organisation; external AI vendors (Anthropic, OpenAI, or in-house).

**Timeline.** 2026 H2 build-out; first operational use 2027 Q1 (in support of the firewall annex negotiation).

**Why this is important.** The window of analytical advantage closes within 12–24 months as every counterparty develops similar capability. Building this internally now captures the window. The site you are reading is a prototype of what this function produces.

## The meta-question — does AI change the negotiation's character?

The seven cross-domain moves and the AI-specific moves above treat AI as a powerful tool that extends existing strategic categories. The deeper question is: does AI *change the character* of the negotiation itself?

Three possible answers, in increasing depth.

**Answer 1: AI is amplification.** AI makes existing strategies cheaper and faster. The negotiation's structure (who is at the table, what they want, how they decide) is unchanged. Most of the moves on this site take this view implicitly.

**Answer 2: AI is information equilibration.** AI closes information asymmetries that have favoured pharma manufacturers historically. The negotiation's structure remains intact, but the bargaining power redistributes. Moves like the Vickrey mechanism become both more viable and more necessary because manufacturer information advantages are eroding.

**Answer 3: AI is structural transformation.** Some negotiation participants (especially patient advocacy organisations, smaller payer entities, smaller regulators) gain analytical capacity comparable to large manufacturers. This shifts the meta-game. The seven cross-domain moves were designed for a world where Roche, the Administration, and the Swiss Federal Council have analytical depth that other actors lack. If that asymmetry disappears, the moves' value propositions change.

**Most likely:** all three are partially true. Layers 1–3 (drug discovery, regulation, pricing) are amplification + equilibration. Layers 4–6 (the negotiation process, broader industry, geopolitical) include genuine structural transformation. The board should plan for both: moves that work in the amplification world, and moves that work in the transformation world.

The moves that work in *both* worlds:

- Pharma Hansa (cluster-level coordination is robust to AI's distribution effects)
- Federalist Papers (public-reasoning campaigns adapt to AI-augmented information environments)
- Coal and Steel sectoral integration (functional cooperation is robust)
- Lex mercatoria (private legal order is robust)

The moves that work *only* in the amplification world:

- Wabi-sabi voluntary excess (assumes the audience's perception remains organising)
- Maori utu register (assumes parties accept closure logic)

The moves that work *only* in the structural-transformation world:

- AI-augmented negotiation infrastructure (Move 13 above)
- AI compliance harmonisation (Move 11 above)

Portfolio implication: the board should weight robust moves higher and treat amplification-only moves as time-limited.

## What this page does not address

This page does not address:

- *Specific AI tools' capabilities.* The state of capabilities is changing too fast for a 2026 snapshot to remain useful. The page focuses on the structural implications, not the specific tools.
- *AI safety considerations.* The broader question of AI safety (alignment, robustness, misuse) is real but beyond this page's scope.
- *Roche's specific AI-discovery pipeline.* This information is partly proprietary and partly inferred; the page treats Roche's AI capability as a strategic variable, not as a specific roadmap.
- *Regulatory specifics under the EU AI Act.* These are technical and will require external counsel engagement; the page identifies the variable, not the resolution.

## What the board should ask the executive team

Five questions:

1. *What is Roche's explicit AI-discovery strategy?* Build, acquire, or partner; what specific platforms; what investment scale.

2. *How is Roche's regulatory-affairs organisation upgrading its AI-augmented capacity?* Parity with FDA's evolving capability is a precondition for credible engagement.

3. *Is Roche participating in EU AI Act compliance discussions?* The Act enters force August 2026; pharma-specific guidance is being negotiated.

4. *Has Roche evaluated AI agents as negotiation participants in the 2027–2028 horizon?* This is a strategic question, not a tactical one.

5. *Does Roche have an AI sovereignty contingency plan?* Data-residency, model-residency, computational-residency flexibility under tightening export controls.

If any of these questions does not have a clear answer, the gap is itself a finding the board should note.
