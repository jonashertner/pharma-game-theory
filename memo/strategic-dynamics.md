# Strategic dynamics

**The deeper reading of the twelve-actor table — telos, contradictions, alliances**

*Read time: 15 minutes · written for the board and the executive committee*

---

## Why this page exists

The [board recommendation](recommendation.html) presents three moves and four decisions. This page explains the strategic *substance* behind them — what each actor is really doing at the table beneath their stated positions, where their internal contradictions create opening, and which alliances run against the standard pharma-policy frame.

It is written for readers who want to understand the negotiation as a multi-decade strategic question, not a quarterly compliance issue. It uses the language of *telos* — the guiding principle each actor exists for — because stated positions in pharma negotiations are routinely tactical and routinely misleading. The telos is the structural fact that survives the next election, the next quarter, the next deal.

Each actor section follows the same four-part schema: **Stated position · Telos · Contradiction · Unexpected alliance**.

---

## Roche / Genentech

**Stated position.** Preserve margins, protect ex-US pricing, navigate MFN and Section 232 without breaking the December 2025 framework.

**Telos.** Roche exists as a vehicle for the **multi-generational stewardship of a scientific innovation engine**, controlled by the family-held voting bloc. This is not a normal public company. Severin Schwan's 97.75% chairman re-election in March 2026, the 39th consecutive dividend increase, the deliberate Genussschein → ROP conversion in March 2026 — these are family-stewardship signals, not quarterly-cycle signals. The deepest mission is to be in business and productive in 2076, not to maximize this quarter's earnings.

**Contradiction.** The MFN deal requires short-term political accommodation that, under continued executive-action pressure, may erode long-term innovation incentive. The family logic ("we will be here in 2076") is in permanent tension with the executive logic ("we need to close this deal for FY26"). Schinecker's CEO role mediates these two horizons; the board's role is to remind the executive that the family logic outranks the quarterly logic when they conflict.

**Unexpected alliance.** Roche and the EU Commission share a 20-year horizon on European pharma-innovation-infrastructure persistence. At 2-3 year horizons they are price counterparties. At 20+ year horizons they are aligned: both want a productive EU pharma cluster intact in the 2040s. This conversation is impossible at Roche-corporate level (it would look self-interested) but appropriate at family-ownership level (the stewardship logic is structurally similar to the Commission's strategic-autonomy logic).

**Action implication.** The recommendation should explicitly frame the firewall annex on a multi-decade horizon. The board should evaluate the +$5B capex flexibility (decision b in the recommendation) against the family's 25-year payoff calculation, not against quarterly ROE.

## US Executive (White House + HHS/CMS + USTR + Commerce + FDA + career staff)

**Stated position.** Defend the MFN program; demand visible deliverables (capex acceleration, TrumpRx SKU expansion, deeper IRA discounts); preserve political optics of "lowering drug prices."

**Telos.** The Trump administration's deep telos in this domain is **the demonstration of executive sovereignty** — that pharma policy can be made by Executive Order, Section 232 proclamation, and administrative discretion, without legislation. The MFN program is a flagship case study in this telos. The drug-pricing benefit is, in a sense, incidental; the *governance pattern* of "I direct, you comply" is the actual achievement. This is why the December 2025 deals were structured as voluntary firm-by-firm agreements rather than uniform statutory rules.

**Contradiction.** Sovereignty by EO is reversible by EO. A successor administration can erase MFN with one stroke. The administration's incentive is therefore to *bake* the agreements into harder-to-reverse forms — bilateral treaties, multi-year manufacturing commitments, USTR-authority instruments. But the political mood of MAGA is anti-treaty and anti-multilateral. So there is an internal contradiction: the only durable form of MFN is the form MAGA philosophically rejects. The administration cannot openly resolve this; it must do so through proxies and procedural ambiguity.

**Unexpected alliance.** Roche and the US Executive share a structural interest in **treaty form over executive-order form**. Roche wants durability; the administration wants legacy. Both lose if MFN is erased by EO in January 2029 or January 2033. Standard pharma-policy framing assumes the administration prefers flexibility, but the *legacy-conscious* faction (USTR, HHS career staff, Treasury under any administration) prefers durability. They will spend political capital Roche does not have to spend.

**Action implication.** The firewall annex should be negotiated *with USTR* as the lead counterparty, not HHS. USTR has institutional interest in durable bilateral framework architecture; HHS has institutional interest in flexible administrative price-setting. Match the institutional telos to the desired outcome.

## US Congress

**Stated position.** Pursue bipartisan PBM reform (CAA 2026 signed 3 February); defend IRA Rounds 1–3 against erosion; protect state-cluster pharma employment (NJ, MA, NC, IN, IL).

**Telos.** Congress's deep telos is **the defense of legislative prerogative against executive encroachment**. The 119th Congress (53R-47D Senate, 220R-215D House) is uncomfortable with the executive making drug-pricing policy by EO and Section 232 because *that is their turf*. Substantively, congressional Republicans agree with Trump on the direction (lower drug prices, MFN-style political optics, US manufacturing); procedurally, they resent the displacement of appropriation and confirmation. Senator Bill Cassidy's primary loss on 16 May 2026 demonstrated the political cost of being too procedurally pure — MAGA voters punish process-defense — but procedural concern persists in committee leadership.

**Contradiction.** Republican congressmen who agree with Trump on substance hate the procedural form. The procedural complaint cannot be made loudly without inviting primary challenge. So Congress acts by indirection: through committee oversight, through bipartisan PBM-reform legislation (a different vehicle), and through quiet support for *legislative* MFN codification (Meuser's H.R. 7837) that no one expects to pass but everyone keeps alive as a procedural placeholder.

**Unexpected alliance.** Roche and Senate Finance committee leadership (Crapo, Wyden) share an unstated interest in **converting MFN into legislation**. For Roche, legislation is more predictable and litigable than EO. For Wyden, legislation re-asserts Senate authority. The EPIC Act track (softening the IRA small-molecule "pill penalty") is a vehicle to demonstrate congressional relevance; it benefits Roche's biologics-heavy portfolio (because the pill penalty doesn't apply to biologics) while providing Wyden a legislative win. Quiet Roche support for the EPIC Act track cross-subsidizes a congressional priority without costing Roche anything material.

**Action implication.** Engage Senate Finance leadership through proxies (PhRMA, Interpharma) on the EPIC Act track. Do not publicly oppose any PBM-reform extensions to commercial markets; the bipartisan coalition is more useful as a structural counterweight to PBM lobbying than as a Roche-opposition target.

## Swiss Federal Council (acting through SECO + FDFA)

**Stated position.** Preserve the November 2025 framework. Defend the 15% tariff cap and the 0% MFN-deal Section 232 track. Maintain Swiss "honest broker" positioning in transatlantic trade.

**Telos.** The Swiss Federal Council's deep telos is the **preservation of Swiss exceptionalism** — the right to be treated differently in trade negotiations because of historical neutrality, multilateral hosting (Geneva, WTO, WIPO, WHO), and CHF stability. The November 2025 framework is precious not primarily because of its terms but because it confirms that Switzerland receives bilateral treatment that the EU, Norway, and Israel did not receive on the same terms. The Trump administration's willingness to negotiate bilaterally with Bern (rather than slot Switzerland into a generic European-tariff bucket) is the substantive achievement.

**Contradiction.** Bilateral treatment under Trump is purchased at the cost of being seen as a vassal — accepting tariff caps the EU could not have negotiated. The 39%→15% reduction is a "win" only against an artificial baseline that Trump set. Internally, the Federal Council's collegial decision-making (seven members, four parties) means any deal must accommodate the most skeptical member, currently SVP-aligned skepticism about US dependency.

**Unexpected alliance.** Switzerland and Israel — both small, both bilateral-favored under Trump, both pharma-relevant (Teva, Compugen) — have a shared interest in **bilateral-deal-preservation architecture** that is stronger than EU-style frameworks. They can quietly coordinate on procedural questions (how to lock in bilateral treatment against administration reversal) without violating neutrality. This alliance has not been public but is structurally probable.

**Action implication.** Roche should encourage SECO to brief Israeli counterparts on the firewall annex template. If both bilateral-favored countries adopt the same confidential-net-price firewall architecture, the precedent becomes a sub-regime in international pharma pricing — much harder to dislodge than a Swiss-only carve-out. Bern has its own interest in proliferating the template (because proliferation legitimizes the bilateral form), so this can be framed as a Swiss diplomatic initiative, not a Roche request.

## Swiss cantons (Basel-Stadt + Basel-Landschaft)

**Stated position.** Preserve cantonal corporate-tax base (Roche + Novartis ≈ one-third of BS corporate tax); protect Basel pharma employment (~11,900 Roche Basel-site employees); maintain Basel as a global pharma cluster.

**Telos.** The Basel cantonal interest is not really "preserve Roche tax revenue." It is **preserve Basel as a multi-decade scientific cluster** — the equivalent of Boston for biotech, Stockholm for medical devices, Cambridge for chemistry. The cluster effect compounds: Roche + Novartis + ETH + Uni Basel + the ~700 life-science firms in the region = a self-reinforcing innovation ecosystem that survives any single firm's relocation decision. The Standortpaket recycling of OECD Pillar 2 revenue back to large pharma is one mechanism among many; the underlying logic is that Basel's competitive position rests on the cluster, not on any single firm.

**Contradiction.** Basel's "pay-to-stay" tax design weakens its bargaining position against future relocations. The more BS subsidizes Roche to stay, the lower Roche's switching cost in the medium term. This is a multi-decade incentive misalignment that cantonal policy cannot fully resolve: BS must subsidize today, even though doing so erodes leverage for tomorrow.

**Unexpected alliance.** Basel-Stadt and the EU Commission share a structurally aligned interest in **pharma-industrial-policy that does not get hollowed out by US capacity-pull**. The Commission's Critical Medicines Act (provisional 12 May 2026) is, in part, a Basel-aligned position: keep manufacturing in Europe. A quiet Basel-EU alliance could be effective: BS supports CMA implementation in exchange for EU recognizing the Swiss firewall annex as compatible-not-competitive with EU industrial policy. Basel's institutional weight is small but its cluster-credibility is high; the Commission would welcome a non-EU European cluster's endorsement.

**Action implication.** Roche should explicitly cultivate Basel–family–Bern coordination. The founding family's stewardship logic and Basel's cluster-preservation logic are *the same logic at different scales*. Roche-corporate cannot easily convene this conversation (it has the appearance of capture); the family can.

## Swiss public & domestic payers (BAG/FOPH + Santésuisse + curafutura + LAMal patients)

**Stated position.** Lower domestic drug prices on the Spezialitätenliste; resist any treaty mechanism that raises Swiss prices to subsidize US benchmarks; defend BAG's statutory authority under KVG Article 52a.

**Telos.** The Swiss public's deep telos in healthcare is **avoiding the Americanization of healthcare cost burden** — premium-affordability anxiety is the dominant Swiss political fear. The 2024 dual defeats of the Kostenbremse (62.8% Nein) and Prämien-Entlastungs (55.5% Nein) initiatives mean the Swiss public has *reluctantly accepted* current trade-offs but remains structurally cost-anxious. Any salient case (a high-priced new Alzheimer's drug; a 30% premium increase; a Schinecker-style public ask) can rupture the implicit acceptance.

**Contradiction.** The Swiss public wants low premiums AND a robust pharma ecosystem (Roche jobs, taxes, R&D) AND innovative therapies (Pluvicto, Vabysmo, trontinemab successors). These three demands are mutually constraining. Currently the constraint is masked by political quiescence — but a single Schinecker-style misstep can activate all three publics (German-speaking, Romandie, Ticino) simultaneously.

**Unexpected alliance.** Swiss public and US patient advocacy (AARP, P4AD) have a parallel interest in **drug-price transparency** but do not coordinate because the institutional vehicles differ. *A Schinecker public ask to raise Swiss prices to subsidize US MFN benchmarks would activate both publics simultaneously* and create the first real US-CH consumer alignment. This is a tail risk Roche must structurally avoid.

**Action implication.** No Roche executive should publicly link Swiss-domestic-pricing to US-MFN negotiations while the firewall annex is being negotiated. The October 2025 Schinecker ask was a strategic error that Bern is still managing. The firewall language must explicitly stipulate that Swiss domestic prices are *not a treaty parameter* — this serves Roche's interest in keeping the two publics decoupled.

## Novartis

**Stated position.** Signed MFN December 2025; protect Pluvicto/Lutathera/Cosentyx/Kisqali margins (Round 3 IRA cohort); coordinate joint Swiss-pharma advocacy with Roche; Narasimhan chairs PhRMA in 2026.

**Telos.** Novartis's deep telos differs from Roche's: it is **executing a major portfolio-platform pivot** from biologics-and-small-molecules to radioligands + cell-and-gene therapy. Pluvicto, Lutathera, Kymriah are the strategic bets. The MFN exposure on legacy products (Cosentyx, Tabrecta) is manageable; the *strategic* concern is protecting the radioligand business model where there is no biosimilar pressure and the manufacturing barrier-to-entry is high.

**Contradiction.** Novartis publicly cooperates with Roche on Swiss-front pharma advocacy but quietly competes for the same EU industrial-policy resources, the same Boston Hub leadership, the same regulatory attention. As PhRMA chair in 2026, Narasimhan is technically the industry's voice but has private interest in shaping that voice to favor Novartis's portfolio profile (radioligand-friendly, manufacturing-intensive) over Roche's profile (biologic-firewall-friendly, IP-protection-heavy).

**Unexpected alliance.** Novartis and biosimilar manufacturers have an under-appreciated shared interest. Novartis spun off Sandoz in October 2023, but the Basel cluster overlap means Sandoz and other biosimilar firms still rely on Swiss regulatory expertise. They are *not enemies* despite formal separation; they have aligned interests on BPCIA interchangeability and on EU pharma legislation. Roche should not expect a unified "Swiss pharma front" against biosimilars.

**Action implication.** Engage Novartis at the chairman level (Schwan ↔ Caforio), not just CEO level (Schinecker ↔ Narasimhan). Joerg Caforio replaced Reinhardt in 2025 and is less invested in the legacy Reinhardt-Schwan relationship. This is an opportunity to reset the joint-Swiss-pharma-advocacy framework on terms more favorable to Roche's portfolio profile — specifically, anchoring the firewall annex as a biologics-protective architecture before Novartis can shape it toward radioligand priorities.

## US PBMs & commercial payers

**Stated position.** Preserve rebate-arbitrage business model; oppose extension of Medicare Part D delinking to commercial markets; defend formulary control.

**Telos.** PBMs' deep telos is **information arbitrage in the prescription-drug supply chain**. They exist because the chain is opaque and they sit in the opacity. Delinking, transparency, and TrumpRx all attack the *foundation* of their business — not just a feature. The CAA 2026 Part D delinking law (signed 3 February 2026) represents the failure mode of their public position: when the gap between what they publicly claim ("lower drug costs") and what they actually do (rebate arbitrage) collapses against policy reality.

**Contradiction.** PBMs cannot publicly defend their information-arbitrage business model. They must publicly claim to "lower drug costs." This creates a permanent gap between public messaging and structural interest. Their political vulnerability is therefore *narrative*, not *substantive* — they lose any debate framed around "what do you actually do?" Cigna's Express Scripts pivot to rebate-free commercial contracting (October 2025) signals that the Big 3 will not negotiate as a bloc; the gap is being managed differently across the three firms.

**Unexpected alliance.** PBMs and Roche have a sub-rosa shared interest: both benefit from confidential net pricing. If TrumpRx expands to commercial markets, both lose. The natural alliance is *against* full price transparency — but it cannot be public for either of them. This is genuine confluence of interest that *only* the model surfaces because it is structurally invisible to standard pharma-policy frames.

**Action implication.** Roche should not publicly oppose PBM reform (politically toxic). But the firewall annex should distinguish between US-channel list-price transparency (settled; not contestable) and ex-US net-price confidentiality (at stake). The PBM lobby will naturally support this distinction; let them carry the public case while Roche stays out of the PBM debate.

## Patient advocacy (AARP, P4AD, disease-specific groups)

**Stated position.** Lower out-of-pocket costs; preserve access to innovative therapies; defend IRA negotiation rounds.

**Telos.** Patient advocacy is *genuinely three movements with three teloses*, not one:

- **AARP (~38M members):** defend Medicare and the social contract; 20th-century organization with vast electoral leverage but slow institutional reflex.
- **P4AD (~40K patient-story network):** reform-driven, refuses pharma money, weaponizes "front group" disclosures; 21st-century organization with media leverage but no electoral muscle.
- **Disease groups (ACS CAN, NMSS, AAFA, Allergy & Asthma Network, etc.):** protect specific patient populations; structurally dependent on pharma funding (≥83% take industry money per KFF); private interest in brand-pricing protection.

**Contradiction.** All three claim to represent "patients" but their teloses diverge sharply. AARP cares about premiums. P4AD cares about pricing reform. Disease groups care about access continuity (which requires drugs to stay launched in the US). A deal that improves any one dimension can hurt the others.

**Unexpected alliance.** P4AD and Roche share an unexpected long-term interest in **honest pharma-funding disclosure**. P4AD wants disclosure because it weaponizes the disclosure against industry-funded disease groups. Roche can *want* disclosure too — because it would make clear that Roche's funding to patient groups is for legitimate reasons (PAP administration, clinical-trial recruitment, education) rather than for advocacy. If Roche led the industry toward industry-leading pharma-funding disclosure standards, it would neutralize P4AD's "front group" attacks against Roche-funded disease groups while distinguishing Roche's funding logic from Pfizer's or AbbVie's.

**Action implication.** Pre-position Roche to announce industry-leading pharma-patient-group funding disclosure in 2027. Not now (during MFN renegotiation). Later, as a separate ESG / governance initiative — but with the strategic goal of pre-empting P4AD's "front group" framing of Roche-funded patient advocacy. This is a multi-year reputation play, ideally suited to the family-stewardship horizon.

## EU reference-pricing apparatus (EMA + G-BA/IQWiG + HAS + NICE + Commission DG SANTE)

**Stated position.** Preserve confidential net-pricing methodologies; defend EU industrial policy on pharma manufacturing; oppose treaty-level disclosure of EU net prices to satisfy US MFN-basket calculation.

**Telos.** The EU pharma reference-pricing apparatus's deep telos is **the construction of EU pharmaceutical sovereignty** — a project that has been gathering momentum since COVID (Vaccine Strategy → HERA → Pharma Package → Critical Medicines Act, provisional 12 May 2026). The Commission, MEPs, and individual member states' HTA bodies are aligned on the meta-objective: Europe must not depend on US administrative pricing decisions.

**Contradiction.** EU industrial policy has historically pressed prices *down* (G-BA / NICE methodology pressures prices low). But the Critical Medicines Act and confidentiality-friendly mechanisms (Germany MRA, January 2025) reflect a different logic: keep margins high enough to retain EU manufacturing. The two pulls — affordability versus sovereignty — are now in active tension. The Commission cannot publicly resolve this contradiction; it manages it through procedural compartmentalization.

**Unexpected alliance.** The EU Commission and the **the founding family** have structurally aligned long-horizon interests in European pharma-innovation-infrastructure persistence. At 2-3 year horizons they conflict (price negotiations). At 20+ year horizons they are aligned (cluster survival, innovation pipeline depth). The family logic of "we will be here in 2076" and the Commission logic of "EU pharma sovereignty by 2050" point in the same direction at multi-decade horizons.

**Action implication.** This is the most counter-intuitive alliance the model surfaces. Roche-family-level leadership (not Roche-corporate-level) could quietly engage EU Commission strategic-autonomy leadership on the question of *how to preserve a healthy EU pharma-innovation pipeline through the 2030s*. This is a different conversation from price negotiation; it is about treating Roche as EU industrial infrastructure rather than as a private-sector counterparty. The founding family's stewardship logic is uniquely suited to this conversation — corporate Roche cannot have it without seeming self-interested.

## Biosimilars & competitors (Sandoz, Celltrion, Samsung Bioepis, Amgen biosimilars, Boehringer, Teva)

**Stated position.** Maximize biosimilar uptake/share; protect BPCIA 180-day interchangeable exclusivity; oppose strengthening of patent/IP protection above current.

**Telos.** Biosimilar manufacturers exist as a **structural pressure mechanism on brand pricing** — they are the demonstrably-priced floor that justifies brand-margin politics. Their telos is to *continue existing as a credible threat*, not necessarily to capture massive market share. The demonstrable price cut (30-80% off brand) is the political asset, even where uptake is modest.

**Contradiction.** Biosimilars need brands to remain expensive (so price cuts are demonstrable) AND they need brands to lose patent protection (so they can launch). Pre-LOE, they are aligned with high brand prices; post-LOE, they want fast switching. Roche's Ocrevus 2029 patent cliff puts Roche on the wrong side of the biosimilar telos within three years.

**Unexpected alliance.** Biosimilars and US Congress (specifically the PBM-reform bipartisan coalition) share an interest in **commercial-market delinking**. Biosimilars benefit from lower formulary-steering barriers; the PBM-reform coalition wants policy wins post-Part-D-delinking. Roche should expect biosimilars to publicly support the bipartisan commercial-PBM-reform push in 2026-2027.

**Action implication.** Plan now for the post-2029 Ocrevus biosimilar landscape. Roche's defenses against Ocrevus biosimilarization should be *clinical and formulation* (next-generation MS therapeutic — trontinemab readouts 2028+), not *legal*. Litigation against biosimilars consumes goodwill the firewall annex needs.

## Investors

**Stated position.** Preserve dividend continuity (39th consecutive increase in 2026); protect margin; maintain capex efficiency; deliver Q4 2026 guidance.

**Telos.** Roche investors split into two structurally different telos populations dressed as one investor class:

- **the family-held voting bloc:** multi-generational stewardship — Roche as a productive scientific institution that pays dividends sufficient for family financial security across generations. The family's wealth horizon is multi-century; Roche's stewardship is one generation's responsibility.
- **Institutional + retail (rest):** total shareholder return on 3-5 year horizons — quarterly margin, dividend continuity, ESG ratings, sell-side narrative. Quarterly earnings are existential at this horizon.

**Contradiction.** The founding family logic can absorb 2026–2028 margin compression for 2030+ pipeline value. Institutional logic cannot. Sell-side coverage (UBS Buy CHF 384, Berenberg Hold CHF 320, RBC) reflects institutional, not family, logic. Roche's investor base *appears* quantitatively aligned but is *qualitatively split*. The board must navigate this split deliberately.

**Unexpected alliance.** The founding family and Basel-Stadt cantonal government have a structurally aligned cluster-preservation telos at different scales. They never appear in the same political room but their interests are nearly identical on Roche's strategic timeline.

**Action implication.** The family-held voting bloc is the single most important strategic asymmetry Roche has against every other actor at the negotiation. The administration, Congress, PBMs, biosimilars, institutional investors all operate on political-cycle or earnings-cycle timeframes. Roche operates on a multi-generation timeframe. *The firewall annex should be valued on a 25-year horizon. At that horizon, every analysis flips.*

## Synthesis: the four moves that flow from the telos analysis

1. **Negotiate the firewall annex with USTR, not HHS** — match the institutional telos (durability) to the instrument (treaty form). Article-of-trade language, not executive-order language.

2. **Convene Basel–family–Bern coordination** — formalize the alignment that already exists between cantonal cluster logic and family stewardship logic. Quarterly informal, semi-annual structured. The forum's existence is itself a strategic signal to other actors.

3. **Open quiet family–Commission dialogue on multi-decade EU pharma-innovation infrastructure** — only at family level, not corporate level. Frame as European industrial-infrastructure stewardship, not as price negotiation.

4. **Pre-position industry-leading pharma-funding disclosure for 2027** — neutralize P4AD "front group" framing on a multi-year horizon. Treats Roche as a different kind of pharmaceutical company than Pfizer or AbbVie, on the dimension that the family stewardship logic actually makes credible.

These are not in the [board recommendation](recommendation.html) directly — they are the substrate that makes the recommendation's three moves coherent. The recommendation tells the board *what to do in 2026*. This page explains *why those moves are the right moves given who is actually at the table and what they are actually doing*.
