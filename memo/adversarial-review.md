# Adversarial review

> A red-team attack on the recommendation. Each section asks: where could the analysis be wrong, and what evidence would change my mind? Written so a sceptical board member can engage with the argument without dismissing it.

**Snapshot:** 19 May 2026 · companion to [the board recommendation](recommendation.html)

---

## How to read this page

For every load-bearing claim in the recommendation, this page documents:

- **The claim** — stated as the recommendation states it
- **The strongest objection** — the argument a hostile critic would make
- **What evidence would settle it** — what would have to be true for the objection to bite
- **Current verdict** — whether the model's recommendation survives the objection on present evidence

The point is not to weaken the recommendation; it is to surface where it is *contingent* and what to monitor. A recommendation that survives its own red team is more defensible than one that hasn't been challenged.

## Objection 1 — The firewall annex is unachievable politically

**The claim.** "Press for symmetric firewall language in the Swiss bilateral annex, modelled on Germany's January 2025 Medical Research Act confidential Erstattungsbetrag mechanism. Closes before the Section 232 effective date of 31 July 2026."

**The strongest objection.** The US Executive's stated MFN program is structurally *transparency-favouring* — visible US-channel price commitments, visible OECD-floor benchmarking, visible TrumpRx pricing. A confidential firewall is the opposite of MFN's political optics. The administration cannot publicly support a "confidential pricing" mechanism without contradicting its own narrative; the proposal is therefore politically infeasible regardless of its analytical elegance.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- A US official (USTR, HHS, Commerce Section-232 lead) on the record acknowledging that confidential net pricing is acceptable in bilateral framework annexes if the *list-price* MFN commitment is preserved.
- A precedent: did the December 2025 MFN deals include any confidentiality language about non-US channels? The 8-K disclosures from the nine December signatories would reveal this. *Verification gap in the [disclosures register](disclosures.html).*
- A statement from Swiss SECO that they have raised the firewall question with USTR and received a non-negative response.

**Current verdict.** The recommendation is *contingent on US-side acceptance of a Swiss-annex confidentiality layer*. The structural argument (USTR's legacy-conscious faction prefers durable bilateral form, German MRA precedent) is plausible but unverified. **A board endorsement of decision (a) should be conditional on USTR back-channel signalling within 60 days.** If no signal is forthcoming, fall back to a less ambitious deliverable: international-reference-pricing protection at 5/10 (vs the target 7/10) via existing confidential-net-price commercial practice, no annex amendment required.

## Objection 2 — The Trump administration's preference for EO form is misread

**The claim.** "Roche and the Trump administration share an interest in *treaty form* over executive-order form. Roche wants durability; the administration wants legacy. Both lose if MFN is erased by a successor EO in 2029."

**The strongest objection.** The Trump administration's *stated* preference is for executive action, tariffs, and administrative discretion — the entire MAGA philosophical position is *against* multilateral treaty form. Trump himself withdrew from the TPP, the Paris Agreement, the JCPOA, and weakened USMCA-Mexico-Canada commitments. To assert that this administration prefers treaty durability over EO flexibility is to attribute to it an institutional patience it has never demonstrated. The "legacy-conscious faction" inside USTR / HHS career staff *may* exist, but they are not the decision-makers.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- A pattern of recent Trump-administration deals being structured as binding bilateral instruments rather than EOs. The November 2025 US–Swiss framework is a *Joint Statement* (which is treaty-adjacent but not a Senate-ratified treaty). The MFN deals themselves are *voluntary firm-level agreements*, not legislation. The pattern *currently observable* is administrative form, not treaty form.
- Statements from USTR leadership indicating preference for bilateral treaty instruments going forward.
- Historical precedent: the Trump-Iran negotiations (JCPOA exit), the USMCA renegotiation, North Korea diplomacy — these reveal a preference for visible-but-revocable forms.

**Current verdict.** The claim is *aspirational, not evidential*. The recommendation should treat it as a hypothesis to *test* through quiet USTR engagement rather than as a settled premise. **If USTR is unreceptive to treaty form within 90 days, the recommendation's Move 1 strategy must shift to entrenching the firewall via congressional mechanism (EPIC Act vehicle, CAA 2027 attachment) rather than bilateral form.** The substance of the firewall is recoverable through legislation; only the procedural elegance is lost.

## Objection 3 — The Hoffmann-EU alliance is operationally vague

**The claim.** "The founding family and the EU Commission share a multi-decade interest in European pharma-innovation-infrastructure persistence. Recommend the chairman initiate periodic, low-profile dialogue with DG SANTE strategic-autonomy leadership."

**The strongest objection.** This is a *talking-point alliance*, not an actionable one. "Quiet dialogue between family ownership and EU Commission strategic-autonomy leadership" sounds sophisticated but has no concrete mechanism: no agenda, no deliverable, no measurable outcome. It is the kind of recommendation that wins applause in strategy decks and accomplishes nothing in execution.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- A specific named EU Commission interlocutor (Olivér Várhelyi, Health Commissioner; Stella Kyriakides predecessor; relevant DG SANTE Director-General) who has signalled openness to multi-decade industrial-infrastructure dialogue with European pharma ownership.
- A concrete shared deliverable: e.g., joint advocacy for amending the Pharma Package's market-protection clause; coordinated position on Critical Medicines Act implementation; shared input to JCA methodology for oncology Phase 3 evidence weighting.
- A precedent: have similar family-ownership-to-Commission dialogues occurred with other European pharma families (Merck-Darmstadt, Bayer, Servier)? Are these productive?

**Current verdict.** The alliance is structurally real but operationally vague. **The board should not endorse the "open quiet dialogue" recommendation as currently stated.** Instead: ask the chairman to identify one specific deliverable that such a dialogue would produce in 12 months, and structure the engagement around that deliverable. If no concrete deliverable can be named, the alliance is interesting but not actionable, and the recommendation in [strategic dynamics §10](dynamics.html#the-unexpected-alliances) should be downgraded from action item to monitoring item.

## Objection 4 — The model overweights the multi-generational asymmetry

**The claim.** "The board should evaluate the three moves on a 25-year payoff, not a 2-year one. At that horizon, every analysis flips."

**The strongest objection.** The 25-year horizon is a *family-stewardship-side* construct, not a Roche-corporate construct. Most board members are not family representatives; CEO Schinecker is on a CEO timeline; institutional investors are on a 3–5 year timeline; executive compensation packages reward 2–5 year performance. To say "the entire analysis flips at 25 years" is to assert that family logic dominates corporate logic, when in practice it constrains but does not dominate. A board that overweights the family horizon may underweight the immediate quarterly compression that costs Schinecker his position before any 25-year vision can play out.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- The actual reported time horizon of recent major Roche capital-allocation decisions: was the $50B US capex pledge framed in 5-year terms or longer?
- Schinecker's own framing: in the January 2026 CNBC interview, did he speak in quarterly terms or in multi-decade terms?
- Family voting block behaviour: has the family pool *publicly* voted on any item where its long-horizon preference differed from institutional preference?
- Schwan's 97.75% re-election margin in March 2026: was this driven by family + institutional alignment, or by family alone with institutional indifference?

**Current verdict.** The 25-year framing is *useful as a constraint check* — i.e., "would this move look good in 2050?" is a defensible filter. It is *misleading as an evaluation metric* — i.e., evaluating ROE compression only against 25-year payoff would lose institutional support before the long horizon arrives. **The board should use the long horizon as a veto check (does this move pass a 25-year test?) but the short horizon as the primary scoring (does this move clear Q4 2027 institutional expectations?).** The recommendation should be reframed accordingly: both horizons matter; either alone misleads.

## Objection 5 — The non-principals at BATNA are not a "fragile equilibrium"

**The claim.** "The May 2026 status quo is feasible but barely. Roche, the US Executive, and the Swiss Federal Council all clear their BATNAs. Every other actor sits exactly at BATNA. The system is one perturbation away from a non-principal walking, and a non-principal walk is politically expensive even when contractually irrelevant."

**The strongest objection.** Non-principals are not at the table. By definition, they cannot "walk" from a deal they did not sign. A non-principal whose BATNA is exactly at parity with the deal experiences *no surplus and no loss* — there is no political pressure to register dissatisfaction. The "fragility" framing imports a stability concept from coalitional games where every actor must consent. The mid-2026 negotiation is *not* such a game.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- Has a non-principal actor in the May 2026 status quo *publicly* objected to the deal terms? (AARP has been quiet; P4AD has criticised industry MFN deals as window-dressing but not walked from advocacy; EU has structurally responded via Critical Medicines Act.)
- Does the model's "below-BATNA → political cost" prediction match observed reality? If PBMs, patient advocacy, EU, and biosimilars all sit at exactly BATNA in the model and none have actually defected in observed events, the model's fragility prediction is *not yet supported* by behaviour.

**Current verdict.** The model's "fragile equilibrium" framing is *consistent with* observed quiescence (no non-principal has walked) but doesn't prove fragility. The risk is that **the model conflates "non-principal at BATNA" with "non-principal poised to walk"** — they are different states. **The recommendation should distinguish between "monitor for non-principal defection" (a maintenance posture) and "the system is fragile" (a crisis posture).** The former is the correct policy stance; the latter overdramatises.

## Objection 6 — The Section 232 sunset is not actually a cliff

**The claim.** "The Section 232 0% track sunsets 20 January 2029. Renegotiation must close *six to twelve months before* — i.e., in 2028 — to give Roche capex-planning lead time."

**The strongest objection.** The Section 232 0% track's January 2029 sunset is *a function of the December 2025 EO's effective date*, which is itself revocable. A successor administration in January 2029 can either: (a) extend the 0% track, (b) renegotiate at higher rates, (c) revoke entirely. The "cliff" framing assumes (b) or (c) is the default; in fact (a) is plausible if Roche has delivered its capex milestones — the next administration may simply *not want to disrupt* a working framework. The cliff is therefore *contingent on political turnover and bad-faith renegotiation*, neither of which is guaranteed.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- Section 232 sunset behaviour in prior administrations: how often do tariff exemption tracks sunset vs extend? The aluminium and steel Section 232 actions provide some precedent.
- Statements from likely 2029 administrations about pharma Section 232 posture.
- Capex-milestone delivery curve: if Roche is *ahead* on capex commitments by mid-2028, the political pressure for renewal is favourable; if behind, it is unfavourable.

**Current verdict.** The cliff is *real but not deterministic*. The recommendation's urgency to "close by 2028" is well-founded as a planning premise but the actual cliff probability is more like 40-60% than 100%. **The board's Move 2 authorisation of capex-allocation flexibility should be valued partly as cliff insurance and partly as flexibility — not as a forced response to a certain cliff.**

## Objection 7 — The novelty engine's suggestions are clever but untested

**The claim.** Move 3: "Pre-position a tail-risk hedge (Q1 2027 framework proposal). The novelty engine's strongest candidate is a reinsurance-style structure: Roche cedes a defined slice of US revenue above a price floor to a confidential pricing-protection facility, in exchange for capped downside if benchmarks shift globally."

**The strongest objection.** A reinsurance-style pharma-pricing hedge has *no precedent* in any major pharmaceutical market. The proposal is structurally elegant but operationally unprecedented. Counterparties for the "confidential pricing-protection facility" don't exist. The legal architecture (is this a derivative? a contract? a side-letter?) is undefined. The accounting treatment is unclear. Tax implications across US/CH/EU jurisdictions are unmapped. Recommending this as a workstream is fine; treating it as a serious Q1 2027 framework proposal is naive.

**What evidence would settle it.**

- Any precedent: have insurance industry models been applied to drug-revenue hedging in any prior context? (Catastrophe bonds in healthcare? Drug-price futures? Pharmacy-cost stop-loss?)
- Counterparty interest: would Swiss Re, Munich Re, or a US specialty reinsurer underwrite such a structure?
- Legal feasibility analysis from external counsel.

**Current verdict.** Move 3 should be **clearly labelled as exploratory R&D, not as a proposable framework**. The board "note only" status (d) in the recommendation correctly captures this. **The board should not endorse Move 3 as a 2026-2027 deliverable; it is appropriately positioned as a multi-year exploration that may or may not yield a viable instrument.** Reframe in the recommendation accordingly.

## Summary: what survives the red team

After this review:

- **Move 1 (firewall annex) survives** as the primary 2026 priority, but with explicit conditionality on USTR back-channel signalling within 60 days. If unfavourable, downgrade to congressional vehicle.
- **Move 2 (milestone-gated capex) survives** as the primary capital-allocation decision, but reframed: not as forced cliff-response but as cliff *insurance* with optionality value.
- **Move 3 (tail-risk hedge) downgrades** from "framework proposal Q1 2027" to "exploratory multi-year R&D workstream" — its current "note only" board status is correct but the surrounding language should match.
- **The Hoffmann-EU alliance recommendation downgrades** from "open quiet dialogue" to "identify one specific deliverable before initiating dialogue." If no deliverable can be named, the alliance is monitoring-only.
- **The "fragile equilibrium" framing softens** to "monitor for non-principal defection" — same operational posture, less crisis vocabulary.
- **The 25-year horizon framing is preserved as a veto check** but not as the primary scoring metric. Quarterly compression risk must be modelled alongside.
- **The treaty-form alliance with the Trump administration is treated as hypothesis, not premise.** Test it through engagement before relying on it.

What the red team *does not* shake:

- The international-reference-pricing firewall is Roche's highest-weight underused lever. The model's identification of this is robust.
- The model's calibration test passes — the actual December 2025 signed state is feasible.
- The Swiss internal-coherence concern is structurally real and the model's three-actor Swiss disaggregation is correct.
- The recommendation's *direction* (durability engineering rather than re-litigation) is correct; only the *certainty* with which moves are recommended needs softening.

The recommendation **survives the red team in substance** but **needs to be tonally more honest about conditionality**. A board memo that overstates certainty courts defeat when conditions shift; a memo that names its contingencies invites engagement and survives revision.
