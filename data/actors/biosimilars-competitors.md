# Biosimilars and Competitor Manufacturers

> The countervailing supply-side actor: every biosimilar launch is a public, demonstrable price cut, and they sit in the negotiation as the "if you don't move, we will" benchmark to Roche's biologics franchise.

*Snapshot: May 19, 2026.*

Covered actors: Sandoz (independent since Oct 2023), Samsung Bioepis, Celltrion, Amgen biosimilars, Teva, Boehringer Ingelheim biosimilars, and the trade vehicles — Biosimilars Forum, Medicines for Europe, and the Association for Accessible Medicines (AAM) / Biosimilars Council.

## Role

Biosimilars are the natural countervailing force to Roche's biologics pricing — they have already eroded Avastin, Herceptin, and Rituxan/MabThera since the 2019 US launches of Mvasi and Kanjinti, and they are now bearing down on Xolair (Celltrion's interchangeable Omlyclo cleared to launch September 1, 2026 under a Genentech settlement) and on the future Ocrevus franchise (Amgen's ABP 692 entered Phase 3 in February 2025; Celltrion's CT-P53 and a Samsung Bioepis ocrelizumab are also in the pipeline, with US patent expiry 28 March 2029). In the mid-2026 MFN/TrumpRx negotiation, they sit at the table not as a party Roche bargains *with* but as the empirical price floor that Roche, CMS, and the White House each cite to discipline brand pricing. Their authority is market-derived (ASP-discounted competition) and statutory (the BPCIA pathway and IRA "bona fide marketing" off-ramp). Observable distinct behavior: their trade associations testify *against* brand pharma and *against* PBM rebate steering, not against the Administration's MFN framework.

## Constituency

- Public shareholders (Sandoz on SIX/OTC, Amgen, Teva, Celltrion on KOSPI; Samsung Bioepis is a Samsung Biologics subsidiary; Boehringer is private).
- GPO and IDN buyers, provider-administered Part B sites (community oncology especially), employer/payer customers.
- 340B disproportionate-share hospitals — a fraught customer (high biologic spend, but biosimilar uptake is ~23 percentage points lower at 340B sites because the spread economics favor brand).
- Patient-access advocates aligned via the "lower-cost" frame.
"Losing the constituency" looks like: Sandoz missing the Q4 2026 Eylea biosimilar launch window or its Keytruda/Opdivo Phase 3 readouts; Celltrion failing to convert Omlyclo's interchangeability into formulary wins; AAM/Biosimilars Forum losing PBM-reform momentum on the Hill.

## Interests (private)

- Maximize US biosimilar uptake and ASP-protected share against brand rebate walls. **Weight: 10/10.** ([Biosimilars Council Congressional testimony — Craig Burton](https://biosimilarscouncil.org/news/biosimilars-council-executive-director-craig-burton-testifies-before-congress-on-biosimilars-crisis/), 2024; [AJMC — Burton Q&A on Stelara/IRA](https://www.managedhealthcareexecutive.com/view/the-power-of-pbms-and-biosimilars-2025-launches-of-the-stelara-biosimilars-the-ira-and-interchangeability-q-a-with-craig-burton-mba), 2025)
- Preserve and accelerate the interchangeability pathway — ideally collapse the biosimilar/interchangeable distinction at FDA approval (the Lee–Hassan bill). **Weight: 9/10.** ([S.1954 Biosimilar Red Tape Elimination Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1954), 2025)
- Stop PBM rebate steering toward brand and rein in PBM private-label biosimilars (Quallent, Cordavis, Nuvaila). **Weight: 9/10.** ([Biosimilars Forum — Reed statement on PBM monopoly](https://biosimilarsforum.org/2024/07/09/biosimilars-forum-calls-for-reform-of-the-pbm-monopoly/), July 9, 2024; [FTC–Express Scripts settlement](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/02/alerts-lifesciences-antc-express-scripts-settles-pbm-ftc-action), Feb 4, 2026)
- Force IRA "bona fide marketing" determinations to *count* — getting a brand off the negotiation list as soon as a biosimilar launches protects pricing headroom and creates real switch incentives. **Weight: 8/10.** ([Hogan Lovells — CMS final guidance, Stelara/Entresto/Xarelto delisting](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/cms-issues-final-guidance-on-ira-2027-drug-price-negotiation-program-2026-27-mfp-effectuation), 2025)
- Protect 180-day first-interchangeable exclusivity under BPCIA §351(k)(6). **Weight: 7/10.** (inferred from statutory positioning; see [Goodwin — May 2025 EOs](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/blogs/2025/05/president-trump-issues-two-executive-orders-concerning-the-affordability-and-accessibility-of-drugs), May 15, 2025)
- Maintain capex incentive in US biologics manufacturing (Sandoz, Boehringer have US-presence stories; the Korean players do not). **Weight: 6/10.** (inferred)
- Keep MFN narrowly tailored to single-source brands so it does *not* drag biosimilar ASPs further down or compress brand prices to the point biosimilar uptake collapses. **Weight: 7/10.** ([CSIS — MFN paradox](https://www.csis.org/analysis/securing-future-us-biopharmaceutical-industry-most-favored-nation-paradox), 2026; [HHS scope confirmation — MFN applies only to single-source brands](https://www.thefdalawblog.com/2025/05/first-few-details-on-mfn-pricing-emerge-from-hhs/), May 2025)

## BATNA

No deal in the MFN/TrumpRx package still leaves biosimilars with a working growth path:
- Continued state-level pharmacist substitution wins (49 states now have substitution statutes for interchangeables).
- Continued IRA "bona fide marketing" delistings as launches occur — Stelara, Entresto, Xarelto are the proof case from CMS's 2025 announcement.
- Continued private-label and contracting workarounds (Blue Shield of California's 90%-off Humira biosimilar deal with Fresenius Kabi via Evio is the template).
- Pivot capacity and capital to ex-US launches (Europe, Canada, LATAM, Asia) where launch timelines are shorter and tender mechanics favor multi-source supply.
Payoff floor: biosimilar segment continues to compound roughly 30–40% on the Amgen/Sandoz revenue base, plus Omlyclo, Opuviz, Afqlir, and the Stelara biosimilars maturing in 2026–27.

## Red lines

- **Any MFN structure that protects brand margins by reducing competitive pressure on brand pricing while leaving biosimilar reimbursement (ASP+6, Part D placement) unchanged.** This would let brands quietly hold formulary position while pretending to discount via DTC channels. Constituency reason: biosimilar share growth is the only metric their boards and shareholders track.
- **Congressional weakening of BPCIA interchangeability or 180-day exclusivity** — this would gut the substitution economics. Constituency reason: it directly eliminates the legal predicate for state pharmacist substitution.
- **MFN scope creep onto biosimilars themselves** — HHS has so far ringfenced biosimilars out; the trade groups will go nuclear if that changes.
- **CMS reading "bona fide marketing" so narrowly that a brand stays on the IRA negotiation list despite a biosimilar launch** — collapses the off-ramp the IRA was supposed to provide.

## Leverage

- Empirical, observable price cuts: trastuzumab biosimilars at 52% ASP discount, bevacizumab at 49%, rituximab at 66% (CMS Q1 2025 ASP data via ASPE). When CMS, the White House, or Congress needs a number to cite against brand pharma, the biosimilars supply it.
- $189bn ten-year savings figure (Biosimilars Council, 2024) — the talking point that anchors every Hill hearing.
- Existing TrumpRx integration: Amgen, Genentech (Roche's US arm), Boehringer Ingelheim, Pfizer are already on the platform with biosimilar SKUs, which positions biosimilar manufacturers as the proof case that the DTC model "works."
- IRA bona fide marketing leverage: the trade groups effectively co-write CMS's standard for what counts as a real launch.
- Manufacturing footprint (Sandoz, Boehringer in particular) gives them a political story aligned with the Administration's reshoring rhetoric.

## Constraints

- BPCIA statutory architecture — 12-year reference exclusivity for the originator, mandatory patent dance, 180-day notice of commercial marketing.
- FDA review timelines and the still-extant biosimilar/interchangeable bifurcation until S.1954/H.R.5526 passes.
- Settlement-determined launch dates (Omlyclo: Sept 1, 2026; Opuviz: Jan 2027) — these are contractual ceilings.
- PBM formulary discretion — even with FDA approval and interchangeability, a private-label biosimilar (Cordavis, Quallent) can crowd out independents.
- Foreign-state actors (Celltrion, Samsung Bioepis are Korean; CinnaGen Iranian) face geopolitical exposure on MFN reference-price math and tariff/trade rhetoric.

- **September 1, 2026** — Celltrion Omlyclo US launch; first direct hit on Roche's Xolair/Genentech franchise.
- **Q4 2026** — Sandoz Afqlir (aflibercept) US launch.
- **January 2027** — Samsung Bioepis Opuviz (aflibercept) US launch.

- **March 28, 2029** — Ocrevus US patent expiry; Amgen ABP 692 Phase 3 readout in 2027–28 sets the competitive starting gun for Roche's largest MS franchise.
- **Annual** — CMS bona fide marketing determinations for IRA-listed drugs; each one converts a brand off the negotiation list and re-prices the segment.

## Notes on intra-actor coalitions

- **Sandoz** (Swiss-listed, spun out of Novartis Oct 2023): largest pure-play, brand-adjacent culture, biggest pipeline (32 assets), most policy-aligned with the Administration's reshoring narrative.
- **Celltrion** (Korean, KOSPI-listed): most aggressive on first-mover interchangeability filings (Omlyclo, CT-P53 ocrelizumab). Highest risk tolerance on patent dance litigation.
- **Samsung Bioepis** (Korean, Samsung Biologics subsidiary): more cautious, settlement-driven (Regeneron settlement for Opuviz). Ocrelizumab in pipeline is the long-game Roche-adjacent bet.
- **Amgen biosimilars**: hybrid — Amgen is itself a brand innovator (Repatha, Tezspire), so its biosimilar advocacy is selectively asymmetric. Sits on both sides of the PhRMA/Biosimilars Forum line.
- **Boehringer Ingelheim**: private, German; Cyltezo gave it the interchangeability first-mover spotlight but commercial uptake has been modest.
- **Teva**: legacy-generics frame, biosimilars are a growth pivot rather than core identity.
- **Trade-association split**: Biosimilars Forum is biosimilar-pure-play (Amgen, Sandoz, Boehringer, Pfizer, Samsung Bioepis, Celltrion, Teva, Coherus, Fresenius Kabi, Organon). AAM/Biosimilars Council also encompasses small-molecule generics, so its IRA messaging blends "pill penalty" reform with biosimilar interchangeability. Medicines for Europe is structurally a European tender-policy body — relevant to MFN only because it controls the international reference prices the White House cites.
- Internal divergence on MFN: pure-play biosimilars want MFN narrow (no bleed into their segment); Amgen and Boehringer, which also sell brands, want MFN to fail outright.
