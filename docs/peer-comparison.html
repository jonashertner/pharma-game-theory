Overview

Peer comparison — where Roche actually sits

To: Roche Executive Committee · From: Strategic-analysis working group · Date: 20 May 2026 · Read time: 11 minutes

Why this exists

Throughout the analysis we make claims about Roche's position relative to peers: that Roche bears reference-pricing contagion at 2–3× the peer rate; that Roche's Dec 2025 outcome was stronger than the average; that the multi-generational time horizon is a Roche-specific asymmetry. This memo defends those claims with side-by-side comparison across eight global pharma peers on eight strategic vectors.

The eight peers (alongside Roche): Pfizer, Merck, Novartis, Eli Lilly, AbbVie, Bristol-Myers Squibb, AstraZeneca. Together with Roche, the nine firms cover the top of the global Pharma pricing-game table; AstraZeneca and Lilly were also in the December 2025 MFN-signing wave; Pfizer, Merck, and BMS signed later through April 2026; Novartis and AbbVie also signed.

Sourcing standard. Public disclosures (annual reports, press releases, SEC filings, CMS announcements) are cited inline where load-bearing. Forward-looking numbers are working-group estimates derived from disclosed growth rates × standard pharma analyst conventions, flagged where used.

The eight vectors

  1. MFN deal scope — what was ceded and when signed.
  2. IRA exposure — cumulative count and revenue impact across Rounds 1–3.
  3. Section 232 / manufacturing footprint — % of US biologics imported vs domestic.
  4. $US capex commitments — announced, sized, and timed.
  5. Pharma % of group — revenue concentration in pharma vs adjacent businesses.
  6. Reference-pricing contagion exposure — ex-US share, biologic concentration, confidential-rebate dependence.
  7. Pipeline density — top-3 forward assets and concentration risk.
  8. Ownership structure — family/founding-shareholder vs widely-held public float.

The headline reads from the matrix

Reading 1 — Roche's Dec 2025 outcome was genuinely better than the average

On Vector 1 (MFN scope), the peer-firm cession set is varied: most peers gave a single product to Medicaid MFN, with the higher-revenue retention of their growth drivers. Roche's outcome is at the better end of the distribution: Xofluza is a low-importance asset (CHF 407m FY25), and the growth-driver portfolio (Vabysmo, Ocrevus, Hemlibra, Phesgo) stayed entirely out of MFN scope. Pfizer's Eliquis cession is materially larger in revenue terms (~$4B); Merck's Januvia cession is similar to Pfizer scale; Lilly's Trulicity cession matters but the Mounjaro retention is the prize.

On Vector 3 (Section 232 footprint), Roche has the largest absolute exposure of the group (~85% Swiss/EU origin). But the 0% rate locked through January 2029 means the current exposure is zero. The peer firms with US-heavy footprints (AbbVie, Lilly, BMS) face less Section 232 risk in absolute terms but also captured less benefit from the rate concession. The asymmetry is conditional: if Section 232 sunsets without renewal, Roche faces material exposure that peers do not.

Reading 2 — The reference-pricing contagion claim is defensible

Vector 6 is where the “2–3× peer exposure” claim from the bottom-line analysis is tested. The stack:

Only Roche stacks all five factors at maximum exposure. AstraZeneca has higher ex-US revenue share but lower biologic-concentration; Novartis has Swiss HQ and biologic concentration but lower ex-US share. The peer ranking on reference-pricing contagion exposure is:

  1. Roche (highest)
  2. AstraZeneca, Novartis (high)
  3. Merck (moderate-high — Keytruda confidentiality is the single largest risk)
  4. Pfizer, BMS (moderate)
  5. AbbVie, Lilly (low-moderate)

The “2–3× peer rate” framing is approximately right if "peer rate" is read as the median of this ranking. The high end of the asymmetry claim (5×) is correct only if compared to the lowest-exposure peers (AbbVie, Lilly).

Implication for the firewall annex. Roche-specific firewall protection has more value to Roche than equivalent protection would have to any peer. Industry-coalition lobbying for transparency-defence (recommended in black swans #3) is a public good Roche disproportionately benefits from.

Reading 3 — Pipeline density positions Roche unusually well, with one named risk

Vector 7 shows Lilly with the most concentrated pipeline (GLP-1 LCM dominant) but the obesity franchise has unique pricing dynamics that may exempt it from the reference-pricing concerns governing the rest of the industry. Merck has Keytruda LCM dependency — the IRA Round 3 candidate Keytruda represents a peer-specific tail risk Roche does not share.

Roche's pipeline density is genuinely strong, with three independent forward franchises (trontinemab brain-shuttle, Itovebi PI3K, Vabysmo high-dose). But trontinemab's platform concentration (black swan #4) is a real risk — if TRONTIER fails on safety, the next-decade R&D narrative shifts to second-tier assets.

Reading 4 — The ownership-structure asymmetry is genuine, not branding

Vector 8 is the most material peer-distinguishing factor. All other peers are widely-held public companies subject to standard activist-investor pressures and quarterly-cycle attention. Roche's founding-family pool structure (referenced as “generational-shareholder structure” per board convention) is a unique asset that lets Roche absorb a 5–7 year strategic transition with a tolerance no peer has.

This is the underlying premise of the multi-generational time-horizon asymmetry. It is also the black swan #1 (“the horizon collapses”) trigger: the asymmetry exists because the ownership structure exists. If it dissolves, Roche becomes a comparable widely-held large-cap pharma optimising on quarterly cash flow and the strategic posture re-prices.

Where the matrix is most uncertain

Three cells are flagged as estimates rather than disclosure:

  1. Section 232 footprint percentages — manufacturers do not consistently disclose origin breakdown. Working-group estimates from facility footprint analysis; accurate to roughly ±10 percentage points.
  2. Pipeline density top-3 — subjective curation; reasonable analysts would adjust 1–2 entries per peer.
  3. IRA exposure forward — CMS has not selected Round 4 or 5 candidates; predictions are derived from current Part D / Part B spend rankings.

The peer-comparison reading would not change materially under reasonable alternative cell values in these three.

What this changes for the board

Three implications.

  1. The relative-to-peers framing throughout the analysis is defensible. Roche's reference-pricing contagion exposure is genuinely top-1 in the peer group; the Dec 2025 outcome is genuinely above the peer median; the ownership-structure asymmetry is genuinely Roche-only. Board-level confidence in these claims should be calibrated to "high."

  2. The firewall annex is more valuable to Roche than to any peer. Roche should lead the industry-coalition transparency-defence work, not free-ride on it. Resourcing implication: dedicated EU government-affairs capacity beyond Roche-specific lobbying, sized to influence sector-wide outcomes.

  3. Peer behaviour is itself a leading indicator. If Novartis (the closest peer on Vector 6) or AstraZeneca initiates an annex-style protection, that confirms the reference-pricing exposure model. If they do not over 2026, either Roche is wrong about the exposure or has discovered a unique strategic asset; monitoring peer disclosures and government-affairs hires is one of the cheapest leading-indicator investments available.
