Novelty engine — domain collision prompts

15 distant analogue domains · open-collider-style structural collision · pre-curated for Roche pricing context

When to use this

Use the novelty engine when standard moves have run out

The standard pharma-pricing playbook is well-trodden. Every actor's negotiators have read the same slide decks. When the model says "Pareto improvements exist but standard moves are exhausted," the novelty engine is the tool: it collides the negotiation with structurally distant problem-solving repertoires (reinsurance, climate finance, antitrust consent decrees, sovereign-debt restructuring, hostage negotiation, ...) to surface moves no one at the table has proposed yet.

Top three for the current scenario:

  • Reinsurance pooling — caps MFN tail risk via confidential quota-share with the US government. Aligns government incentive with Roche's market success.
  • Sovereign-debt CAC clauses — bind all 17 MFN signatories to comparable treatment, removing the holdout incentive that currently makes Roche's deal feel like a sweetheart carve-out.
  • Climate loss-and-damage fund — decouples US capex acceleration from Basel cantonal-tax-base damage. Removes the Swiss-internal blocker.
The novelty engine generates Claude prompts that collide the current scenario with each of these domains — surfacing negotiation moves that don't exist in any actor's standard playbook. Run python -m novelty.collide --scenario <name> from the repo root to write a fresh batch of prompts; the output is a Markdown file that you paste into a Claude session.

The 15 domains

Click any domain to expand its principles and Pharma-pricing relevance:

Climate finance NDC bargaining climate-finance-ndc

Nationally Determined Contributions under the Paris Agreement — voluntary commitments that countries set themselves and ratchet upward over time.

Operational principles

  • Asymmetric ambition (each party sets a target consistent with its own constraints; no global price)
  • Ratchet mechanism (commitments must rise on each 5-year cycle; backsliding politically expensive)
  • Common-but-differentiated responsibility (rich-world goes first)
  • MRV (measurement, reporting, verification) as the enforcement layer
  • Just-transition finance for affected industries

Why it matches

Pharma pricing is a multi-party asymmetric-power problem where any binding single-price commitment creates BATNA violations somewhere. The NDC structure of "set your own target, demonstrate progress, MRV verifies" lets Roche offer different price commitments per channel without a single MFN floor that EU/UK/CH reference back into.

Sources

  • https://unfccc.int/process-and-meetings/the-paris-agreement
Reinsurance pooling reinsurance-pooling

Risk transfer from primary insurers to reinsurers via quota-share, excess- of-loss, and retrocession layers — managing catastrophic tail risk.

Operational principles

  • Quota share (cede a fraction of every policy)
  • Excess of loss (cover only losses above an attachment point)
  • Retrocession (reinsurer cedes its own risk further up)
  • Confidential bordereaux (claims data shared only with the reinsurance pool)
  • Stop-loss caps that align incentives

Why it matches

MFN exposure is fundamentally a tail-risk problem for Roche: its expected value loss from MFN is moderate, but worst-case (full ex-US spillover) is catastrophic. Reinsurance-style structures could transform this: Roche cedes a slice of revenue above a price floor to a price-protection facility, in exchange for capped downside. Confidential bordereaux ⇄ confidential net-price agreements.

Sources

  • https://www.swissre.com/institute/research.html
Antitrust consent decrees antitrust-consent-decree

Settlement instruments where regulated firms accept ongoing behavioural remedies + a court-supervised compliance monitor in exchange for ending litigation.

Operational principles

  • Compliance monitor with subpoena powers
  • Behavioural remedies (no specific structural break-up)
  • Periodic reporting cycles (semi-annual to court)
  • Sunset clauses (typically 5–10 years)
  • Self-executing penalties for documented breaches

Why it matches

MFN is hard to verify across channels (Medicaid, Part B/D, TrumpRx, commercial). A consent-decree-style monitor (CMS-appointed, with access to non-public PBM rebate data) would replace public-list-price politics with documented compliance. A sunset (e.g., 5 years) handles MFN's political contingency.

Sources

  • https://www.justice.gov/atr/consent-decrees
Sovereign debt restructuring (Paris Club + IIF) sovereign-debt-restructuring

Coordinated bilateral debt relief for sovereign borrowers, with comparable-treatment clauses ensuring no creditor gets a sweetheart deal.

Operational principles

  • Comparable treatment (no creditor extracts more than others)
  • Collective Action Clauses (CACs) — supermajority can bind holdouts
  • Hidden NPV transfers (face value preserved; coupon stretched)
  • Debt-for-equity / debt-for-nature swaps
  • Trigger conditions tied to GDP growth, commodity prices

Why it matches

The MFN deal landscape has a holdout problem: a manufacturer that signs MFN sees competitors hold out and benefit from the political shield it provides. A CAC-style clause that binds all signatories to comparable treatment removes the holdout incentive. NPV-preserving structures (delayed effective dates, royalty-based payments) hide the discount while satisfying the political optic.

Sources

  • https://clubdeparis.org/en
Cap-and-trade emissions markets emissions-trading

Tradable permits to emit pollutants, with a declining cap that creates scarcity and a market price.

Operational principles

  • Aggregate cap declines over time (creates scarcity)
  • Tradable rights (most efficient abater wins)
  • Banking and borrowing across periods
  • Auction vs free allocation (revenue or rent-protection)
  • Border carbon adjustments to prevent leakage

Why it matches

The MFN framework currently runs on a per-firm voluntary basis with no market in compliance. A tradable price-concession market could let firms with high US exposure (Roche/Genentech) buy compliance from firms with low US exposure. Border carbon adjustment analogue: a Section-232 surcharge that's offsettable by purchased MFN credits.

Sources

  • https://ec.europa.eu/clima/eu-action/eu-emissions-trading-system-eu-ets_en
Patent pools / Medicines Patent Pool patent-pool-mpp

Voluntary licensing platform where rights-holders pool patents and generic manufacturers obtain non-exclusive licenses for LMIC supply.

Operational principles

  • Tiered royalties by income tier
  • Non-exclusive licensing (multiple generics, competitive pressure)
  • Geographic carve-outs
  • Quality and pharmacovigilance obligations
  • Pre-negotiated dispute resolution

Why it matches

The MPP separates the high-margin and low-margin geographies via licensed generics under controlled terms. Applied to MFN: Roche could license a US "competitor" generic-like channel via TrumpRx with capped volumes and royalties, preserving the brand price for commercial-insured while hitting the visible MFN-price political win.

Sources

  • https://medicinespatentpool.org/
WTO TRIPS flexibilities (Doha Declaration) wto-trips-flexibilities

Multilateral framework for compulsory licensing of patented medicines in public-health emergencies, with negotiated safeguards.

Operational principles

  • Compulsory license triggered by public-health declaration
  • Adequate remuneration via judicially-determined royalty
  • Limited duration tied to the emergency
  • Procedural safeguards (prior negotiation requirement, except emergency)

Why it matches

The compulsory-license threat is one of the US executive's strongest BATNAs. Pre-negotiating "adequate remuneration" formulas now would remove uncertainty: Roche accepts a defined royalty trigger in exchange for procedural safeguards that prevent ad-hoc march-in.

Sources

  • https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/trips_e/public_health_e.htm
Critical minerals supply agreements critical-minerals-supply

Long-dated offtake contracts between Western governments and producers for strategic materials, with price collars and stockpile guarantees.

Operational principles

  • Price collars (floor + ceiling)
  • Volume commitments (take-or-pay)
  • Strategic stockpile triggers
  • Government as price-of-last-resort buyer
  • Domestic content preferences

Why it matches

Trump's Section 232 framing treats pharma like critical minerals. The collar structure (Roche guaranteed a price floor on a basket of drugs, government guaranteed a ceiling) reframes the conflict from "MFN cuts Roche prices" to "long-dated industrial-policy contract with predictable terms." Stockpile triggers for emergency supply.

Sources

  • https://www.iea.org/topics/critical-minerals
Insurance quota share / cession insurance-quota-share

A reinsurer takes a fixed percentage of every premium and every loss on the ceded book — proportional risk sharing.

Operational principles

  • Proportional sharing of upside AND downside
  • Cession commission to reward profitable underwriting
  • Loss-corridor sharing
  • Slipping commissions (sliding scale tied to loss ratio)

Why it matches

A quota-share over Roche's US drug revenue with the US government: government takes X% of every dollar of revenue plus X% of every dollar of MFN-credited price concession. Aligns government's incentive with Roche's market success rather than against it.

Sources

  • https://www.actuaries.org.uk
Hostage crisis negotiation hostage-crisis-negotiation

Time-pressured, asymmetric-information bargaining with a captor seeking assurance and a negotiator seeking de-escalation.

Operational principles

  • Build the captor's BATNA (not just defeat them)
  • Face-saving exits (rituals of surrender that aren't humiliation)
  • Time as a stabiliser (long talks, not short ultimatums)
  • Selective concessions on low-symbolic / high-substantive issues
  • Mediator entry as escape valve

Why it matches

The Trump-administration "deal or duty" public framing is structurally a hostage frame. Face-saving is the central problem: Roche cannot be seen capitulating; the administration cannot be seen losing. Time- stabilising tactics (multi-year phase-ins, conditional triggers) and face-saving rituals (announcement choreography, third-party endorsers) come straight from this playbook.

Sources

  • Voss, Chris. *Never Split the Difference* (2016).
Construction EPC / milestone-gated finance construction-epc-finance

Large engineering-procurement-construction contracts use milestone-based payments with independent engineer certifications — releasing capital only as work is verified.

Operational principles

  • Milestone definition with independent engineer certification
  • Liquidated damages for schedule slippage
  • Retention escrow (5–10% withheld pending defects-correction period)
  • Performance bonds from third parties
  • Variation orders with predefined pricing

Why it matches

Roche's $50B US capex pledge has no enforcement mechanism. Milestone- gated tariff exemptions (build verified → next tariff tranche reduced) align Roche's investment timeline with the administration's political timeline. Retention escrow analogue: a portion of MFN price relief held back until the corresponding capex milestone is met.

Sources

  • FIDIC Conditions of Contract (https://fidic.org/)
Climate loss-and-damage fund climate-loss-damage-fund

Established at COP27/28 to compensate developing nations for climate-attributable losses they cannot adapt to.

Operational principles

  • Attribution science as eligibility test
  • Responsibility-vs-capacity ladder for contributions
  • Pre-agreed disaster triggers (parametric)
  • Avoiding "liability" framing politically toxic to contributors

Why it matches

The Swiss tax-base / Basel-cantons concern is a "loss and damage" problem: capacity relocation to the US causes specific identifiable harms to a specific identifiable jurisdiction. A loss-and-damage-style side fund (Roche or US government contributing into a Basel-cantonal adaptation fund) decouples the trade deal from the host-country welfare hit, removing a Swiss-side blocker.

Sources

  • https://unfccc.int/topics/adaptation-and-resilience/the-big-picture/loss-and-damage
Most-favored-customer clauses (in private contracts) most-favored-customer-clauses

Contract provisions where a seller guarantees a buyer the lowest price offered to any comparable buyer — the *private-contract* MFN.

Operational principles

  • Comparator definition (which buyers count?)
  • Carve-outs (volume tiers, channel-specific)
  • True-up mechanisms (retroactive credit if a better price is given elsewhere)
  • Audit rights with confidentiality bracket
  • Most-favored-nation only on specific dimensions

Why it matches

The structure inverts the government's MFN: rather than the US benchmarking to the lowest developed-country price, structure a most-favored-customer where US channels are guaranteed the *best commercial-market US price*. Removes the international-reference-pricing contagion entirely.

Sources

  • https://www.americanbar.org/groups/antitrust_law/
Diplomatic immunity carve-outs (Vienna Convention) diplomatic-immunity-carveouts

Functional vs personal immunity — diplomats are protected only for official acts, subject to negotiated exceptions.

Operational principles

  • Functional immunity narrower than personal immunity
  • Waiver of immunity by the sending state
  • Persona non grata as informal sanction
  • Reciprocity as base discipline

Why it matches

"Diplomatic carve-out" for Swiss pharma is currently nebulous. Vienna- Convention-style functional/personal split: certain Roche acts (US-channel pricing) governed by US law; certain acts (global-margin protection) governed by Swiss law. Reciprocity-anchored — Swiss-firm protection in US matches US-firm protection in Switzerland.

Sources

  • https://legal.un.org/ilc/texts/instruments/english/conventions/9_1_1961.pdf
Wild salmon catch-share concessions (Alaska / Pacific NW) salmon-fishing-concessions

Tradable individual fishing quotas with strict conservation cap, set annually by joint US-Canada-tribal commission.

Operational principles

  • Cooperative quota-setting body with all stakeholders
  • Annual reset (no permanent positions)
  • Indigenous-rights set-aside (some quota guaranteed to tribes)
  • Catch logbooks + observer requirement
  • Transferability between vessels of same fleet only

Why it matches

Annual reset is the key insight: structure the MFN cap as an annually- renegotiated quota with stakeholder body (manufacturers + administration + patient-advocacy + payers), rather than a permanent legal regime. Removes the legitimacy crisis of indefinite executive action and lets MFN adjust to actual market evidence.

Sources

  • https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/topic/laws-policies/catch-shares
