1. The legal instruments

(US government actions — the load-bearing legal documents of the 2025–2026 regime.)

1.1 Executive Order 14297 — "Delivering Most-Favored-Nation Prescription Drug Pricing to American Patients"

  • Filing type: Executive Order
  • Filer: President Donald J. Trump
  • Date signed: May 12, 2025
  • Federal Register publication: May 15, 2025; 90 FR 20749–20751; FR Doc. 2025-08876
  • Permalink (Federal Register): federalregister.gov/documents/2025/05/15/2025-08876
  • Permalink (White House): whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/05/delivering-most-favored-nation-prescription-drug-pricing-to-american-patients
  • What it does and what is non-obvious:
  • Directs HHS to communicate MFN price targets to manufacturers of single-source drugs within 30 days (by June 11, 2025).
  • If "significant progress" is not made, directs HHS to propose MFN rulemaking and FDA to facilitate importation of cheaper drugs from abroad.
  • Section 4 directs HHS Secretary to "facilitate direct-to-consumer purchasing programs for pharmaceutical manufacturers" — the legal foundation for TrumpRx.gov (which then launched February 5, 2026).
  • Directs the Attorney General and FTC Chair to bring antitrust enforcement against "anti-competitive practices" of foreign-pricing partners — a Sherman Act / Section 5 hook that was not in the failed 2020 MFN interim final rule.
  • Non-obvious: the EO frames MFN as a trade matter rather than a Medicare matter, which is why HHS could couple compliance with Section 232 tariff exemption (see §1.5) rather than enforce through CMS rulemaking only.

1.2 CRS Legal Sidebar LSB11319 — "Most-Favored-Nation Prescription Drug Pricing Executive Order: Legal Issues"

  • Filing type: Congressional Research Service Legal Sidebar
  • Filer: CRS (Library of Congress)
  • Permalink: congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11319 · PDF version
  • What it reveals:
  • The 2025 EO's enforcement architecture is materially different from the 2020 interim final rule (which was enjoined by three district courts on APA grounds in December 2020) because the 2025 EO relies on voluntary manufacturer agreements plus Section 232 tariff leverage, sidestepping the notice-and-comment vulnerability.
  • CRS flags constitutional risk under the unconstitutional conditions doctrine and possible First Amendment compelled-speech claims — the exact theories Novartis lost on at the Third Circuit (see §7.2).
  • HHS defines the MFN reference basket as OECD members with per-capita GDP ≥ 60% of US per-capita GDP — a narrower reference basket than the 2020 rule and one that excludes most lower-cost EU peers, making the effective MFN ceiling closer to Germany/UK/France than to Greece/Portugal.

1.5 Proclamation 11020 — "Adjusting Imports of Pharmaceuticals and Pharmaceutical Ingredients into the United States" (Section 232)

  • Filing type: Presidential Proclamation under §232 Trade Expansion Act 1962
  • Date signed: April 2, 2026
  • Federal Register publication: April 9, 2026; FR Doc. 2026-06956; 91 FR 18183
  • Permalink (Federal Register): federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/09/2026-06956
  • Permalink (White House): whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/04/adjusting-imports-of-pharmaceuticals-and-pharmaceutical-ingredients-into-the-united-states
  • Permalink (American Presidency Project — Proclamation 11020 text): presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/proclamation-11020-adjusting-imports-pharmaceuticals-and-pharmaceutical-ingredients-into
  • What it does:
  • 100% ad valorem duty on patented pharmaceuticals and APIs from manufacturers without an MFN agreement.
  • 20% reduced rate for companies with a Commerce-approved onshoring plan, rising back to 100% on April 2, 2030.
  • Country-specific caps embedded in trade deals: 15% ceiling for EU, Liechtenstein, Japan, South Korea, Switzerland; 10% ceiling for UK.
  • Annex II: MFN signatories — duty-exempt.
  • Annex III: 17 large companies — effective date July 31, 2026; all others September 29, 2026.
  • Generics, biosimilars, and US-origin pharmaceuticals carved out.
  • Non-obvious:
  • The 20%-to-100% step-up in 2030 creates a back-loaded compliance cliff that is not obvious from press coverage — companies that take the onshoring discount face a far steeper effective tariff in their long-range planning models than the headline 20% suggests.
  • The "15% Switzerland cap" is the operational hinge of the November 14, 2025 Joint Statement: it is what made the Swiss government's $200B investment commitment politically defensible at home.

1.6 Joint Statement — US/Switzerland/Liechtenstein Framework on Reciprocal Trade

  • Filing type: Joint Statement (framework, not binding treaty)
  • Date: November 14, 2025
  • Permalink (White House): whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/11/joint-statement-on-a-framework-for-a-united-states-switzerland-liechtenstein-agreement-on-fair-balanced-and-reciprocal-trade
  • Permalink (USTR): ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2025/november/ambassador-greer-issues-statement-framework-historic-deal-switzerland-and-liechtenstein
  • Permalink (White House fact sheet): whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/11/fact-sheet-the-united-states-switzerland-and-liechtenstein-reach-a-historic-trade-deal
  • What it does:
  • Caps US tariffs on Swiss/Liechtenstein originating goods at the higher of MFN or 15%, with carve-outs in the "Aligned Partners" Annex.
  • Critical pharma line: The US "intends to promptly ensure that the MFN tariff and the tariff imposed pursuant to Section 232 do not exceed 15 percent for originating pharmaceutical goods and semiconductors of Switzerland and Liechtenstein subject to Section 232 tariffs."
  • Switzerland commits to $200B and Liechtenstein $300M of US investment over five years.
  • Switzerland agrees to remove tariffs on a range of US agricultural products and to establish TRQs for US poultry, beef, bison.
  • Non-obvious:
  • The 15% pharma cap was operationalized in Proclamation 11020 (§1.5 above) five months later — so the framework statement is what locked in the pharma tariff math for Roche and Novartis well before the proclamation was signed.
  • It is a framework, not a treaty — neither chamber of Congress has ratified anything. The 15% cap therefore rests on continuing executive discretion under §232.

1.9 Public Law — Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026

  • Filing type: Public Law (omnibus appropriations + PBM Title)
  • Date signed: February 3, 2026
  • Permalink (KPMG / Sidley summary): sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2026/02/congress-passes-significant-federal-pharmacy-benefit-manager-reform-impacting-pharmaceutical-market
  • Permalink (Mintz): mintz.com/insights-center/viewpoints/2146/2026-02-06-congress-passes-landmark-pbm-reform-2026-spending-bill
  • What it does:
  • Part D PBM delinking: PBM compensation in Medicare Part D may no longer derive from rebates, spread pricing, or other price-linked revenue. Compensation must be "bona fide service fees" at fair market value.
  • 100% rebate pass-through to Part D plans.
  • Strengthens Any-Willing-Pharmacy (AWP) requirements; directs CMS to define "reasonable and relevant" contract standards.
  • Effective date for delinking: plan year beginning January 1, 2028 (calendar-year plans deferred to 2029).
  • Non-obvious:
  • Although signed February 3, 2026, the delinking does not bite until 2028 — giving PBM parents (CVS, Cigna, UnitedHealth) almost two years of margin runway. This is why Cigna's October 2025 "rebate-free by 2027" Express Scripts pivot (see §5.3) is voluntary front-running of the statute, designed to preempt a tighter rule.
  • The Act does not extend delinking to commercial markets — leaving the largest pool of PBM rebate revenue legally untouched.
  • Verification gap: I could not surface a single canonical Public Law number / GPO link for the bill text within the research window.

9. What the filings reveal that public statements don't

Cross-cutting synthesis across §§1–8:

9.1 The Section 232 tariff is the real enforcement engine — not voluntary MFN compliance.

The MFN Executive Order (§1.1) is structurally voluntary. What made signing mandatory in practice was the 3-year tariff grace period attached to MFN deals (Pfizer fact sheet §1.3; Annex II of Proclamation 11020 §1.5). Reading the 17 MFN announcements alongside the §232 Annexes shows that every signatory received explicit Annex II inclusion. The MFN regime is, functionally, a Section 232 tariff-credit program — not a Medicare pricing program. This is not visible in the headline fact sheets but is the operative legal architecture in the Proclamation and the proclamation's three Annexes.

9.2 The "Switzerland 15%" cap is the load-bearing concession.

Roche and Novartis disclosures cannot price the §232 regime at 100%; they price it at 15% (§232 cap from the Joint Statement §1.6, locked into Proclamation 11020 §1.5). This is why Roche's Q1 2026 report (§3.3) maintains full-year guidance: the worst-case exposure absent the cap would be enough to force guidance withdrawal, but with the cap and MFN-signatory Annex II treatment, the residual hit is materially smaller. The November 14, 2025 Joint Statement is therefore the most consequential single document for Swiss pharma from a P&L standpoint — more so than any of the company-specific MFN deals.

9.3 US-capex pledges aggregate to >$370B in five years — but a large fraction is recharacterized M&A and pre-existing plans.

Adding the disclosed US-investment commitments: Pfizer $70B + AstraZeneca $50B + Roche $50B + AbbVie $100B + others ≈ $370–450B+ across the 17 signatories. But the Pfizer figure includes the Metsera acquisition closed September 21, 2025 (pre-MFN-deal); Genentech's North Carolina plant ($700M, see BioSpace) was announced before the MFN deal; AstraZeneca's $50B includes Rockville campus expansion already planned. The headline aggregates are inflated by reclassification of in-flight spend.

9.4 The IRA program survived all major legal challenges in 2024–2025 — closing one strategic option.

The Third Circuit's September 4 and September 11, 2025 rulings (§§7.2, 7.3) and the broader pattern of pharma losses (every court that has reached merits has ruled against industry) mean the legal option to defeat IRA price negotiation is functionally exhausted absent Supreme Court intervention. This forecloses a litigation-only strategy and explains why Lilly publicly opposes MFN codification while accepting the executive MFN deal — they prefer a revocable EO regime to an irrevocable statute, because EO 14297 could be undone by the next administration but a statute would not be.

9.5 Two-track PBM reform: voluntary commercial pivot in 2025 + statutory Part D delinking in 2028.

Cigna's October 27, 2025 rebate-free announcement (§5.2) preceded the CAA 2026 statutory delinking (§1.9) by 99 days — and the statutory delinking does not bite commercial markets at all. The PBM disclosures, read in sequence, show the Big-3 PBMs front-running voluntary commercial reform to preempt harsher rules, while the 2026 CAA touches only Medicare Part D. Commercial rebate economics survive intact in statute; PBM 10-Ks (CVS §5.1, UNH §5.3) reflect this duality.

9.6 Biosimilars are the structural winner of the 2026 regime.

§232 carves out biosimilars. MFN scoping (HHS communications referenced in CRS LSB11319 §1.2) restricts MFN pricing to single-source brand products. So a biosimilar entering the US market in 2026 (e.g., Celltrion Omlyclo, §6.1) faces neither tariff drag nor MFN price ceiling competition from a "same molecule" perspective — while the reference brand (Roche Xolair) faces both. This produces an unusually large biosimilar economic premium relative to historical norms.

9.7 The opacity of the MFN agreements is itself a strategic signal.

Wyden's April 21, 2026 disclosure legislation (§8.4) exists because the 17 manufacturer MFN agreement texts are not public. The White House fact sheets describe pricing on specific listed products (Praluent, Xofluza, Ozempic, etc.) but not the breadth of the manufacturer book or escalation triggers. This information asymmetry — the agreements are confidential but the tariff exemptions they generate are public via Annex II of Proclamation 11020 — is itself a constraint on competitor benchmarking and on Congressional oversight.

9.8 Forward-looking signals — MFN cohort expansion through 2027 likely.

CMS Round 3 (§1.7) selecting 15 drugs for IPAY 2028 — first to include Part B drugs — combined with the H.R. 7837 mechanism (mandatory MFN pricing for non-signatories by December 2028, §8.1) signals a likely "second 17" cohort during 2026–2027: mid-cap specialty pharma not in the initial group (e.g., Vertex, Biogen, BioMarin, Alnylam, Moderna, Incyte). 10-K risk-factor language across that cohort during the February 2026 filing window will be a leading indicator.

Snapshot summary (200 words)

By May 19, 2026, the US pharmaceutical pricing regime rests on three interlocking instruments: Executive Order 14297 (May 12, 2025) establishing voluntary MFN pricing; Proclamation 11020 (April 2, 2026) imposing Section 232 tariffs of up to 100% with carve-outs for MFN signatories and country-specific caps (15% Switzerland/EU/Japan); and the US–Swiss–Liechtenstein Joint Statement (November 14, 2025) that locked in the 15% Swiss pharma cap before the proclamation was issued. Layered on top are CMS IRA Rounds 1–3 (Round 3 selected January 27, 2026, first to include Part B), CAA 2026 (signed February 3, 2026) Part D PBM delinking, and TrumpRx.gov direct-to-consumer launch (February 5, 2026). All 17 named manufacturers — including Roche/Genentech (signed December 19, 2025) — have signed; Regeneron was last on April 23, 2026. Industry IRA litigation has failed at the Third Circuit (Novartis, BMS/J&J — September 2025) and Fifth Circuit (PhRMA — September 2024, remanded). The dominant strategic insight is that the MFN regime is operationally a Section 232 tariff-credit program rather than a Medicare-pricing program: Annex II of Proclamation 11020 is the document that gives the MFN deals their economic teeth.
