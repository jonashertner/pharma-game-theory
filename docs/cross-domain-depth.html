Cross-domain strategy II — depth

Going deeper. Operational architecture for the three most generative moves from the original cross-domain page, the strategic-family clustering, three additional moves derived from the meta-pattern, and an honest accounting of where each analogy fractures.

Snapshot: 19 May 2026 · companion to cross-domain strategy I

What this page argues

The seven moves on the original cross-domain page were generated by single-domain analogy. This page goes one level deeper.

It does four things.

First, it operationalises the three most generative moves — Vickrey mechanism, Pharma Hansa, Antarctic-Treaty freeze — into rigorous design documents that could be handed to counsel, a mechanism designer, or a diplomatic team without further translation.

Second, it surfaces a meta-pattern that explains why these specific source domains were generative. The lesson then tells us where to look next.

Third, it adds three more moves derived from the meta-pattern: a Federalist Papers public-reasoning campaign, a Coal and Steel sectoral-integration play, and a lex mercatoria private-legal-order construction.

Fourth, it identifies the strategic-family clustering of the resulting ten moves, and the honest fracture lines where each analogy breaks.

I. The meta-pattern

The seven original moves come from source domains that look unrelated: Chinese imperial political theory, medieval mercantile law, microeconomic mechanism design, international sovereignty regimes, Polynesian exchange anthropology, forest ecology, Japanese aesthetics. Surface diversity is total.

Beneath the diversity there is a single shared structural problem each domain has solved.

They all answer the question: how does cooperation persist despite competition?

  • Mandate of Heaven — legitimacy-based cooperation between ruler and subjects whose interests are not fully aligned
  • Hanseatic League — institutional cooperation across cities that compete for trade
  • Vickrey mechanism — information-revealing cooperation in competitive auctions
  • Antarctic Treaty — cooperation amid frozen sovereignty conflict
  • Maori utu — ritualised cooperation that prevents adversarial escalation
  • Mycorrhizal network — invisible cooperation between trees competing for sunlight
  • Wabi-sabi — aesthetic cooperation that accumulates goodwill in trade and social relations

The mid-2026 pharma-pricing negotiation is, at the meta-level, the same problem. Roche, the US Executive, Novartis, Bern, the EU Commission, PBMs, patient advocacy, biosimilars, and the rest are not fully cooperative and not fully adversarial. They are coopetitors — actors with both shared and conflicting interests, whose strategy must accommodate both poles simultaneously.

This is why the analogies are generative. They are not random distant fields; they are fellow solvers of the same meta-problem. The novelty engine's discipline should be to pull from any domain that has solved a variant of competition-cooperation duality.

Other domains that solve the meta-problem — and that the cross-domain page has not yet mined:

  • The Federalist Papers (build a coalition for cooperative institutions while preserving state rivalry)
  • The European Coal and Steel Community (sectoral integration before political integration)
  • Lex mercatoria (transnational private legal order operating beneath sovereign rivalry)
  • The Schengen Area (open internal borders, preserved external ones)
  • The Concert of Europe (great-power coordination preserving state rivalry)
  • Mont Pelerin Society (long-duration intellectual movement that reshapes policy through slow conversion)
  • Improvisational comedy's "yes, and" discipline (collaborative narrative-building between adversarial-by-default performers)
  • The open-source software governance of the IETF (rough-consensus standards-making across competing implementations)
  • Co-ed mixed-doubles sports (lacrosse, mixed-doubles tennis, equestrian)
  • Polyandrous marriage systems (multiple-husband cooperation despite reproductive competition)
  • Glass-Steagall (structural separation that prevented coordinated banking failure)

Three moves derived from this richer pool are developed in Part III below.

II. Operational depth on the three most generative original moves

II.a. The Vickrey mechanism — actual design

The original move proposed a sealed-bid commitment mechanism. The operational design is multi-attribute multi-unit VCG (Vickrey-Clarke-Groves) over a structured outcome space.

The bidders. All MFN signatories (17 as of 23 April 2026) plus any new entrants. The mechanism is open; entry is by signed participation agreement.

The items. A vector space of (drug × channel × commitment-type) tuples. Drugs are identified by their MFN-relevant subset of each manufacturer's portfolio. Channels are Medicaid, Medicare Part B, Medicare Part D, TrumpRx.gov, and commercial. Commitment types are MFN discount depth, US-manufacturing milestones, patient-assistance-program scope, R&D commitment, and pipeline pre-launch pricing protocols.

The bids. Each bidder privately submits, for each (drug, channel, commitment-type) tuple in scope, a continuous value representing the maximum sustainable concession depth. Bids are sealed under attorney-client-privilege protection.

The allocation rule. Choose the cohort C* maximising V(C) = ∑_i v_i(allocation_i) — the administration-deliverable value — subject to no-walk constraints u_i(C) ≥ reservation_i for every signatory i, where u_i is bidder i's evaluated utility under cohort C. The reservation_i is the bidder's own bid for "the worst combination of commitments I would still sign at."

The payment rule. Each bidder pays the externality they impose on others — the standard VCG payment rule. Specifically, for bidder i, the payment is V(C \ i) − V(C) + v_i(allocation_i), where C* \ i is the optimal allocation excluding bidder i's bids. This is the maximum-revenue truth-revealing rule.

Incentive compatibility. VCG is dominant-strategy IC for single-parameter domains (Vickrey 1961, Clarke 1971, Groves 1973). For multi-attribute settings, IC requires the cycle-monotonicity condition (Saks-Yu 2005; Rochet 1987). For pharma pricing, the relevant domain structure satisfies cycle-monotonicity as long as the commitment-type space is finite and the utility functions are quasilinear in concession depth — both conditions are met in this design.

Antitrust safety. The mechanism is structurally equivalent to FCC spectrum auctions: a neutral arbiter (RAND or NBER) administers; bids are sealed; each manufacturer's specific bids and allocations are not revealed to other manufacturers; the cohort outcome is aggregate. The FCC spectrum auctions have not been antitrust-challenged in 35 years; the design pattern is recognised by DOJ Antitrust as procompetitive. Counsel should produce a no-objection letter from DOJ Antitrust before the first auction.

Political feasibility. The administration receives a cohort architecture that maximises its own utility function — defined operationally as the administration-deliverable value (visible price cuts, capex commitments, patient-access guarantees). How the cohort is composed across manufacturers is opaque to the public and to other manufacturers. This is the politically optimal form of mechanism design: the administration gets credit for the outcome without exposing the trade-offs that produced it.

Implementation details.

  • Arbiter selection: RAND Corporation (operationally precedented in healthcare mechanism design) or NBER (academic credibility).
  • Bid submission: encrypted bid format submitted to the arbiter through secure portal. Each bidder retains a notarised copy.
  • Allocation computation: standard integer-linear-programming solver (e.g., CPLEX, Gurobi). Computation time is polynomial in the number of bidders × items.
  • Payment computation: VCG payment rule, computed by the arbiter, communicated privately to each bidder.
  • Public announcement: the administration announces the resulting cohort as the next phase of MFN architecture, without revealing individual manufacturers' bids.
  • Dispute resolution: standard arbitration under the participation agreement.

Timeline. Six-month design phase (Q3–Q4 2026); pilot auction Q1 2027 covering IRA Round 4 selection criteria; first full auction Q3 2027 covering the full MFN cohort architecture for 2028–2030.

Cost. Mechanism design + arbiter operations: approximately $5–10M total, shared among participating manufacturers via the participation agreement.

II.b. The Pharma Hansa — actual charter

The original move proposed a city-level alliance among pharma cluster municipalities. The operational architecture has five elements: member criteria, founding members, governance, legal personhood, and charter.

Member criteria. Modelled on Hanseatic League historical accession criteria. A city qualifies for membership if all five conditions are met:

  1. Headquarters criterion. Host of at least one major pharma firm headquartered in the city or its immediate metropolitan area (not a regional office).
  2. Academic criterion. Host of at least one major research university with significant pharma-relevant chairs (academic-industrial linkage).
  3. Operational criterion. Host of at least one major contract-manufacturing or biopharmaceutical-production facility (operational depth).
  4. Public-investment criterion. Municipal government with demonstrated financial commitment to cluster infrastructure (e.g., dedicated cluster-funding line in the municipal budget).
  5. Charter-acceptance criterion. Formal acceptance of the antitrust safety clause.

Founding members. Applying the five criteria identifies seven cities for the founding cohort, distributed across three continents:

  • Basel (Switzerland): Roche, Novartis HQ; Uni Basel and ETH Zurich; Lonza CDMO; cantonal government active in cluster.
  • Boston-Cambridge MA (United States): Pfizer, Sanofi US sites, Biogen, Vertex HQ; Harvard, MIT, BU; Mayor + State partnership via MassBio.
  • Cambridge UK: AstraZeneca HQ; University of Cambridge; CDMO depth; AstraZeneca's $1B+ Cambridge facility.
  • Heidelberg (Germany): Sanofi Aventis Deutschland operations; Heidelberg University; substantial biotech cluster (BioRN).
  • Stockholm/Uppsala (Sweden): AstraZeneca historical, GE Healthcare Life Sciences; Karolinska Institutet, Uppsala University; substantial CDMO.
  • Singapore: Roche, GSK, MSD sites; NUS, NTU; major CDMO hub (Lonza Singapore); EDB-administered cluster funding.
  • Hyderabad (India): Indian generics manufacturing dominance; major teaching hospitals; multiple CDMOs.

Governance. Modelled on the Hanseatic Diet (Hansatag). Three-element structure:

  • Annual Diet (Hansatag). Hosted in rotation among member cities. Decisions on major matters require consensus across the three quartiers.
  • Three-quartier system. European (Basel, Cambridge UK, Heidelberg, Stockholm), North American (Boston-Cambridge MA), Asian (Singapore, Hyderabad). Each quartier elects two representatives to the Standing Committee — six members total.
  • Standing Committee. Meets quarterly. Provides continuity between Diets. Empowered to make operational decisions within the Diet's strategic framework.

Legal personhood. The secretariat is structured as a Belgian association sans but lucratif (ASBL) — a non-profit legal entity recognised under Belgian law and amenable to cross-border operations. All member cities are founding signatories. Member cities provide annual funding via municipal budget appropriations: an indicative budget of CHF 2M per year, distributed pro rata by city pharma-cluster-employment size.

The founding charter. Seven-article document.

  • Article I — Purpose. "The Pharma Hansa exists to advance cluster-resilience cooperation across pharma manufacturing and innovation cities, in the interests of preserving global pharmaceutical innovation infrastructure across the 21st century."
  • Article II — Membership. Cities meeting the five criteria above may apply for membership. Admission requires Diet supermajority (5 of 7 founding members).
  • Article III — Governance. Diet, quartier system, Standing Committee, secretariat as described above.
  • Article IV — Scope. Talent retention and academic-industrial linkage; supply-chain resilience and operational coordination; regulatory-mechanics input to national policy decisions; research-infrastructure cooperation.
  • Article V — Antitrust safety clause. "No part of this Charter or any operational decision under it shall be construed as permitting member cities or their hosted firms to coordinate on pharmaceutical pricing, market allocation, customer allocation, or any other dimension restricted under applicable competition law. Compliance with EU, US, Swiss, and Indian competition law is paramount."
  • Article VI — Withdrawal. Two-year notice, no penalty. Withdrawing city remains observer for two years after withdrawal.
  • Article VII — Amendments. Require Diet supermajority and quartier consensus.

Timeline. Initial bilateral discussions Q3–Q4 2026 (Basel cantonal government convenes counterparts). Founding Diet November 2026 in Basel (AGM-adjacent). First Standing Committee meeting Q1 2027. First Pharma Hansa policy position published Q3 2027.

Cost. Secretariat establishment + first-year operations: approximately CHF 5M, split among the seven founding cities. Annual ongoing: CHF 2M.

II.c. The Antarctic-Treaty pharma-pricing freeze — actual treaty

The original move proposed a multilateral pact freezing pharma-pricing-regime expansion for 15 years. The operational architecture is a seven-article treaty drafted in conscious parallel with the Antarctic Treaty (1959).

Parties. United States, European Union (as a collective party under Article 218 TFEU), Switzerland, United Kingdom, Japan, Canada, Australia. Optional Phase-2 parties: Republic of Korea, Israel, Singapore.

Preamble. "The Parties, recognising that overlapping claims of jurisdiction over pharmaceutical pricing — the US Most-Favored-Nation Executive Order, the Inflation Reduction Act, Section 232 pharmaceutical tariffs, the European Union Pharma Package and Critical Medicines Act, the United Kingdom NICE technology-appraisal system, the Swiss LAMal pricing-list system, the Japanese MHLW reimbursement system, and similar instruments in other Parties — create instability detrimental to all Parties' interests in continued pharmaceutical innovation and accessible patient care; affirming the Parties' desire to avoid unilateral expansion of these regimes while preserving each Party's legal claims to such authority within its own jurisdiction; have agreed as follows."

Article I — Freeze of regulatory expansion. "No Party shall expand the scope of its existing pharmaceutical-pricing-regime instruments beyond their 19 May 2026 baseline scope without multilateral consent of all Parties under Article IV."

Article II — Scientific cooperation. "The Parties establish a Joint Scientific Committee for the sharing of pharmaco-economic data, post-marketing-surveillance outcomes, comparative-effectiveness research, and rare-disease horizon-scanning. The Committee shall meet quarterly and produce annual reports."

Article III — Sovereignty preservation. Mirroring Antarctic Article IV: "No acts or activities taking place while the present Treaty is in force shall constitute a basis for asserting, supporting, or denying a claim to sovereign authority over pharmaceutical pricing in any Party's jurisdiction. No new claim, or enlargement of an existing claim, to such authority shall be asserted while the present Treaty is in force."

Article IV — Consultative Meetings. "The Parties shall convene annually, in rotation among the Parties' capitals, to consider matters of common interest pertaining to pharmaceutical pricing regimes. A quarterly review committee shall consider proposed actions by any Party that may affect the freeze established in Article I."

Article V — Compliance and dispute resolution. "Disputes between or among Parties concerning the interpretation or application of the present Treaty shall be referred for resolution to a dispute panel constituted under the procedures of the World Trade Organization Dispute Settlement Understanding. Panel reports shall be binding upon the Parties pending appellate review."

Article VI — Term and withdrawal. "The present Treaty shall remain in force for fifteen years from entry into force, and shall be automatically renewed for successive five-year periods unless a Party gives notice of withdrawal not less than two years prior to the end of a term. During the two-year notice period, the withdrawing Party shall participate in mandatory consultation with the remaining Parties."

Article VII — Entry into force and ratification. "The present Treaty shall enter into force when ratified by all original signatories under their respective constitutional procedures."

Ratification politics.

  • United States. Hardest. The Trump administration may prefer executive-agreement form to avoid Senate ratification (Senate two-thirds is unlikely under current composition). Alternative: executive agreement under Trade Promotion Authority delegation. TPA expires 1 July 2027 and would need reauthorisation; depending on the political composition of Congress, this is plausible but not certain. Backup: a congressional-executive agreement under the existing Trade Act of 1974 framework, requiring only simple majority in both chambers.
  • European Union. Requires Council unanimity for treaties under shared competence (pharma pricing crosses health and trade competence boundaries). COREPER consultation, EP consent. Member-state sensitivities: Germany (pharma-pricing reform politically salient), France (DG SANTE alignment), Netherlands (industrial-policy interests), Italy (price-control history). Estimated negotiation: 18–24 months.
  • United Kingdom. Post-Brexit, treaty ratification requires Parliament majority (Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010). Politically tractable; current government has been receptive to pharma-industrial-strategy cooperation.
  • Switzerland. Federal Council adopts; Federal Assembly approves; subject to optional referendum if 50,000 signatures gathered within 100 days. Referendum risk is real but historically Switzerland has not blocked trade treaties.
  • Japan. Diet approval required. Tractable but slow due to MHLW's deliberative culture.
  • Canada / Australia. Both have Westminster-style ratification; relatively quick.

Comparable precedents.

  • Antarctic Treaty (1959): negotiated in approximately 2 years; ratified by 12 states within 2 years of signature; renewed continuously since. Strong precedent.
  • Outer Space Treaty (1967): negotiated in 3 years; 110+ states have ratified.
  • Biological Weapons Convention (1972): freeze approach applied to bio-weapons development; weak verification but durable since.
  • Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968): similar freeze structure but with more rigorous verification. The pharma freeze is structurally closer to BWC than NPT.

Timeline. Initial sounding of US and EU positions: Q4 2026 (Swiss Federal Council leads). Formal negotiation: Q1 2027 – Q4 2028. Signature target: November 2028. Ratification: 2029 (all parties). Entry into force: 1 January 2030, immediately after the US Section 232 sunset.

Cost. Negotiation and secretariat establishment: approximately CHF 50–100M across all parties over the 2026–2030 period. Ongoing secretariat: CHF 10–15M per year.

III. Three additional moves from the meta-pattern

III.a. The Federalist Papers move

Source. Hamilton, Madison, and Jay's serial public-reasoning campaign in 1787–88, published anonymously under the byline "Publius" in New York newspapers. Eighty-five essays. They argued the case for ratifying the proposed US Constitution to state ratifying conventions. The essays did not invent the Constitution; they built the political coalition that ratified it.

Structural insight. The firewall annex (Move 1 of the board recommendation) faces the same problem the Federalists faced: a structurally defensible design with no extant political coalition. The case has to be made publicly, at length, in serial form, by credible authors, before the coalition can form.

The move. Roche commissions a twelve-essay public-reasoning campaign published over six months. Authored by two or three independent academics: a Harvard political scientist familiar with industrial-policy literature, an MIT mechanism-design economist familiar with auction theory and platform regulation, a Zurich-based international-law scholar familiar with cross-jurisdictional trade architecture. The essays are published in The Atlantic, the Financial Times, and Neue Zürcher Zeitung — three publications with distinct serious-political readerships across the US, UK/EU, and Switzerland.

Concrete instrument. A twelve-essay series titled (for example) "The Pharma Trilemma: Innovation, Access, Sovereignty." Each essay addresses one structural feature of the negotiation. Funded openly by Roche through a one-time grant to a host institution (e.g., Brookings, Bruegel, or Avenir Suisse), with explicit editorial-independence clauses ensuring authors retain final say on content. Total cost: $200–500K including author honoraria, publication coordination, and accompanying standalone website.

Counterparty. The publications themselves; the academic authors and their host institutions; opinion-shaping media outlets that will react to the series.

Timeline. Q4 2026 commissioning; Q1 2027 first essay; final essay Q3 2027. The campaign deliberately predates the firewall-annex negotiation window so the political space is established before specific instruments are tabled.

Verification. Does the campaign reshape policymaker speech and media framing? Three measurable signals: citations by named US/EU/CH policymakers in speeches and statements; opinion-page coverage in publications outside the original three (indicates spread); references in subsequent academic or think-tank papers.

Risk mitigation. The Roche funding could be detected as a credibility-killer. Mitigation: declare the funding source openly at launch — the Federalists' anonymity was effective in 1787 but is not in 2026, when concealment would invite worse scandal than full disclosure. Editorial-independence clauses are public.

III.b. The Coal and Steel Community move

Source. Jean Monnet's 1951 European Coal and Steel Community. France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg pooled sovereignty over a narrow functional area (coal and steel production) — not over their full economies. The bet: cooperation in one sector would create institutional infrastructure (High Authority, Common Assembly, Court of Justice) that would later spill over into wider integration. ECSC → EEC (1957) → EC (1967) → EU (1993). The bet paid off across 40 years.

Structural insight. Comprehensive pharma-pricing-regime reform is politically impossible — too many veto players, too many entrenched interests, too many sovereignty objections. Sectoral reform in one narrow functional area is plausible, particularly if the chosen area is uncontroversially sympathetic. The narrow win is valuable on its own; the spillover bet is upside.

The move. Pick one narrow functional area where US–EU–CH cooperation is mutually beneficial and politically defensible. Three candidates, in order of tractability:

  1. Pediatric oncology orphan drugs. Saving children's lives is broadly popular; the drugs are few (~50–100) and the patient populations small; commercial competition is limited.
  2. Pandemic-preparedness antivirals and vaccines. COVID memory still operative; clear public-health rationale; existing precedents (CEPI, Vaccine Strategy, BARDA-EU partnerships).
  3. Rare-disease ATMPs. Limited commercial competition; high patient need; novel manufacturing modalities mean existing pricing regimes are poorly suited.

Pediatric oncology is the highest-tractability starting point.

Concrete instrument. A trilateral US–EU–CH agreement covering pediatric oncology orphan drugs. Includes: joint horizon-scanning for drugs in late-stage development, coordinated benefit assessment using a shared methodology, mutual price-floor commitment (no party drives prices below a coordinated floor that maintains manufacturer R&D incentives), joint patient-access protocols (cross-border treatment where domestic capacity is insufficient), shared post-marketing safety surveillance.

A small Brussels-based secretariat administers the agreement. The "High Authority" equivalent has authority only over administrative implementation, not over the substantive policy. The substantive policy remains with each party.

Counterparty. USTR + HHS + CMS at the US end; EU Commission DG SANTE; Swiss FOPH. Initial sounding through Swiss Federal Council (which has the convening credibility).

Timeline. Initial bilateral soundings Q1 2027; trilateral negotiation Q3 2027 – Q4 2028; signature target Q1 2029. First operational cycle 2029–2030.

Verification. Does the agreement produce observable cooperation outcomes? Three measurable signals: joint horizon-scanning reports published quarterly; coordinated pricing on at least 10 pediatric oncology drugs by end of 2030; patient-cross-border-treatment cases handled under the agreement reach 100+ per year by end of 2030. If all three yes, watch for spillover to adjacent functional areas (adult orphan drugs, then expand). If yes-but-narrow, the win is still real.

Risk mitigation. Spillover may not occur and the agreement remains a narrow technical instrument. Mitigation: structure the institutional infrastructure to be modularly expandable — the secretariat is designed to absorb additional functional areas without re-architecting governance. The narrow agreement is durable on its own merits even without spillover.

III.c. The lex mercatoria move

Source. Medieval merchant law (12th–17th century) operating transnationally without state sanction. Merchants travelling between sovereign jurisdictions developed shared commercial norms, dispute-resolution mechanisms (commercial arbitration via merchant guilds), and contract conventions binding regardless of which state's courts they appeared in. Modern descendants: ICC International Court of Arbitration, UCP 600 (Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits), Incoterms, the New York Convention on Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958).

Structural insight. Pharma firms are transnational. The sovereignty conflict between national pricing regimes is a state-level problem, but the actual transactions occur between firms operating across borders. A transnational private legal order — pharma-specific commercial norms operating between firms regardless of state regulation — could provide a stable substrate beneath the sovereignty conflict. This is structurally what lex mercatoria did for medieval long-distance trade.

The move. Major pharma firms jointly develop a "Pharma Trade Code" — a structured body of commercial norms governing inter-firm transactions on cross-border drug supply, licensing, technology transfer, and pricing protocols. The Code includes its own arbitration mechanism (ICC pharma-specialised panel or a new dedicated arbitration body modelled on AAA Healthcare Payor-Provider Arbitration). The Code is binding between firms through contractual incorporation, not through state ratification.

Concrete instrument. A 50–100 page Pharma Trade Code, drafted by external counsel from major firms in consultation with the ICC International Court of Arbitration. Hosted by IFPMA (International Federation of Pharmaceutical Manufacturers and Associations) — an industry-neutral body. Major pharma firms commit to incorporating the Code by reference in all commercial agreements involving cross-border drug supply, licensing, or technology transfer.

The Code covers, in approximately twelve chapters: drug supply contracts (delivery, quality, force majeure), licensing agreements (royalty structures, territory, exclusivity), technology transfer (know-how, manufacturing process, training), pricing protocols between firms (transfer pricing, cross-licensing royalties), dispute resolution procedures, applicable law and forum selection, treatment of regulatory changes, intellectual-property allocation, confidentiality, anti-corruption compliance, sustainability provisions, and amendment procedures.

Counterparty. IFPMA secretariat; general counsels of major pharma firms (Roche, Novartis, Pfizer, Lilly, J&J, AstraZeneca, GSK, Merck, Sanofi, BMS, AbbVie, Amgen); the ICC International Court of Arbitration.

Timeline. Twelve-month drafting (Q1 2027 – Q4 2027). Adoption target Q1 2028. First arbitration case under the Code: 2029.

Verification. Are pharma-cross-border-contract disputes increasingly resolved under the Trade Code rather than national courts? Measured by case statistics from ICC, AAA, LCIA. Target: at least 30% of major pharma-firm cross-border commercial disputes referred under the Code by end of 2031.

Risk mitigation. National regulators may perceive the Trade Code as private-sector encroachment on regulatory authority. Mitigation: the Code explicitly disclaims any regulatory function — it governs commercial relationships between firms only, not pricing to consumers, payers, or governments. Compliance with national pricing regulation remains entirely separate.

IV. Strategic-family clustering

The seven original moves plus the three new moves cluster into four strategic families. The families reveal that the moves are not independent — combinations are more powerful than single moves.

Family 1 — Signal discipline

Mandate of Heaven + wabi-sabi. Both target the narrative environment. The Mandate-of-Heaven ceremonial pipeline provides high-frequency political theatre (quarterly milestone announcements); the wabi-sabi voluntary excess statements provide cumulative goodwill capital. Combined: a unified communications discipline where Roche's signals match the administration's needs and the broader audience's needs at multiple frequencies — short-term ceremony, medium-term goodwill, long-term trustworthiness. Either alone is half a strategy. The Federalist Papers move belongs in this family as a structural-narrative complement: it builds the intellectual coalition that the ceremonial pipeline and voluntary excess support.

Family 2 — Architectural reshape

Pharma Hansa + Antarctic-Treaty freeze. Both target the structural map of the negotiation. The Hansa builds a trans-sovereign cluster constituency at the city level; the Treaty freezes regulatory expansion at the state level. Combined: cities lobby their own states for Treaty ratification, providing political infrastructure that pure diplomacy cannot. The Coal and Steel move belongs in this family as a sectoral-bridge complement: it builds the functional substrate that gives the Hansa and the Treaty operational meaning.

Family 3 — Information design

Vickrey mechanism + Maori utu. Both target the information architecture of the negotiation. Vickrey reveals manufacturer-private-preference truthfully through dominant-strategy IC; utu closes each disclosed item before escalation. Combined: a preference-revealing structured-closure regime where information emerges into the open and is metabolised before it becomes a basis for further conflict. The lex mercatoria move belongs in this family as a transnational-substrate complement: it builds the contractual infrastructure within which Vickrey-revealed preferences and utu-closed items can be enforced.

Family 4 — Industry coordination

Mycorrhizal compact + Hansa. Both target industry-level cooperation. The Mycorrhizal Compact is the operational layer (supply chain, regulatory expertise, platform-tech cross-licensing); the Hansa is the political-diplomatic layer. Same actors at two altitudes. Combined: cluster-resilience at city level + operational resilience at firm level. The lex mercatoria move belongs in this family too as a contractual layer between firms.

Observed pattern

The Hansa, the Federalist Papers move, the Coal and Steel move, and the lex mercatoria move each appear in multiple families. They are multi-purpose moves — they build foundations that several other moves depend on.

This is a strategic implication for sequencing: do the multi-purpose moves first. The Hansa is the highest-value first move because it is foundational for three of the four families.

V. Honest fracture lines

Each move's analogy has structural limits. The board should know where each analogy breaks before relying on it.

Mandate of Heaven

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes the administration is rational about its own legitimacy interests. If the administration is not rational — if it sees Roche's ceremonial cooperation as weakness to be extracted — the move fails. Watch for: administration responses that demand more ceremony as preconditions, indicating the cooperation is being weaponised rather than reciprocated.

Pharma Hansa

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes cities have interests sufficiently aligned to cooperate, despite being in different sovereign states. If a member city's state government overrides cluster-city interests (e.g., the German federal government overriding Heidelberg, or US federal politics constraining Boston), the Hansa loses operational autonomy. Watch for: state-level political pressure on member cities; municipal officials being constrained from public statements supporting Hansa positions.

Vickrey mechanism

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes a politically neutral arbiter and the absence of pre-bid collusion. If the administration intervenes in the arbiter's selection or operation, the mechanism is corrupted. If manufacturers collude pre-bid (which the VCG payment rule discourages but does not prevent in repeated games), bids will be suppressed below true values. Watch for: signs of administrative pressure on the arbiter; statistical patterns in bid distributions that suggest coordination.

Antarctic-Treaty freeze

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes Parties' interest in stability exceeds their interest in unilateral advantage. If a Party (most likely the US) sees an opportunity to extract major unilateral gain by withdrawing from the Treaty, the freeze unravels. Watch for: political signals in the US that successor administrations may not respect the freeze; preparation for unilateral pharma-pricing-regime expansion.

Maori utu

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes both parties accept the closure logic. If the administration rejects the closure framing and treats each "closed" item as merely the previous round of an open-ended negotiation, utu is reduced to standard concession-extraction. Watch for: administration language describing closed items as "first phase" or "initial commitment" — markers of non-closure intent.

Mycorrhizal compact

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes antitrust authorities accept the operational-only framing. If DOJ Antitrust or DG COMP perceives the compact as price-coordination in disguise, the compact is challenged. Watch for: antitrust counsel raising concerns; competing firms that decline to participate citing antitrust risk.

Wabi-sabi voluntary excess

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes voluntary excess generates goodwill capital that can be drawn upon in subsequent negotiations. If the administration or other actors treat the excess as new baseline expectations (rather than as discretionary excess), the move generates no capital and produces only ongoing cost. Watch for: administration statements that treat the voluntary excess as required, or as the starting point for further demands.

Federalist Papers move

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes that public-reasoning campaigns shape policy outcomes. In an era of media fragmentation, the campaign may reach only the existing pro-cooperation audience without converting sceptics. Watch for: campaign citations clustering within existing aligned outlets only; absence of cross-aisle engagement.

Coal and Steel move

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes functional cooperation spills over into adjacent areas. The original ECSC spillover took 40 years and required specific political conditions (post-war reconciliation, US Marshall Plan funding, Soviet threat). The pediatric-oncology-orphan-drug starting point may produce a narrow win without spillover, leaving the broader sovereignty conflict untouched. Watch for: cooperation outcomes that are operationally durable but do not generate political momentum for further integration.

Lex mercatoria move

Where it breaks. The analogy assumes that private legal orders can operate beneath sovereign authority without triggering it. Modern states are more attentive to private legal orders than medieval states were; a Pharma Trade Code that becomes effective could attract regulatory attention (e.g., DOJ Antitrust, EU DG COMP, German Bundeskartellamt). Watch for: regulatory comment on the Trade Code's scope; targeted enforcement actions against firms relying on Code-based dispute resolution.

VII. What this page deliberately does not claim

These moves are generated by cross-domain analogy, not validated by precedent in this domain. The analogical reasoning is honest; the source domains are correctly described; the structural similarities are real; the translations are intellectually defensible. But the moves have not been tested in practice in pharma pricing. Each carries the risk of any first attempt: the analogy may hold only loosely, the political environment may be misread, the counterparty may not respond as expected.

The fracture-line analysis in Part V is the honest accounting of where each analogy could break. The board should treat the moves as bets — not as predictions — and size each bet to its failure cost.

The cross-domain page generates option value. The original cross-domain page (Part I) introduced the option set; this depth companion (Part II) operationalises three options into actually-buildable instruments and adds three options the meta-pattern surfaces. The board's task is to convert the option value into a portfolio: pick the moves whose failure cost is bounded, whose success value is asymmetric, and whose mechanisms reinforce each other.

The portfolio I would recommend, given the strategic-family clustering and sequencing logic, is:

  • Always-on (low-cost, multi-family value): Pharma Hansa, Federalist Papers campaign.
  • Discrete bets with clear failure modes: Vickrey mechanism pilot, Coal and Steel sectoral negotiation.
  • Long-cycle architectural bets: Antarctic-Treaty freeze.
  • Continuous behavioural disciplines: Mandate of Heaven ceremony, wabi-sabi voluntary excess, Maori utu register.
  • Operational risk-mitigation: Mycorrhizal compact (with rigorous antitrust safeguards), lex mercatoria Trade Code.

This is a coherent portfolio. It is not all of the moves; it is the moves whose interactions create reinforcing structure.
