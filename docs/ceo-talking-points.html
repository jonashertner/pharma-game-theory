Overview

CEO talking points by audience

To: CEO Thomas Schinecker, Corporate Communications, IR · From: Strategic-analysis working group · Date: 20 May 2026 · Read time: 8 minutes

What this is

Four audience-specific message architectures, each with: opening line (the apex), three substantive points, what to avoid, and one quotable line worth memorising. Designed for CEO use across the 2026 communication cycle (Q2 earnings call, employee town hall, regulatory engagements, family-pool consultation).

The architecture is deliberately non-uniform. The same strategic posture should be communicated differently to each audience because each audience has different decision rights, different vocabularies, and different evidentiary standards.

Audience 1 — Investors (Q3 2026 earnings call, October 2026)

Opening line

“We secured the December 2025 deal at industry-leading terms, and the durability work we are doing now is what makes the deal pay off over 2027 to 2031.”

The three substantive points

  1. What we won. The Roche outcome in the December 2025 MFN wave was stronger than peers (cite Xofluza-only cession, 0% Section 232 rate, growth-driver portfolio retained, only Xolair on IRA Round 3). Pharma Division +9% CER in 2025, US +8% CER, top-5 growth drivers at CHF 21.4B combined. The signing was the consolidation of a position negotiated through six months of Swiss-state engagement and the $50B US capex pre-commitment.

  2. What we are doing in 2026. Converting the framework into durable institutional architecture through bilateral protection mechanisms. This is the natural next step from December 2025; it is not a new initiative or a reaction to deterioration. Reference: the November 2025 framework already contemplates supplemental annexes.

  3. What we are positioning for in the 2030+ decade. Trontinemab Phase 3 readouts target 2028+; Vabysmo high-dose franchise; Itovebi PI3K oncology launch ramp. The $50B US capex is sized for franchise growth, not just tariff defense.

What to avoid

  • Specific CHF impact ranges. Analyst noise; sets anchor points for negative-surprise comparisons. Refer questions to the [investor day deck] for ranges, do not volunteer them on calls.
  • The phrase “firewall annex.” Internal working-group language. External vocabulary: “bilateral protection mechanisms”, “structural supplement to the November 2025 framework.”
  • Probability assignments from the decision-quality framework. Sets up callbacks if probabilities update. Refer to scenarios qualitatively (“central case”, “adverse case”) not numerically.
  • Black-swan framing. Right for the board; wrong for earnings calls where it sounds defensive. Tail risks should be referenced in 10-K disclosures (standard practice) not in proactive investor communication.

One quotable line

“December 2025 was the negotiation. 2026 is the architecture. The drugs that drove +9% growth in 2025 are the same drugs we are positioning for the 2030 decade.”

Audience 2 — Employees (all-hands town hall, Q3 2026)

Opening line

“The pricing pressure on our industry is real and it is not going away. What we did in December was buy ourselves the time and the space to do the science we want to do.”

The three substantive points

  1. Why the December deal matters to research and operations. The 0% Section 232 rate through 2029 protects Swiss-origin manufacturing and the Basel/Kaiseraugst/Penzberg jobs that depend on it. The growth-driver portfolio retention means Vabysmo, Ocrevus, Hemlibra, and Phesgo — the work many of you are directly contributing to — is positioned for continued growth. No restructuring is contemplated as a result of the deal.

  2. What is changing operationally. $50B in US capex is real and creates real opportunity in Indianapolis, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, California, and at the fifth site. We expect to hire approximately 12,000 people over the five-year build. Genentech in South San Francisco remains the discovery engine; the US build is manufacturing and translational, not basic science.

  3. What is not changing. The science strategy is unchanged: brain-shuttle Alzheimer's remains a top priority; oncology and immunology pipelines continue; the diagnostics franchise is integral to the strategy, not subordinate. The dual-share structure that anchors the long-horizon strategy remains. The board re-elected Severin Schwan with 97.75% support in March 2026 — that is what continuity looks like.

What to avoid

  • CHF-impact specifics. Most employees do not benchmark on revenue; framing the conversation in revenue terms inadvertently makes the conversation about layoffs and cost discipline.
  • Trump-administration politics. Employees are politically diverse; the message should be policy-neutral. Refer to “the US administration” or “the December 2025 framework” without political coloration.
  • “Industry-leading terms.” Peer comparison framing is investor-appropriate; with employees, it can sound like Roche is celebrating while peers struggle. Avoid.

One quotable line

“The December deal was not the end of something. It was the beginning of how we operate for the next ten years.”

Audience 3 — Regulators (USTR, SECO, FDA, EMA bilateral engagements)

Opening line

“We are committed to making the November 2025 framework structurally durable. That requires technical work on confidentiality, on data, and on reference-pricing mechanics — and we are prepared to do that work.”

The three substantive points

  1. For USTR specifically. The US–CH framework can be supplemented in ways that strengthen US-side interests without compromising US-side MFN authority. Specifically: confidential ex-US net-price aggregation, anonymised access-by-indication data, low-income-country reference-price floor commitments. Roche is prepared to develop technical proposals through Q3 2026.

  2. For SECO specifically. The Swiss-side interest in pharma-pricing stability across BAG list cycles is structurally aligned with US-side interest in MFN-benchmark predictability. A bilateral annex that protects confidentiality on both sides would be net-positive for Swiss federal trade policy. Roche supports SECO leading the technical drafting.

  3. For FDA / EMA. Pricing discussions are separate from regulatory matters; Roche's commitment to safety, efficacy, and post-market surveillance is unchanged. The bilateral framework work does not affect FDA approval timelines, EMA scientific advice, or any other regulatory engagement.

What to avoid

  • Industry-coalition framing in front of USTR or SECO. Regulators dislike the impression of being lobbied collectively. Frame Roche's engagement as company-specific and technically-grounded, not as part of a PhRMA / EFPIA / IFPMA push.
  • The November 2025 framework as “the deal Roche secured.” The framework was a Swiss-state-led intervention; framing it as a Roche-secured outcome implicitly diminishes SECO. Frame as “the framework SECO secured, which Roche is supporting.”
  • Patent-term or pricing-mechanism technical detail at the political-engagement level. Save for the working-group level. Political engagement should be high-level posture only.

One quotable line

“Confidentiality is not opposition to transparency. It is the operational pre-condition for the rebate structures that make affordable access workable.”

Audience 4 — The generational-shareholder pool (governance consultation, ongoing)

Opening line

“The strategic posture we are recommending reflects the kind of long-horizon thinking that has guided this company across generations. We are giving up small things now to preserve large things later.”

The three substantive points

  1. Continuity of strategic posture. Every major commitment the board has made since 2023 (the $50B US capex, the December 2025 MFN signing, the proposed firewall annex) is consistent with the long-horizon orientation that has anchored the company. The current strategic moment is unusual in its political compression, but the response is in continuity with prior practice.

  2. The Hatch-Waxman analog. The pattern of trading specific revenue concessions for durable institutional architecture is exactly the pattern that produced the 1984 Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act, which has shaped the industry for forty years. The firewall annex is calibrated to the same logic. See Hatch-Waxman analog for the deeper case.

  3. What changes if the long-horizon assumption is wrong. Black swan #1 is named explicitly in the analysis: the multi-generational time horizon is the structural asymmetry that justifies the variance-tolerant strategic posture. If that assumption changes — for any reason — the strategic posture must be re-engineered. The working group has been instructed to pre-write the post-horizon strategic memo as a tabletop exercise; this is not an anticipated event, but the discipline of preparing for it is itself the right operational posture.

What to avoid

  • Treating the consultation as performative. Pool members hold real decision rights and contribute real strategic perspective. The consultation should be substantive and bidirectional, not a presentation.
  • Quarterly-cycle financial framing. The audience operates on multi-generational frames; framing in quarterly or annual cycles is mis-calibrated.
  • Naming pool individuals in any public-facing document. The working group's reference is to the “generational-shareholder structure” in all external materials; this convention should be preserved.

One quotable line

“The asymmetry we have over every other principal at the table is the ability to think in decades instead of quarters. The strategic question is whether we use it deliberately or default to peer-firm time horizons.”

Cross-audience consistency checks

Three messages should be identical across all four audiences:

  • “December 2025 was strong.” Avoid suggesting the deal was suboptimal or extracted under duress; the framing is consistent good outcome for Roche.
  • “2026 is durability work, not defensive work.” Avoid defensive language across all four audiences; the posture is institutional architecture, not damage limitation.
  • “The science strategy is unchanged.” Trontinemab, oncology, immunology, diagnostics remain. The pricing work is structural; the science work continues at full pace.

Three messages should vary:

  • CHF impact specifics: Detailed for investors only (within bounds), absent for employees, suppressed for regulators (politically sensitive), framed in multi-decade terms for the pool.
  • Peer comparison: Defended for investors, avoided for employees, neutralised for regulators, deferred for the pool (Roche-specific, not industry-comparative).
  • Probability and uncertainty language: Qualitative for investors, narrative for employees, absent for regulators, explicit for the pool (who are the ultimate variance-tolerance underwriters).

What this implies for Q3 2026 communications planning

The Q3 2026 earnings call (~22 October 2026) is the central public communication moment. Recommend:

  • IR rehearses the investor-audience message architecture through August 2026.
  • Internal town hall happens before earnings call to ensure employees hear the message first (Sept 2026).
  • Regulator engagements should precede both (June–September 2026) to avoid the appearance of public-then-private messaging mismatch.
  • Pool consultation should happen mid-cycle (August 2026) so consensus is in place before public exposure.

If pre-conditions on Item 1 produce a Branch A → Branch B re-rating per the decision-quality framework, the Q3 message architecture adapts as follows: replace “bilateral protection mechanisms” with “framework supplement work”; otherwise the architecture is unchanged.
