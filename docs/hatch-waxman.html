The Hatch-Waxman analog — what 1984 teaches us about 2026

To: Roche Executive Committee · From: Strategic-analysis working group · Date: 20 May 2026 · Read time: 14 minutes

Why this analog matters

Throughout the analysis we recommend that Roche trade specific revenue concessions for durable institutional architecture. The single best historical example of this pattern working is the Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act of 1984 — Hatch-Waxman. The Act's mechanism is not just an analogy to invoke; it is a case study in how to structure a deal so that the institutional gains survive the political coalition that produced them. Forty years on, the 1984 architecture still governs every generic launch and every branded patent-term decision in the United States.

This memo unpacks the Hatch-Waxman bargain: what was traded, what was won, how it survived, and what specifically Roche should learn for the 2026 firewall annex.

The 1984 situation

Heading into 1984, the US prescription-drug industry faced a structural threat analogous to (though not identical to) what Roche faced in 2025. Generic drugs were essentially unregulated for off-patent products, but a strict FDA interpretation of the 1962 Kefauver-Harris amendments meant that approving a generic version of an innovator drug required full repetition of safety and efficacy trials. The result: post-patent-expiry, only ~35% of branded drugs faced generic competition, because the regulatory cost of generic entry exceeded the gross margin available. Senator Edward Kennedy (D-MA) had pushed multiple bills to compel generic competition; Senator Orrin Hatch (R-UT) had pushed counter-bills to extend patent terms to compensate for FDA review delays. Both bills had failed individually. The industry was politically exposed, and the consumer / payer lobby had momentum.

The phase transition came when Representative Henry Waxman (D-CA) and Senator Hatch found a structural trade: generic-friendly mechanisms in exchange for patent-term restoration. The bargain's genius was that it gave each constituency something it valued more than what it conceded. Generics got an Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) pathway that allowed bioequivalence-based approval rather than full clinical re-trial. Branded pharma got two things back: (a) patent term restoration of up to 5 years for time consumed in FDA review (the “regulatory term recapture”) and (b) the Bolar exemption, which allows generic developers to conduct bioequivalence work pre-patent-expiry without infringing — but only as it relates to FDA submission, not for actual generic launch before expiry. The Act passed Congress 362-0 in the House and unanimously in the Senate. President Reagan signed it on 24 September 1984.

The trade in summary form: branded pharma accepted accelerated generic entry post-patent in exchange for durable institutional architecture governing the patent-term lifecycle. The trade was asymmetric in time: branded firms gave specific revenue (faster generic entry on existing drugs); they received structural lifecycle protection for all future drugs.

What made the architecture durable

Forty years later, Hatch-Waxman's architecture is still the operating regime. It has survived eight presidential administrations from both parties, dramatic shifts in healthcare politics, multiple Supreme Court interpretations, the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) extension to biologics in 2010, and the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. Why?

First, the architecture was codified at the statutory level, not in regulatory rule-making or executive action. A statute requires Congressional repeal; regulatory rules can be unwound by a single administration. The firewall annex parallel: structuring as a treaty (Senate-ratified) or as a TPA-delegated congressional-executive agreement makes the protection survive a 2028 administration change. Structuring as a Joint Statement (the November 2025 US–Switzerland framework form) does not.

Second, both constituencies got something they could publicly defend. Generic manufacturers got 90%+ of off-patent volume within 18 months (vs ~30% pre-Act); they could not credibly attack the Act without forfeiting their own gains. Branded pharma got the patent-term restoration mechanism that has, over forty years, contributed an estimated $40–80B of cumulative incremental revenue. Each side had skin in the game to defend the architecture. The firewall annex parallel: a structure that protects ex-US confidentiality but also includes specific consumer-payer-side mechanisms (e.g., commitment to data transparency on access-by-indication, or to a defined Most-Favored-Nation cross-reference floor for low-income countries) is more durable than one that protects only Roche's ex-US net price.

Third, the architecture was technically robust to changes in market conditions. Generic launches surged in the 1990s; rather than triggering a backlash, the Act's patent-term-restoration provisions stabilised the branded business by ensuring high-value innovator drugs retained 11–13 years of effective exclusivity. The combination was self-reinforcing: branded firms launched more high-value drugs because exclusivity was durable; generics launched faster because the pathway was efficient; consumers benefited from both. The firewall annex parallel: protection language should be designed for the world that exists post-MFN, not for the pre-MFN world. Specifically, it should accommodate Most-Favored-Nation pricing as a US-side reality and protect ex-US confidentiality conditional on (not in conflict with) that reality.

Fourth, the bargain was sequenced. Generic-friendly mechanisms phased in over 12–18 months; patent-term restoration applied to all drugs, not just future-launched ones. Branded firms saw value before they paid the full concession cost. The firewall annex parallel: the annex should provide some near-term protection (e.g., committed confidentiality on existing reimbursement-channel data) before requiring full Roche-side reciprocation. A long-tail trade where Roche pays first and gets paid later is structurally unstable.

What did not survive, and why

Not everything in the 1984 Act survived intact, and the reasons are instructive.

The 180-day generic exclusivity provision — intended to incentivise first-generic filings by giving the first applicant six months of generic-only sales — was widely gamed through pay-for-delay arrangements between branded and first-generic firms. The Federal Trade Commission litigated these for decades; the Supreme Court eventually addressed them in FTC v. Actavis (2013). The mechanism was structurally vulnerable to a coordination problem the drafters did not anticipate. Lesson for the firewall annex: any provision that requires multilateral coordination among private parties (e.g., industry-wide confidentiality protocols) is structurally weaker than one that operates between sovereign parties. Bilateral US–CH protection is more durable than industry-coalition protection.

The Bolar exemption survived but became a litigation focal point. Branded firms repeatedly challenged generic developers' bioequivalence work as exceeding the exemption's scope; generics argued for broader interpretation. The exemption's text was narrow (“solely for uses reasonably related to” FDA submission), and judicial interpretation tightened over time. Lesson: drafting precision matters. Specific definitions of “confidential rebate”, “net price”, “reference jurisdiction”, and similar load-bearing terms are worth weeks of GC time to get right.

Patent-term restoration calculations became a source of litigation throughout the 1990s, with companies challenging FDA's calculation of regulatory delay versus FDA challenging companies' clock-stopping claims. Lesson: any quantitative formula in the annex (e.g., “net price below which confidentiality protection vests”) should be drafted with explicit calculation methodology and dispute-resolution procedure. The November 2025 framework has provisional language on tariff rate determination; this should be tightened.

The Roche-specific lessons

Translating the Hatch-Waxman pattern to the 2026 firewall annex, six concrete drafting choices:

  1. Structure at statutory or treaty level, not executive-agreement level. This is the single most consequential durability lever. A statute or treaty survives administration turnover; an executive agreement does not. The Trade Promotion Authority (TPA) framework that produced US–Korea (KORUS) provides one templated pathway; the more durable Article II treaty pathway (NAFTA → USMCA precedent) is another.

  2. Make the deal mutually defensible. Roche should propose specific consumer-payer-side commitments embedded in the annex (e.g., transparency on access-by-indication data, low-income-country reference floor, or a defined manufacturer responsibility to shortage-prevention). These commitments cost Roche little but give the US-side political defenders something to point to. A one-sided annex protecting only Roche-side confidentiality is easier to politically attack and easier to unwind.

  3. Anchor in MFN, do not oppose it. The firewall should accommodate Most-Favored-Nation pricing as a settled feature of the US landscape and protect ex-US confidentiality conditional on that reality. Drafting that positions confidentiality as anti-MFN invites political opposition; drafting that positions confidentiality as MFN-compatible (e.g., “confidential ex-US net prices remain confidential except where required for MFN benchmark calculation, in which case anonymised aggregate forms shall suffice”) is harder to attack.

  4. Sequence the trade so Roche pays second. The annex should grant some near-term confidentiality protection before requiring full Roche reciprocation (e.g., $50B capex milestone progression, R&D commitment, or specific drug-pricing commitments). The 1984 sequencing precedent was branded firms saw value first; the same architecture maximises annex durability.

  5. Define load-bearing terms with explicit calculation methodology. “Net price”, “reference jurisdiction”, “confidential rebate”, “MFN benchmark” should each have a dedicated definitional section with worked examples. The Hatch-Waxman litigation history is the cautionary tale.

  6. Bilateral, not multilateral. Industry-coalition agreements suffer the same coordination problems that bedevilled the 180-day exclusivity provision. The firewall annex should be a bilateral US–CH instrument that other Swiss-domiciled or US-trading-partner countries may join via parallel annex (creating a network of bilateral instruments) but not multilateralise into a single document requiring industry-wide coordination.

Where the analog breaks down

Two important disanalogies must be acknowledged.

The 1984 deal had two principal constituencies (branded vs generic pharma). The 2026 deal has many. The MFN coalition includes US Executive, US Congress, manufacturers (multiple), Swiss government, EU stakeholders, and patient-advocacy groups. A bilateral US–CH annex sidesteps some of this multilateralism but cannot ignore it — EU pharmaceutical strategy and patient-advocacy groups can attack the annex from outside its bilateral scope. Hatch-Waxman's relative bilateral simplicity is not directly available; mitigation strategies (defensive industry coalitions, EU-level engagement) are required in parallel.

The 1984 institutional environment was more pharma-favourable than the 2026 environment. Reagan's administration was deregulatory; the Supreme Court's pre-Loper Bright doctrine deferred to agency expertise; the 1980s healthcare-cost debate had not yet escalated. The 2026 environment includes a populist administration that signed MFN with the express intent of compressing pharma pricing; SCOTUS post-Loper Bright will more aggressively review agency interpretations of any annex; healthcare costs are politically central in a way they were not in 1984. Durability under 2026 conditions requires more careful drafting than 1984 required, not less.

The honest probability assessment

Hatch-Waxman's success was contingent on specific 1984 features. The 2026 firewall annex faces a harder environment. But the pattern — trade specific revenue for durable institutional architecture, structure at the highest legal level available, make the deal mutually defensible — remains the highest-leverage strategic posture available to Roche.

The base rate for “industry-favourable institutional architecture surviving 40 years” is, looking across post-1980 US pharma legislation, about 1 in 4. Hatch-Waxman, BPCIA (2010), and PDUFA (1992) survived. The Medicare Modernization Act non-interference provision (2003) was unwound by IRA (2022). The Bayh-Dole Act (1980) survived but with eroding enforcement on march-in rights. The 2003 Medicare Part D structure was substantially modified by IRA. For the firewall annex, plan for 20–30% probability of full 20+ year durability; 40–60% probability of meaningful 10-year durability; 20–30% probability of being unwound within 5 years. Those probabilities should themselves shape the annex's commercial value calibration.

What this implies for the 2026 board agenda

Five specific implications.

  1. Item 1 should explicitly target statutory or treaty-level legal form. The current briefing language is ambiguous on legal form. The Hatch-Waxman analog argues this is the single most consequential drafting choice. Recommend: working-group output by 30 June 2026 should include a recommended legal-form pathway, not leave the choice open.

  2. The annex should include a mutual-defensibility provision. Roche should propose specific consumer-payer-side commitments embedded in the annex. Working-group output by 15 July 2026 should include a draft of such provisions.

  3. The annex should be drafted with MFN compatibility, not MFN opposition. This is a posture choice that affects every load-bearing clause. Recommend the working group adopt this framing explicitly.

  4. Sequencing should be Roche-pays-second. Specific milestones in the $50B capex programme can be tied to annex protection-vesting events.

  5. Calibrate annex commercial value at 30%-meaningful-20yr-durability, 50%-meaningful-10yr-durability, 20%-unwound-within-5yr. The CHF 18–55B NPV range used throughout the analysis assumes ~10-year durability; if statutory/treaty-level form is achieved, the upper end shifts higher; if only executive-agreement form is achievable, the range compresses to CHF 10–30B.

The 1984 deal worked because of specific features that the 2026 deal can deliberately replicate. The replication is not automatic. The board's endorsement of Item 1 should specify these features, not leave them to working-group discretion.
