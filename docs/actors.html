Roche / Genentech

A signed-up but reluctant MFN counterparty using a $50B US capex pledge to buy three tariff-free years while it manages oncology, ophthalmology and Alzheimer's launches into the IRA era.

Role

Roche Holding AG is the Basel-based, dual-share Swiss pharma-and-diagnostics group whose US subsidiary Genentech is the legal counterparty for federal pricing arrangements. By revenue Roche is a top-3 global pharma (group sales CHF 60.5B in 2025; Pharmaceuticals Division CHF 47.7B, +9% CER) with the broadest oncology franchise of any incumbent and a unique therapeutics-plus-diagnostics structure (Roche FY25 investor update, 29 Jan 2026). At the table it shows up in two seats: Genentech as one of the nine manufacturers that signed MFN deals on 19 Dec 2025 (White House fact sheet, 19 Dec 2025), and Roche Holding as the Swiss-domiciled parent whose tariff exposure is governed by the 14 Nov 2025 US–Switzerland framework. CEO Thomas Schinecker (Austrian; ex-Diagnostics head, CEO since March 2023) speaks for the group; Dr. Severin Schwan chairs the board, re-elected with 97.75% support on 10 March 2026 (Roche AGM 2026 release, 10 Mar 2026).

Constituency

  • Board of Directors, chaired by Severin Schwan (Roche CEO 2008–2023, now Chair) (Roche AGM 2026, 10 Mar 2026).
  • the founding family shareholder pool controlling a majority of family-held voting rights through a pooling agreement dating to 1948 — eighteen named individuals and entities including André the founding family (Vice-Chair), a family representative, family branches, and the charitable Foundation Wolf (Family Business Magazine, 2021; pool composition confirmed on Roche's own disclosures). The pool slipped below 50% in 2021 when a family member exited but remains the dominant voting block.
  • Non-voting bearer shareholders (Genussscheine, listed on SIX as ROG; ADRs as RHHBY on US OTC) — heavy ownership by global index funds and Swiss pension funds.
  • ~103,000 employees worldwide; the Genentech South San Francisco campus is the cultural anchor of the US business.
  • Swiss federal and cantonal stakeholders (Basel-Stadt in particular): Swiss federal trade negotiators secured the 14 Nov 2025 framework partly on the back of Roche, Novartis and Nestlé US-investment commitments (Al Jazeera, 14 Nov 2025).

Losing the constituency means: the family pool publicly siding against management at AGM (vote share below ~90% on a Schwan or Schinecker item would be read as a rupture), Basel cantonal pushback on job moves, or institutional revolt against margin compression.

Interests (private)

  • Defend ex-US net price — prevent MFN spillover via external-reference-pricing baskets in Germany (AMNOG), France (CEPS), UK (NICE/VPAG), Japan. Roche's discussions with the US explicitly invoke global cost-sharing: Schinecker said the dialogue was about "a different way and better sharing of cost for innovation… countries with less money should pay less, but also countries which have more money should pay more" (Fierce Pharma summary of Q4 2025 call, Jan 2026). Weight: 10/10.
  • Buy down Section 232 tariff exposure to 0% through Jan 2029 — Roche imports the bulk of its US biologics from Basel/Kaiseraugst and Penzberg; the MFN-plus-onshoring track sets the applicable rate to zero until 20 Jan 2029 versus 100% otherwise (WilmerHale, 13 May 2026; White House Section 232 EO, 2 Apr 2026). Weight: 10/10.
  • Protect Vabysmo, Ocrevus, Phesgo, Hemlibra, Xolair growth curves — these five were called out as the top 2025 growth drivers in the US (Roche FY25 investor update, 29 Jan 2026). Weight: 9/10.
  • Preserve the Genentech R&D ecosystem in South San Francisco as the company's primary discovery engine — inferred from repeated Schinecker references to keeping innovation in the US footprint (CNBC, 23 Apr 2026). Marked inferred. Weight: 8/10.
  • Maximize ROI on the Alzheimer's brain-shuttle franchise (trontinemab) — Phase 3 TRONTIER 1/2 enrolling at 800 patients each; preclinical Phase 3 added in 2025; readouts targeted for 2028+ (Pharmaphorum, Aug 2025). MFN pricing on the eventual launch is existential to that NPV. Weight: 9/10.
  • Avoid Xolair value destruction beyond what biosimilars already imply — Xolair (co-marketed with Novartis) is on the IPAY 2028 IRA list and faces a biosimilar that can market from ~Sept 2026 (Health Affairs, Jan 2026). Weight: 6/10.
  • Lock in confidentiality of net prices to avoid international reference contagion — inferred from industry-wide rebate practice and Schinecker's framing of pricing as a multilateral, not a US-disclosure, problem. Marked inferred. Weight: 9/10.

Positions (public)

  • MFN signature (Genentech) — committed to lower Xofluza in the TrumpRx direct-to-consumer channel from $168 to $50, plus state Medicaid MFN access, in exchange for a 3-year suspension of Section 232 pharma tariffs (White House fact sheet, 19 Dec 2025; Fierce Pharma, 19 Dec 2025).
  • $50B US capex over five years — new/expanded manufacturing in Indiana (CGM hub, ~$550M Indianapolis), Pennsylvania (gene therapy), Massachusetts (AI/CVRM R&D), California, plus a fifth site; ~12,000 jobs (Roche press release, 22 Apr 2025; CNBC, 22 Apr 2025; Fierce Biotech, 2025).
  • Constructive but conditional on global rebalancing — Schinecker on the Q4 2025 call: "We've been in discussions with the U.S. government for most part of this year" and the conversation is about international cost-sharing, not US unilateralism (Fierce Pharma, Jan 2026).
  • "Roche's U.S. drug prices will be lowered" — Schinecker's CNBC headline statement on 29 Jan 2026 acknowledged the directional concession publicly (CNBC video, 29 Jan 2026).
  • Doubling down on US — at Q1 2026 results (23 Apr 2026), Schinecker reaffirmed the US investment plan while complaining about Swiss franc strength suppressing reported CHF sales (CNBC, 23 Apr 2026). Group Q1 2026 sales were CHF 14.7B, +6% CER, –5% CHF (Globe Newswire ad hoc, 23 Apr 2026).
  • Participating in IRA Round 3 for Xolair — Roche/Novartis signed agreements to participate by the 28 Feb 2026 deadline; CMS confirmed all 15 manufacturers in by 13 Mar 2026 (CMS press release, 27 Jan 2026).

BATNA

If Roche walked away from the MFN track: - It would face the 100% Section 232 tariff on patented imports from Switzerland from 31 Jul 2026 (large companies) — reduced by the US–Swiss framework to a 15% MFN ceiling for Swiss-origin product, but only with country-level cooperation, not the 0% MFN-deal rate (Crowell & Moring, 2026; Al Jazeera, 14 Nov 2025). - The realistic walk-away is therefore 15% landed-cost surcharge on cross-border biologics (Ocrevus, Vabysmo, Hemlibra, Tecentriq, Phesgo). With the US worth ~50% of Pharma sales (US growth +6% in 2025, top region; Roche FY25 investor update, 29 Jan 2026), 15% on the imported share is a multi-CHF-billion margin hit annually — a meaningful but survivable floor. - Roche could redirect supply through accelerated US capacity (the $50B plan), but the build-out is 3–5 years; in the interim, ex-US manufacturing is largely unavoidable. - "No deal" payoff floor: keep current ex-US net prices intact, eat 15% tariff drag, lose the 0% MFN-track preference, and risk being publicly pressured by name (as Eli Lilly and others were before signing). The MFN-signed counterfactual swap gives 0% tariff through 2029 + Xofluza concession + Medicaid MFN access. Roche took it.

Red lines

  • No legally binding US net-price disclosure that pierces ex-US confidential rebate agreements — would crash AMNOG, CEPS, NICE and JP NHI reference pricing simultaneously. Crossing this would be a multi-billion-CHF group EBIT hit; the board and family pool would not ratify. Inferred from industry-wide rebate confidentiality posture.
  • No MFN scope creep beyond single-source brand drugs into Roche's biosimilar-exposed franchise (Xolair) or into Part B oncology administered drugs (Tecentriq, Perjeta, Phesgo) under terms worse than the IRA process — IRA Part B inclusion in Round 3 is a precedent Roche will fight to contain via litigation participation if needed (Health Affairs, 2026). Crossing here would force Genentech to choose between US market access and FDA approval.
  • No compulsory licensing or march-in on Genentech IP — Bayh-Dole march-in would be treated as expropriation; the family-shareholder pool would publicly object. Inferred.
  • No US deal terms that automatically trigger MFN status in Swiss or EU procurement contracts — Switzerland's tariff framework is bilateral; importing US MFN concessions into Swiss reference price baskets would unwind the Genentech deal's economics. Inferred.

Leverage

  • Oncology + Dx companion-diagnostic stack — no other manufacturer can credibly threaten to slow-walk Foundation Medicine companion Dx for new US-launched targeted therapies. Bites against CMS coverage-with-evidence-development pathways.
  • Trontinemab brain-shuttle Alzheimer's pipeline — only credible competitor pipeline to lecanemab/donanemab with a materially better ARIA-E profile (1% at 3.6 mg/kg, 91% amyloid-clearance at six months; Pharmaphorum, 2025). Bites against Medicare/CMS, who need affordable AD therapy.
  • $50B onshoring pledge already credibly underway — Indianapolis CGM hub, Pennsylvania gene therapy plant, MA AI R&D center; gives Roche standing to demand quid-pro-quo treatment (Roche release, 22 Apr 2025).
  • Swiss diplomatic backing — the 14 Nov 2025 US–Switzerland framework was partly justified to Bern by Roche/Novartis investments; the Federal Council has political skin in protecting them (KOF/ETH, Nov 2025).
  • Vabysmo, Hemlibra, Ocrevus US patient volumes — withholding supply or slow-walking new indications bites against MedPAC and Part B payors.

Constraints

  • family-shareholder voting pool can effectively veto strategic shifts; share class structure under Swiss Code of Obligations means the family controls voting without controlling cash flow rights (Family Business Magazine, 2021).
  • Swiss listing rules (SIX Art. 53 LR) require ad hoc disclosure of price-sensitive information — any MFN deal that materially changes guidance must be disclosed promptly (Globe Newswire Roche Art. 53 release, 23 Apr 2026).
  • IFRS reporting plus SEC requirements for ADR holders (Form 20-F equivalent disclosures) limit the ability to keep deal economics off-balance-sheet.
  • EU competition law and German AMNOG mechanics — any list-price reduction in the US that gets posted (e.g., on TrumpRx) feeds straight into European reference pricing baskets, narrowing what Roche can concede on transparency without cross-jurisdictional damage.
  • IRA Part B negotiation participation is statutorily compelled absent excise tax of 95% of US sales — Roche has no realistic refusal posture for Xolair (CMS, 2026).

Timeline pressures

  • 31 Jul 2026 — Section 232 tariffs take effect for "large" pharma manufacturers; Roche's MFN-deal 0% rate applies from this date (Crowell & Moring, 2026).
  • Sept 2026 — Xolair biosimilar (Celltrion's Omlyclo / others) expected to begin marketing, eroding LOE economics in advance of any IRA-negotiated price (Health Affairs, 2026).
  • 1 Jan 2027 — IRA Round 2 negotiated prices take effect for the 15 Part D drugs selected in Jan 2025 (Xolair was not in Round 2; it is in Round 3).
  • 1 Jan 2028 — IRA Round 3 prices, including Xolair, take effect (CMS press release, 27 Jan 2026).
  • 20 Jan 2029 — MFN-deal 0% Section 232 tariff exemption sunsets; renegotiation pressure crests just before US presidential transition.
  • 2028+ — TRONTIER 1/2 trontinemab readouts; pricing/MFN posture set today will determine the Alzheimer's franchise NPV.
  • Vabysmo / Tecentriq LOE clocks — Tecentriq biosimilar entry expected in the US around 2028–2029; Vabysmo IP runs longer, but is a top-3 IRA Part B negotiation candidate for future rounds (inferred).

Notes on intra-actor coalitions

Roche-as-actor is a Swiss-parent / US-subsidiary coalition with three identifiable factions: - Genentech (South San Francisco) US commercial + government affairs — owns the MFN deal text and the TrumpRx execution. Most exposed to the US regulatory state. - Basel group HQ + Schinecker / Hippe (CFO) — owns the global pricing posture and the $50B capex narrative. Sees MFN through a global net-price-protection lens, not a US-access lens. - the family-shareholder pool + Chair Schwan — long-horizon owners. Their willingness to absorb US margin compression in exchange for innovation continuity is what makes the deal politically possible. Divergence risk would surface if MFN spills into Europe.

US Executive Branch (coalition)

Principal pricing-policy actor in the mid-2026 US pharma negotiations: a multi-agency coalition (White House / HHS / CMS / USTR / Commerce / FDA) wielding tariff, statutory and bully-pulpit leverage in parallel.

Role

The US Executive enters the May 2026 pricing negotiations not as a single negotiator but as a synchronized stack of authorities. The White House sets political direction and brokers the bilateral "MFN deals" with individual manufacturers; HHS/CMS runs the statutory Medicare Drug Price Negotiation Program (IRA) and writes the MFN price-target methodology; USTR negotiates the trade-policy wrapper (the US–Switzerland–Liechtenstein Framework signed November 2025, the parallel EU/Japan/Korea deals); Commerce administers the April 2, 2026 Section 232 pharmaceutical tariff proclamation; FDA controls market access and approval timing. The defining behavior that makes this a distinct actor — rather than a sub-bullet of "the US government" — is that it operates outside Congress: every major instrument deployed since May 2025 (the MFN EO, the Section 232 proclamation, TrumpRx, the bilateral company deals) is executive action, not legislation. (WH EO, May 12, 2025; WH Section 232 proclamation, Apr 2, 2026)

Key personnel as of snapshot date: HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. (sworn in Feb 13, 2025, 52–48 confirmation) (HHS press release); CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz; USTR Ambassador Jamieson Greer (Senate Finance Wyden letter, Jan 28, 2026); FDA Commissioner seat in flux — Marty Makary resigned May 12, 2026, one week before snapshot (NPR, May 12, 2026).

Constituency

The President's electoral base (cost-of-living messaging on prescription drugs), the Republican congressional coalition that did not repeal the IRA in 2025, cabinet appointees serving at pleasure, the Senate confirmation pipeline, and — institutionally — career CMS/FDA staff with their own statutory duties. "Losing the constituency" looks like: visible MFN deals collapsing before 2026 midterms, FDA approval slowdowns blamed on the administration, or a court enjoining a flagship instrument (MFN regulation, Section 232) before November 2026.

Interests (private)

  • Bank a steady stream of visible "win" announcements on drug prices through Nov 2026 midterms — the December 2025 nine-deal package and the April 2026 Regeneron closeout (17 manufacturers, 86% of branded market) were staged precisely for this purpose. Weight: 10/10. (WH fact sheet, Dec 19, 2025; WH Regeneron fact sheet, Apr 23, 2026)
  • Convert the negotiation into US onshoring: the nine December 2025 deals carried a collective $150B US manufacturing commitment; the Swiss Framework promises ~$200B Swiss investment over 5 years ($67B in 2026). Weight: 9/10. (WH fact sheet, Dec 19, 2025; Federal Register, Dec 18, 2025)
  • Tariff revenue and the China-substitution narrative on APIs — Section 232 was framed explicitly as a national-security/supply-chain instrument. Weight: 7/10. (WH Section 232 fact sheet, Apr 2026)
  • Avoid a pharma-sector stock crash that would feed a "Trump tanked biotech" story; this is why MFN agreements are voluntary deals (not regulation) and why Section 232 carved exemptions for signatories. Weight: 7/10. (Inferred from EO structure; see Crowell tariff alert, Apr 2026)
  • Maintain donor-class working relationships with pharma CEOs — the optics are confrontational, the deal flow is collegial. Weight: 5/10. (Inferred)
  • Keep FDA throughput high enough to claim innovation credit; Makary's Commissioner's National Priority Voucher pilot (1–2 month reviews) was the operational expression. Weight: 6/10. (AJMC on Makary regulatory shifts, 2026)

Positions (public)

  • "Americans will pay no more than the lowest price paid in any other developed nation" — MFN target = lowest OECD-country price where GDP/capita ≥ 60% of US. (WH EO, May 12, 2025)
  • 17 manufacturers covering 86% of the branded market have agreed to MFN — first round of bilateral negotiations declared "closed" with Regeneron. (STAT, Apr 23, 2026; WH fact sheet, Apr 23, 2026)
  • TrumpRx.gov is the public-facing storefront for MFN-priced direct-to-consumer drugs; launched Feb 5, 2026 with 43 SKUs from AstraZeneca, Eli Lilly, EMD Serono, Novo Nordisk, Pfizer. (WH TrumpRx fact sheet, Feb 2026; BioPharma Dive, Feb 2026)
  • Patented pharmaceutical imports face a 100% default Section 232 tariff (effective Jul 31, 2026 / Sep 29, 2026); 15% preferential rate for EU, Japan, Korea, Switzerland/Liechtenstein under signed trade frameworks; 0% for firms with executed MFN + onshoring agreements (effective deferral until ~Jan 2029 for compliant firms). (WH Section 232 EO, Apr 2, 2026; Crowell alert, Apr 2026)
  • IRA negotiation continues: Round 1 (10 drugs) prices in effect Jan 1, 2026 (38–79% discounts off 2023 list); Round 2 (15 drugs, IPAY 2028) prices to be published Nov 30, 2026, effective Jan 1, 2028; Round 3 (15 drugs incl. Xolair, Cosentyx, Botox, Trulicity — first-ever Part B drugs) announced Jan 27, 2026, all 15 manufacturers signed participation by Mar 13, 2026, initial CMS offers due by Jun 1, 2026. (CMS Round 3 press release, Jan 27, 2026; CMS Round 1 negotiated prices fact sheet; CMS Round 3 participation fact sheet, Mar 2026)
  • "Foreign freeloading" framing — HHS and CMS publicly position MFN as ending OECD cost-shifting onto American patients. (HHS press release on MFN targets)

BATNA

If negotiations with a holdout manufacturer or foreign government break down, the executive walks to a stack of unilateral instruments — none of which require Congress: 1. Let the 100% Section 232 default rate hit the holdout's imports on Jul 31 / Sep 29, 2026. 2. Issue a notice of proposed rulemaking imposing MFN by regulation on Medicare/Medicaid (the EO's "fallback" pathway). 3. Pursue drug-importation certification under FDCA §804, with FDA waivers for personal importation from low-cost OECD countries (named explicitly in the May 2025 EO). 4. Public shaming via Truth Social / press conferences naming individual CEOs (the same lever used to force the Sep–Dec 2025 deal cascade after the Jul 2025 "17 letters"). The floor payoff: even if zero further bilateral deals are signed, the executive can claim the 17-of-17 first-round sweep and the Round 1 IRA price cuts as concrete 2026 deliverables. (WH EO §6, May 2025; Sidley analysis, Apr 2026)

Red lines

  • Cannot be visibly described as "backing down" from MFN — the slogan is the policy. Walking back the OECD-floor language would cost the President his signature drug-pricing claim into the midterms.
  • Cannot sign a Swiss/EU framework that lets Roche/Novartis/Sanofi pay less than US-headquartered firms for equivalent US market access — domestic-manufacturing constituency would revolt.
  • Cannot let TrumpRx.gov go dark or be seen as failing — the brand is personal to the President. (Inferred from naming convention; see CNBC TrumpRx coverage, Feb 5, 2026)
  • Cannot accept a Senate-blocked CMS or FDA appointment that strips operational control during the Round 3 negotiation window (Jun–Nov 2026).
  • Cannot openly contradict the IRA statute while courts have it under review — must continue running the program as written even while attacking it rhetorically (and KFK/Oz have, in fact, continued the negotiation cycle on schedule).

Leverage

  • Section 232 tariff authority — bites every foreign-headquartered manufacturer (Roche, Novartis, Sanofi, GSK, AstraZeneca, BI, Novo, BMS-via-EU-mfg). The 100/20/15/0% tiered structure is a discrimination engine, not a uniform tax. (WH Section 232 EO)
  • FDA approval timing and CNPV priority vouchers — silent leverage; bites every firm with a pending NDA/BLA. (AJMC on Makary shifts)
  • CMS IRA selection list — every February cycle, CMS picks new drugs. Selection is statutorily constrained but the interpretation of "qualifying single-source drug" is HHS discretion (e.g., the Xolair-biosimilar carve-out decision due by Nov 1, 2026). (Health Affairs IPAY 2028 analysis)
  • Bully pulpit — used in July 2025 ("17 letters") to force the September 2025 AstraZeneca/Pfizer deals and the December 2025 nine-deal cascade. (PharmExec, July 2025)
  • Defense Production Act / critical-product designations — implicit leverage on supply chain.
  • Inter-agency timing — the executive can synchronize a USTR trade-framework announcement, a CMS pricing notice, and a Section 232 amendment in the same news cycle.

Constraints

  • IRA statute — CMS cannot deviate from the Inflation Reduction Act's negotiation framework Congress wrote in 2022. The MFP, the timelines, the eligibility rules are statutory. (KFF IRA factbase)
  • APA notice-and-comment — MFN-by-regulation requires rulemaking; cannot just be a White House announcement. The EO acknowledges this by contemplating future NPRMs.
  • Active litigation — PhRMA, Merck, Teva, BMS, Novartis, AstraZeneca, and (Feb 11, 2026) AbbVie have suits pending; the Fifth Circuit reinstated PhRMA's case after oral argument in Oct 2025; AstraZeneca cert petition is on the SCOTUS docket. Every CMS move is shadowed by potential injunction. (Health Affairs litigation tracker; Fierce Pharma)
  • Section 232 procedural requirements — Commerce must complete an investigation and the President must act on its recommendation; vulnerable to procedural challenge.
  • Senate confirmation drag — Makary's May 12, 2026 resignation creates a confirmation gap at FDA right as Section 232 implementation begins.
  • Federalism — Medicaid MFN provisions interact with 50-state Medicaid programs.

Timeline pressures

  • Jun 1, 2026 — CMS initial offers to Round 3 manufacturers due. (CMS Round 3 press release)
  • Jul 31, 2026 — Section 232 tariffs go live for Annex III companies; Sep 29, 2026 for the rest. (Crowell alert)
  • Nov 1, 2026 — Xolair biosimilar-determination deadline; affects whether Round 3 MFP applies in 2028.
  • Nov 3, 2026 — US midterm elections. Single most important political deadline.
  • Nov 30, 2026 — Statutory deadline for CMS to publish Round 2 (IPAY 2028) negotiated prices.
  • Jan 1, 2028 — Round 2 prices effective; first-ever Part B negotiated prices.
  • Apr 2, 2030 — Section 232 onshoring-rate sunset (20% → 100%); MFN signatory exemptions also re-evaluated around Jan 2029. (Foley Hoag alert, Apr 2026)

Notes on intra-actor coalitions

The "US Executive" is six sub-actors with non-aligned incentive vectors:

  • White House (political wing) — wants visible deal announcements, branded artifacts (TrumpRx.gov, "17 of 17"), and headline tariff numbers. Discount factor: extremely short (weeks). Will trade structural detail for press hits.
  • HHS / CMS (technical wing) — bound by IRA statute and APA. RFK Jr. is politically aligned with the White House but operationally constrained; Oz at CMS must defend the negotiation program in court even while the President criticizes it. Reported HHS leadership churn (Deputy Secretary O'Neill and General Counsel Stuart exiting Feb 2026) signals friction. (STAT, Feb 13, 2026)
  • USTR (trade wing) — Ambassador Greer's instrument is the Swiss/EU/Japan/Korea framework structure. Wants reciprocal pharma market access and protected IP, not domestic price control. Senate Finance Democrats (Wyden's Jan 28, 2026 letter) have publicly questioned the Swiss deal's pharma giveaways. (Wyden letter)
  • Commerce (Section 232 wing) — Howard Lutnick's department wants US manufacturing investment commitments; the 100/20/15/0 tariff ladder is its instrument. Operationally distinct from HHS's pricing instrument.
  • FDA — Makary's tenure showed friction with HHS on approval decisions; the May 12 resignation leaves the agency partly rudderless during the Section 232 ramp and Round 3 negotiation. Career reviewers are reportedly resisting White House/HHS political overrides. (STAT, May 16, 2026)
  • Career civil service (CMS actuaries, FDA reviewers, USTR career staff) — runs the actual machinery. Slows political velocity. Most consequential during the Jun–Nov 2026 Round 3 offer-counteroffer cycle.

Congressional Republicans add a seventh, semi-external faction: STAT (Mar 17, 2026) reports MFN itself "divides Trump and Congress" — Senate Republicans worry MFN-by-regulation chills R&D. (STAT, Mar 17, 2026)

Role

The 119th Congress (sworn in 3 Jan 2025) is the legislature that wrote the IRA drug-pricing program in 2022 and that retains exclusive authority to codify, modify, or repeal it. Under unified Republican control — Senate 53R-47D (incl. 2 independents caucusing with Democrats), House 220R-215D (CRS, Membership of the 119th Congress, 4 Aug 2025; Ballotpedia, 119th Congress) — it has largely deferred to the Trump administration's executive-led MFN and Section 232 strategy rather than legislating its own pharma-pricing reset, but has acted in one major area: PBM reform via the Consolidated Appropriations Act 2026 (signed 3 Feb 2026), which delinks PBM compensation from Part D list prices (Mintz, 6 Feb 2026; Pharmacy Times). The four committees that matter for the Swiss-pharma file are: Senate Finance (Chair Mike Crapo R-ID, RM Ron Wyden D-OR — Medicare/Medicaid/trade), Senate HELP (Chair Bill Cassidy R-LA, RM Bernie Sanders I-VT — FDA, NIH, public-health), House Energy & Commerce (Chair Brett Guthrie R-KY — Medicare Part B, FDA, 340B), and House Ways & Means (Chair Jason Smith R-MO — Medicare Part D, tariffs, tax).

Swiss Federal Council (Bundesrat) — acting through SECO and FDFA

Switzerland's collegial seven-member executive: the legal counterparty signing any US trade deal, with SECO running the table and FDFA managing the diplomatic frame.

Role

The Federal Council (Bundesrat) is the Swiss government's seven-member collective executive and the formal counterparty to the US Trade Representative in the bilateral trade negotiations triggered by the November 14, 2025 Swiss–US Joint Statement on Framework for an Agreement on Reciprocal Trade (SECO, Nov 14, 2025). Operational lead sits with SECO inside the Department of Economic Affairs (WBF), headed by Federal Councillor and 2026 President Guy Parmelin (SVP) (WBF, Jan 1, 2026). The Federal Department of Foreign Affairs (FDFA), under Ignazio Cassis (FDP), provides diplomatic framing, ambassadorial access in Washington, and coordination across the federal administration, but explicitly does not lead the substantive negotiation — Cassis has described his role as "door opener" inside a collective Federal Council strategy (SRF, Aug 2025). The 2025 negotiating phase was personally led by then-President Karin Keller-Sutter (FDP, Finance), whose telephone diplomacy with President Trump in late July 2025 became the central political episode of the dispute (NZZ, 2025). On January 14, 2026 the Federal Council adopted the formal negotiating mandate for a binding trade agreement (admin.ch press release, Jan 14, 2026; Swissinfo, Jan 14, 2026).

Constituency

The Federal Council answers to (i) the Federal Assembly (National Council + Council of States), which elects each councillor and must approve any treaty; (ii) the foreign-policy commissions APK-N/APK-S and the Conference of Cantonal Governments (KdK), both formally consulted on the US mandate (admin.ch, Jan 14, 2026); (iii) the Swiss electorate, which can force an optional treaty referendum if 50,000 signatures are collected; SP co-president Cédric Wermuth has publicly floated launching one (Swissinfo, 2026); and (iv) peak associations — economiesuisse (cross-sector) and Interpharma (pharma) on the employer side, SGB/USS on the union side. "Losing the constituency" looks like a lost referendum, a Parliament rejection, or a withdrawal of cantonal consent — any of which would force re-negotiation under expired US patience.

Interests (private)

  • Preserve 15% ceiling on US tariffs across all Swiss exports (machinery, watches, chemicals, pharma) — the November framework is fragile and tariff snap-back to 39% is the explicit US fallback. Weight: 10/10. (Al Jazeera, Nov 14, 2025)
  • Avoid being framed as captured by Big Pharma; SP and Greens are already attacking the $200B investment pledge as a corporate giveaway. Weight: 8/10. (inferred from Swissinfo, 2026)
  • Protect "honest broker" / neutrality positioning to keep Switzerland viable as a diplomatic venue — Cassis has been criticized that neutrality risks becoming "an excuse for political allegiance" (cash.ch, 2026). Weight: 7/10.
  • Keep the cantonal coalition stable, especially Basel-Stadt/Basel-Landschaft (Roche/Novartis tax base) and Zurich (banking/insurance). Weight: 8/10. (inferred)
  • Prevent capital flight and CHF over-appreciation that a tariff escalation would trigger; SNB independence constrains direct Federal Council action. Weight: 7/10. (inferred)
  • Avoid setting a precedent in which a foreign power dictates Swiss domestic drug pricing, which would breach the statutory authority of BAG (FOPH) under KVG/LAMal Art. 52a. Weight: 9/10. (inferred)

Positions (public)

  • The 15% cap is a success worth defending: Parmelin announced retroactive effect from Nov 14, 2025, hours after being elected 2026 President (20 Minuten, Dec 10, 2025).
  • Section 232 pharma tariffs are "an additional burden for the exporting Swiss pharmaceutical industry" — Federal Council expressed regret but accepted that the 15% Swiss cap remains intact (Swissinfo, Apr 2026; watson.ch, Apr 2026).
  • The Federal Council formally adopted the binding-agreement negotiating mandate on Jan 14, 2026, after consulting APK-N, APK-S, and KdK (admin.ch, Jan 14, 2026).
  • Keller-Sutter has publicly stated about her July 2025 Trump call: "Within minutes it was clear that he would wipe this deal off the table" — signaling to domestic audiences that Trump deal-making is volatile and any agreement carries political risk (cash.ch, 2025).
  • No public Federal Council position links the US deal to LAMal/KVG pricing reform; the Bundesrat has so far treated MFN-spillover into Swiss domestic pricing as a separate health-policy file under EDI/BAG.

BATNA

"No deal" by the US-imposed March 31, 2026 / July 31, 2026 milestones means reversion to a tariff snap-back trajectory: a return to the 39% reciprocal rate floated in summer 2025 (NZZ, 2025), plus full Section 232 exposure at up to 100% on patented pharmaceuticals for any Swiss firm without an MFN-style company-level agreement (Crowell & Moring, Apr 2026). Procedurally, the Federal Council's fallback is a WTO Dispute Settlement complaint (slow, currently dysfunctional with US blocking the Appellate Body), coordination with EU/Japan/Korea (each of whom already has a 15% deal), and limited retaliatory tariffs — which the Federal Council has signaled it will not use because Switzerland's market is too small to bite the US.

Red lines

  • No commitment that the Federal Council cannot deliver through Parliament/referendum — a treaty rejected at home would destroy Swiss diplomatic credibility for a generation and end careers (Swissinfo, 2026).
  • No sectoral carve-out for pharma that visibly sacrifices machinery, watches, or chemicals — economiesuisse has explicitly stated it is "not purposeful to focus on individual economic sectors" and intra-employer unity would crack (Berner Zeitung, 2025).
  • No undertaking that binds federal authority over domestic drug pricing (KVG Art. 52a, Spezialitätenliste) — that authority sits with BAG and the EDI, not the Federal Council acting alone, and would require legislation.
  • No formal abandonment of neutrality (e.g., joining US-led sanctions regimes as part of the deal) — would trigger SVP and pacifist-left opposition simultaneously.

Leverage

  • Roche's $50B and Novartis' multi-billion US capex commitments are formally Swiss-domiciled corporate decisions but were politically credited to Bern in the November framework (Fortune, Apr 22, 2025; Al Jazeera, Nov 14, 2025). The Federal Council can credibly suggest these commitments are revocable if framework conditions deteriorate.
  • Switzerland's role in API/intermediates supply chains: a meaningful share of US-consumed patented drugs depends on Swiss manufacturing (Basel cluster, Visp).
  • CHF/SNB and Swiss financial-sector relevance to US dollar clearing and asset management — a side-lever rather than a primary one.
  • Diplomatic credibility: Geneva-based good offices and OECD/WTO coordination capacity.

Constraints

  • Federal Constitution Art. 184 (foreign policy is Federal Council competence) but Art. 166 (Parliament approves treaties); important agreements trigger optional referendum under Art. 141.
  • Collegiality (Art. 177 BV): decisions are collective, any one councillor can delay by demanding new joint deliberation.
  • Federalism: cantons retain healthcare competencies (KV implementation, hospital planning) — Federal Council cannot trade these away.
  • Statutory authority over drug pricing rests with BAG/EDI under KVG; binding pricing commitments to the US would require legislative change.
  • Parliamentary commissions APK-N/APK-S must be consulted on negotiating mandates (admin.ch, Jan 14, 2026).

Role

Basel-Stadt (BS) and Basel-Landschaft (BL) are the host jurisdictions for Roche's global headquarters (Grenzacherstrasse 124/Bau 1 tower in Basel-Stadt) and for the Roche manufacturing and R&D footprint that spills into Kaiseraugst (AG) and Reinach (BL). They are formally outside the US–Swiss trade negotiation — Article 54 of the Federal Constitution gives the Bundesrat exclusive foreign-affairs competence — but they hold the cantonal levers that determine whether Roche's $50bn US capex pledge translates into a Basel contraction or only a US expansion: corporate tax design, zoning and building permits for the Basel campus, university R&D partnerships, and the political mood of the host city. The Regierungsräte (cabinets) of both cantons, plus the Handelskammer beider Basel as joint business voice, behave as a distinct actor whose interest function is cluster preservation, not federal trade policy. (Roche Building 1 — Herzog & de Meuron / Grenzacherstrasse 124 HQ, accessed 2026-05-19; Roche Quartierinfo — laufende Arealentwicklung Basel, accessed 2026-05-19)

Constituency

  • Grosser Rat (BS, 100 seats) and Landrat (BL, 90 seats) — cantonal parliaments that approve tax law, budgets and Standortpaket-style subsidies.
  • Cantonal voters via referendum — the binding constraint: BS voters approved the OECD-Pillar-2 "Standortpaket" on 18 May 2025 by 63.2% (27,094 yes / 15,765 no), validating the political model of recycling minimum-tax revenue back to pharma. (SRF — Basel-Stadt sagt klar Ja, 2025-05-18; BaZ — OECD-Mindeststeuer fliesst zu 80 Prozent an Pharmakonzerne, 2025)
  • Handelskammer beider Basel (HKBB) — cross-cantonal business chamber, the de facto private-sector channel into the cantonal cabinets. (HKBB — Jahresmediengespräch 2025)
  • Universities — Universität Basel and FHNW, both with deep Roche/Novartis research ties.
  • Roche/Novartis employees as voters — Roche alone employs ~13,500–15,000 people in Switzerland, the majority in Basel-Stadt (~11,900 at Basel site) and the wider Basel area. (Roche Schweiz Über uns, accessed 2026-05-19)
  • "Losing the constituency" = a cantonal election in which a left-Green-led majority refuses further pharma-tax recycling, or a popular initiative imposing minimum-tax revenue redistribution that breaks Roche's tolerance for Basel as HQ.

Interests (private)

  • Protect Roche/Novartis cantonal corporate-tax payments. Roughly 500 "Statusgesellschaften" — overwhelmingly pharma — pay ≈CHF 500m/year, or 15–20% of cantonal tax revenue; Roche and Novartis together are estimated to be roughly one-third of all corporate tax in BS. Weight: 10/10. (BaZ — So gut verdient Basel an Novartis und Roche, 2024; NZZ — Basel ist zu abhängig von der Pharmabranche, 2023)
  • Preserve Basel as the global pharma HQ cluster. Life sciences contribute ~39% of nominal gross value-added in BS, the largest sectoral share of any Swiss canton. Weight: 10/10. (Amt für Wirtschaft und Arbeit BS — Life Sciences Industrie, accessed 2026-05-19)
  • Protect ~33,000 life-sciences jobs in the Basel Area (Roche, Novartis, ~700+ life-science firms in the cluster). Weight: 9/10. (Basel Area Supercluster, accessed 2026-05-19)
  • Protect the local R&D ecosystem (Roche pRED, Institute of Human Biology, Switzerland Innovation Park Basel Area, Uni Basel). Roche announced ≈CHF 1.2–1.4bn further Basel-area investment through 2030 (incl. Bau 12, ~CHF 500m R&D building, completion Q4 2029) — protecting this pipeline is the realistic counterweight to the US capex shift. Weight: 9/10. (SRF — Roche weiht 1.2-Mrd-Forschungszentrum ein; Baublatt — Roche investiert 1.2 Mrd in Basel)
  • Defend Basel-Stadt's donor-canton status in NFA (Finanzausgleich). Pharma tax volatility transmits straight into BS NFA contributions. Weight: 7/10. (swissinfo — Finanzausgleich Basel-Stadt anpassen)
  • Avoid the "pharma puppet" perception. The 2025 Standortpaket referendum showed that ~37% of BS voters already view the canton as captured. Crossing the optics line risks the next initiative. Weight: 6/10. (Bajour — Alles für die Pharma, 2025)
  • (Inferred) Diversify the tax base over time — quietly hedge against pharma over-concentration via biotech-startup support and ICT. Weight: 5/10.

Positions (public)

  • Regierungspräsident Conradin Cramer (LDP/BS) used the Basel canton's first-ever WEF appearance in January 2026 to brand BS publicly as a "leading life sciences hub" with Roche chair Severin Schwan and Regierungsrat Kaspar Sutter on the panel — explicit signal that the cantonal executive will not let federal trade tension dent the marketing of Basel as Roche's home. (BaZ — Basel-Stadt am WEF: Conradin Cramer kritisiert Trump, 2026-01; BaZ — Basel reist erstmals ans WEF, 2026-01)
  • Regierungsrat Kaspar Sutter (SP/BS, Department of Economy, Social Affairs and Environment) has publicly warned that Trump's tariff threats "strengthen the tendency for production to shift to the USA for the US market," while framing Basel's counter-offer as "Rechtssicherheit, Verlässlichkeit, Investitionssicherheit" (legal certainty, reliability, investment security). (Blick — Basel zittert: US-Handelsstreit gefährdet Pharma-Hochburg; BaZ — BeOne Medicines verlegt Sitz nach Basel)
  • BL Regierungsrat (Regierungspräsident Anton Lauber FDP; Volkswirtschaftsdirektor Thomas Weber SVP) visited Roche Pharma (Schweiz) AG in Reinach (~160 employees) and publicly emphasised a "clear location strategy that secures and strengthens the Life Sciences locations Basel/Nordwestschweiz." (Kanton BL — Regierungsrat besucht Roche Pharma Reinach)
  • Handelskammer beider Basel publicly welcomed the November-2025 US-Swiss 15% framework as "significant relief" that "corrects the market-distorting asymmetry vs European competitors", but noted 30% of regional pharma exports go to the US and that "Trump's new pricing model changes everything." (HKBB — Stimmungsbarometer Herbst 2025; Badische Zeitung — HKBB Umfrage US-Wirtschaftspolitik)
  • BS voters (18 May 2025) approved the Standortpaket 63.2%:36.8%, of which ~80% of disbursements flow to firms with >2,500 employees (de facto Roche, Novartis, Lonza). (BaZ — OECD-Mindeststeuer fliesst zu 80 Prozent an Pharmakonzerne)

BATNA

If the federal US–Swiss deal collapses or Roche substantially relocates capacity to the US: BS budget loses roughly CHF 250–400m/year in directly-attributable pharma corporate-tax receipts (Roche + Novartis share of the ~CHF 500m Statusgesellschaft block), forcing either (a) a public-services squeeze, (b) a renegotiation of NFA contributions to the federal pot, or (c) an accelerated 5–10 year diversification toward biotech SMEs and ICT — none of which restore the fiscal floor in time. Politically, BS retains the Roche HQ tower and ~CHF 1.2bn of already-permitted construction through 2030 as a sunk-cost anchor — that is the actual payoff floor. (NZZ — Pharma hat Basel reich gemacht — droht das Ende?)

Red lines

  • No public federal commitment to wind down a specific Basel Roche site. Crossing it would politically destroy the sitting Regierungsrat at the next cantonal election (BS Regierungsrat re-elected 2024 for 4-year term).
  • No federal cession of cantonal healthcare or tax authority (BAG drug-list authority is federal, but cantonal corporate tax sovereignty and cantonal hospital procurement are not on the table) — violating cantonal sovereignty under Art. 3 BV would trigger a Kantonsreferendum.
  • No federal acceptance of a US-imposed MFN drug-pricing concession that demonstrably suppresses Roche's Swiss-booked profit (which is what funds the BS tax base via the principal-company / IP-holding structures).
  • No surrender on the OECD-Pillar-2 / Standortpaket recycling model, which the BS electorate just ratified.

Leverage

  • Cantonal corporate-tax design. BS effective rate ≈13.04% pre-2026; the canton voted in 2025 to move to a two-tier tariff with the cantonal profit-tax for profits >CHF 50m raised to 15% in 2026, calibrated exactly to absorb the OECD Pillar 2 top-up locally rather than ceding it to the federal Ergänzungssteuer pot. This is the textbook example of cantonal fiscal leverage post-BEPS. (Handelskammer beider Basel — Basler Standortpaket; BaZ — Basel-Stadt reagiert mit Förderpaket auf OECD-Mindeststeuer)
  • Zoning + building permits for the Roche Basel campus (Bau 12, the next 72m tower scheduled for Q4 2029 completion) — BS is the permitting authority. A neighbour appeal (Rekurs) against the Roche tower is already public. (SRF — Anwohner-Rekurs gegen Roche-Ausbau)
  • Standortpaket subsidies — up to ~CHF 270–310m/year in innovation- and environment-fund disbursements, 80% of which structurally goes to large pharma. Effectively a cantonal price for Roche staying.
  • University research partnerships (Uni Basel, D-BSSE ETH Zurich in Basel) — non-fungible asset Roche cannot replicate in the US within 5 years.
  • Voting bloc of Roche employees — Roche-employee residency in BS/BL gives the cantons informal but real veto power over any federal deal that would visibly damage their employer.

Constraints

  • Article 54 BV — foreign-affairs competence is federal; cantons can lobby but not negotiate the US deal directly.
  • OECD/G20 BEPS Pillar 2 (effective 2024 in CH) — kills the historical sub-15% corporate-tax lever; BS can recycle revenue but not undercut the floor. (HKBB — Position OECD-Mindeststeuer)
  • Cantonal referendum risk — any further Standortpaket-style transfer is subject to fakultatives Referendum and could be overturned.
  • NFA solidarity logic — too aggressive a pharma-only tax break invites complaint from net-recipient cantons.
  • Federal preemption on drug pricing (KVG, HMG, BAG Spezialitätenliste) — the cantons cannot directly trade off Swiss list prices for tariff relief.

Timeline pressures

  • Mid-2026 US–Swiss negotiation window — overlaps with BS 2027 budget drafting (Q3 2026); a deal that visibly threatens Roche tax receipts hits the next budget cycle directly.
  • 31 July 2026 — Section 232 pharma tariff effective date for large companies; the 15% Swiss rate is "reducible to zero" only with a successful bilateral pricing deal. (Crowell & Moring — Section 232 pharma tariffs, 2026-04)
  • Q4 2029 — scheduled completion of Roche Bau 12 in Basel; any signal before then that Roche is "moving to the US" can stall the project mid-build.
  • 2028 BS Regierungsrat election — Cramer (LDP) and Sutter (SP) both face voters; their handling of the US deal is a live campaign issue.
  • BL Regierungsrat term — Lauber serves as Regierungspräsident 2025/26; political turnover at end of 2026.

Notes on intra-actor coalitions

  • Basel-Stadt (urban, SP-led plurality + LDP/FDP, Greens influential) carries the HQ tower, the higher tax dependence, and the more diverse political coalition. The "protect pharma" line is bipartisan but the terms are contested (Standortpaket referendum had a clear left-Green opposition led by BastA! and parts of SP-base). (BastA! — 500 Millionen für die, die schon sehr viel haben)
  • Basel-Landschaft (more rural, FDP/SVP-dominant cabinet) is also pharma-exposed via Reinach (Roche Pharma Schweiz, ~160 employees), Pratteln/Muttenz chemistry, but the cluster mass sits in BS. BL is more pro-business and less likely to apply political brakes to a federal deal.
  • Handelskammer beider Basel acts as the joint operational front and consistently lobbies Bern; it papered over the BS/BL split during the 2025 Standortpaket campaign and is the channel most likely to be tactically aligned with Roche's federal lobbying.
  • Latent fault line: BS Greens/SP-left bloc vs BL bourgeois bloc on "diversify the economy at Roche's expense" — currently dormant but activates if Roche announces concrete Basel cuts.

Swiss Public & Domestic Payers — BAG, Santésuisse/curafutura, patients-as-voters

The dual-identity actor: Switzerland's residents are simultaneously LAMal patients paying record drug prices, taxpayers cashing Roche's corporate-tax cheque, and voters whose referendum threat backstops any US deal touching the Spezialitätenliste.

Role

The demand-side, regulatory-payer side of Swiss pharma — distinct from the Federal Council (which negotiates the US deal) and from the Basel cantons (which bank Roche's tax). It bundles four interlocking entities. (1) The Bundesamt für Gesundheit (BAG/FOPH) runs the Spezialitätenliste (SL) of medicines reimbursed under LAMal/KVG/OKP, setting ex-factory prices via therapeutic comparison plus international comparison against nine European reference countries (DE, FR, AT, BE, NL, DK, FI, SE, UK) on a three-year review cycle (BAG — Medikamentenpreise FAQ, 2025; BAG — Überprüfung alle drei Jahre, 2025). (2) Santésuisse (~62 OKP carriers) and (3) curafutura (Helsana, CSS, Sanitas, KPT), which carry the financial pain of any SL price increase through annual premium-setting (santésuisse — Auslandpreisvergleich 2025, 2025; curafutura — Positionen, 2026). (4) Patients-as-voters, channelled through Krebsliga Schweiz and allied patient organisations, the federal Preisüberwacher Stefan Meierhans, the consumer press (K-Tipp, Beobachter, Stiftung für Konsumentenschutz / Sara Stalder), and — most powerful — the federal popular-initiative and referendum machinery that came close to a constitutional healthcare cost cap on 9 June 2024 (SRF — Kostenbremse scheitert, 9 Jun 2024).

Constituency

~8.8M residents under mandatory LAMal insurance, paying head-tax premiums set per canton. The constituency: cantonal premium-payers (2026 average +4.4%, adult monthly mean CHF 465.30) (BAG — Prämien 2026, 24 Sep 2025); patient-organisation members; trade unions (SGB/USS) and the political left (SP, Grüne) campaigning on premium relief; and at the margin SVP/FDP coalitions defending industry-friendly framings. "Losing the constituency" looks like (a) a successful popular initiative forcing a constitutional cost cap, (b) a referendum killing a US treaty perceived as raising Swiss drug prices, or (c) Bundesrat replacement of an EDI/BAG head seen as captured by pharma — all live on the 2026–2028 horizon.

Interests (private)

  • Lower domestic ex-factory drug prices toward European averages — the Auslandpreisvergleich shows Swiss generics ~45.3% and biosimilars ~29.9% more expensive than the reference basket, and originals materially above (santésuisse–Interpharma APV 2025, 2025). Weight: 9/10.
  • Slow LAMal premium growth — premiums rose +4.4% for 2026 after sharp 2023–2025 increases, and the political cost of further rises is now extreme (BAG, 24 Sep 2025; SRF Prämien 2026, 2025). Weight: 10/10.
  • Preserve Roche/Novartis Swiss tax base, jobs, and R&D footprint — never said out loud by santésuisse or patient groups, but priced into every parliamentary vote on cost containment, and visible in the 62.8% rejection of the Mitte cost-brake initiative (Swissinfo — Kostenbremse 62.8% Nein, 9 Jun 2024). Weight: 7/10 (inferred).
  • Maintain fast access to innovative medicines (early launch in CH), particularly oncology — Krebsliga's standing demand (Krebsliga — Krebsmedikamente, 2025). Weight: 8/10.
  • Avoid being the country whose published "list" price props up the US MFN benchmark — i.e., avoid becoming the floor against which the US negotiates everyone else's price down, which would create direct retaliatory pressure on Roche to raise Swiss list prices to keep US comparators palatable (SWI — Riskante Kampf um Arzneimittelpreise, 28 Sep 2025). Weight: 8/10 (inferred from spillover logic).
  • Defend BAG's statutory authority over the SL against any US treaty term that would constrain it. Weight: 9/10 (inferred from KVG Art. 52a and BAG institutional interest).

Positions (public)

  • BAG: each three-year SL review cuts prices on roughly half of reviewed molecules by ~12% average; recurring savings >CHF 1.5B/year; consultation on Kostendämpfungspaket-2 ordinance package opened 18 Feb 2026 through 26 May 2026 (BAG Kostendämpfungspaket 2, Feb 2026; BAG Vernehmlassung, 18 Feb 2026).
  • Santésuisse: Swiss generic prices must be cut to European levels via APV-style mechanism, distribution margins slashed, confidential-discount transparency within 3–5 years (santésuisse APV statement, 2025).
  • curafutura: backs a reference-price (Festbetragssystem) for generics; welcomes CHF ~400M/year savings from the volume-threshold price cuts Parliament passed in March 2025 (curafutura, 2026).
  • Preisüberwacher Stefan Meierhans: Swiss medicine prices "deutlich höher" than abroad; cheapest UK generics cost <20% of OKP-reimbursed Swiss equivalents; demands annual price reviews (Preisüberwacher — Medikamente, 2025).
  • Sara Stalder (Konsumentenschutz) on Roche CEO Schinecker's October 2025 push for higher Swiss prices: "Es gibt keinen Grund, diese weiter zu erhöhen" — prices already at record highs and any rise feeds straight into premiums (20min, Oct 2025).
  • Manuela Weichelt (Grüne, NR) calls it unacceptable that "ein US-Präsident die Schweizer Krankenkassenprämien beeinflusst" and labels Roche's launch-delay threats a "Drohgebärde"; Kris Vietze (FDP) is the lone mainstream voice openly accepting spillover for "einige hochinnovative Medikamente" (20min, Oct 2025).
  • Krebsliga Schweiz: demands pricing transparency for oncology drugs; calls confidential rebates incompatible with sustainable financing (Krebsliga, 2024–25).
  • No Swiss patient or payer organisation has yet issued a formal public position on the US–Roche MFN deal's Swiss spillover as of 19 May 2026 — a notable silence after 20min reported in December 2025 that the deal does "not immediately" raise Swiss prices because of state regulation (20min, 20 Dec 2025).

BATNA

"No deal" — a US–Swiss trade agreement that closes without touching SL pricing — leaves the actor at its status-quo payoff: continued 3-year SL reviews (next major cohort under Kostendämpfungspaket-2 from Q3 2026), ~4%/year premium drift, ~CHF 9.2B/year OKP medicine spend (BAG FAQ, 2025), plus the option of a second initiative cycle — the 9 Jun 2024 SP Prämien-Entlastungs-Initiative lost 55.5% Nein but the indirect counter-proposal forcing higher cantonal premium subsidies took effect (admin.ch, 9 Jun 2024). The BATNA floor is livable; this actor is not desperate to settle, which is precisely why it can credibly threaten a referendum on any US deal crossing its red lines.

Red lines

  • Any treaty clause that raises SL prices to protect US MFN benchmarks — would trigger a popular initiative within 18 months and politically destroy the EDI head who signed it.
  • Any cession of BAG's statutory price-setting authority under KVG Art. 52a to a trade-treaty mechanism — would violate the federal-statute framework and be challenged before the Federal Supreme Court; civil servants at BAG would face institutional capture allegations.
  • Any sealed confidential-rebate regime that prevents Santésuisse and curafutura from verifying that insurers (and ultimately premium-payers) capture the savings — both associations have explicitly demanded transparency within 3–5 years (santésuisse APV statement, 2025).
  • For patient organisations: any deal that trades faster US access for slower Swiss launches — Krebsliga's first-order interest is timely oncology access (Krebsliga, 2025).

Leverage

  • Statutory price-setting authority: BAG controls SL inclusion and ex-factory pricing; no US trade deal can override KVG without parliamentary amendment, which is referendum-exposed.
  • Referendum threat: 50,000 signatures suffice for an optional treaty referendum; SP and Grüne have signalled willingness to launch one against any US deal seen as a pharma giveaway (Swissinfo — direkte Demokratie und Handelsabkommen, 2026).
  • Popular-initiative pipeline: even after the June 2024 double defeat, a relaunched cost-brake initiative remains a credible 24-month threat — the Mitte vote of 37.2% Yes already exceeds typical relaunch thresholds.
  • Consumer-press megaphone: K-Tipp, Beobachter, Blick, Sara Stalder, and Preisüberwacher Meierhans produce a steady cadence of high-salience price-comparison stories that move public opinion (Beobachter — immer höhere Medikamentenkosten, 2024–25).
  • Premium-rate announcement cycle: annual late-September BAG press conference is a guaranteed news event that translates any pricing concession into a household number; the 2026 round (4.4%) was framed around "alterende Bevölkerung, neue Therapien und teurere Medikamente" (BAG, 24 Sep 2025).

Constraints

  • LAMal/KVG statutory framework binds BAG to therapeutic comparison + nine-country APV; BAG cannot unilaterally apply US-style negotiated pricing without legislative change.
  • Cantonal authority over hospital pricing and premium subsidies under federalism — BAG cannot dictate to cantons, and a US deal cannot dictate to either layer.
  • OECD/EFTA framework on transparency in medicines pricing — confidential US-style net-price deals sit awkwardly against Swiss transparency norms.
  • No seat at the Bern–Washington negotiating table: this actor must influence Federal Council/SECO via Parliament, EDI consultation channels (Vernehmlassung), and public opinion — never directly.
  • Federal Court oversight: BAG SL listing decisions are appealable to the Bundesverwaltungsgericht, and Roche has used and threatened this route (the Lunsumio withdrawal episode) (SWI, 28 Sep 2025).

Timeline pressures

  • 26 May 2026: close of Vernehmlassung on Kostendämpfungspaket-2 ordinance (KVV/KLV revisions) — fixes the framework BAG will use for the next SL review cohort (BAG Vernehmlassung, 18 Feb 2026).
  • Late Sep 2026: annual BAG premium-rate announcement for 2027 — the single most politically-charged healthcare news event of the year and the hardest deadline by which any US-deal spillover must not show up in premiums.
  • 2026–2027 SL triennial reviews: rolling cohorts include molecules in Roche's growth portfolio (Ocrevus listed since 2018 reviewed previously; Vabysmo, Hemlibra, Polivy on accelerating SL cadences) — BAG's published guidance commits to recurring savings >CHF 1.5B/year from these reviews (BAG Kostendämpfungspaket 2, Feb 2026).
  • Eidgenössische Wahlen 2027: federal elections — the SP and Grüne have already signalled premium relief and pharma transparency as priority campaign issues.
  • July 31, 2026: US Section 232 pharma tariff tier takes effect (Crowell & Moring, Apr 2026) — if Roche responds by raising Swiss list prices to manage MFN exposure, the political crisis lands inside the September 2026 premium-announcement window.

Notes on intra-actor coalitions

This actor is genuinely fractured and a sophisticated counterparty (Roche, USTR) can exploit the fractures: - BAG / EDI (technocratic): wants defensible methodology (APV + therapeutic comparison) and institutional autonomy; would accept incremental access-vs-price trade-offs that preserve SL authority. - Santésuisse + curafutura (commercial): want lower ex-factory prices and lower distribution margins; do NOT want a patient backlash that drives population pressure for non-OKP supplementary insurance markets they cannot price. - Krebsliga + patient organisations: prioritise speed-of-access over absolute price, sometimes implicitly aligning with Roche/Interpharma against insurers, particularly on rare-disease and oncology drugs (Krebsliga, 2025). - Preisüberwacher + consumer press (populist): focus on the headline price gap to Europe; less concerned with R&D incentives, openly hostile to "Schaufensterpreise" with confidential rebates. - General public-as-taxpayer (latent): in the 9 June 2024 votes, 62.8% rejected the Mitte cost cap and 55.5% rejected the SP premium cap — German-speaking Switzerland in particular signalled that it values pharma prosperity (jobs, Basel cluster, federal tax) enough to tolerate high prices it claims to resent (Swissinfo, 9 Jun 2024; admin.ch — Prämien-Entlastung, 9 Jun 2024). The Romandie and Ticino voted Yes to both initiatives — the cost-control coalition is real but geographically asymmetric. This is the load-bearing insight: the same Swiss voter wants lower drug prices AND a thriving Roche, and at the ballot box has so far chosen the latter.

US Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) and Commercial Insurers

The middlemen whose rebate-arbitrage business model is being squeezed simultaneously from below (TrumpRx direct-to-consumer), above (MFN/IRA-compressed list prices), and the side (federal delinking + FTC scrutiny).

Snapshot date: May 19, 2026.

Role

PBMs are the formulary-and-rebate intermediaries between drug manufacturers, dispensing pharmacies, and end payers (employers, health plans, Medicare Part D, Medicaid MCOs). Three vertically-integrated entities — CVS Caremark (CVS Health), Express Scripts (Cigna/Evernorth), and OptumRx (UnitedHealth Group) — processed roughly 80% of all US prescription claims in 2025 (Drug Channels, Mar 2026). Each of the Big 3 is fused with either a large commercial insurer (Aetna inside CVS, Cigna Healthcare inside Cigna, UnitedHealthcare inside UNH) or, in CVS's case, the largest retail pharmacy chain. In the mid-2026 pricing fights they occupy an unusual seat: nominally on the "payer" side against Pharma in formulary negotiations, but increasingly adversaries of the White House MFN/TrumpRx push because direct-to-consumer manufacturer pricing routes around them entirely. Their observable behavior in 2025–2026 — Cigna/Express Scripts announcing a "rebate-free" commercial model (Healthcare Dive, Oct 2025); CVS pivoting its Medicare bid book around delinking — is best read as preemptive repositioning ahead of statutory change.

Constituency

Public-company shareholders of CVS Health, Cigna, and UnitedHealth Group (each among the 25 largest US firms by revenue); ~270 million covered lives across the three integrated platforms; employer plan-sponsor clients (the Business Group on Health, ERIC, large self-funded employers); Medicare Part D plan partners; and the PBM trade association PCMA. "Losing the constituency" looks like: (a) large self-funded employers shifting to transparent/pass-through PBM startups (Mark Cuban's Cost Plus pharmacy, Capital Rx, Navitus, Rightway) at contract renewal; (b) shareholder revolt if Health Services operating income compresses below mid-single-digits; (c) CMS disqualifying a PBM from Part D bids for non-compliance with the February 2026 CAA delinking provisions.

Interests (private)

  • Preserve a viable rebate-arbitrage / spread-pricing revenue stream for as long as legally possible, even as the public model shifts to "pass-through." FTC found Big 3 generated >$7.3 billion in excess-of-acquisition revenue on 51 specialty generics 2017–2022 alone. Weight: 10/10. (FTC Second Interim Staff Report, Jan 14 2025)
  • Maintain formulary control — the single biggest source of bargaining leverage against manufacturers, and the asset that vertical integration monetizes. Loss of formulary discretion = loss of pricing power. Weight: 10/10. (inferred from House Oversight findings of formulary steering, House Oversight, Jul 23 2024)
  • Avoid disintermediation by TrumpRx.gov for the highest-margin specialty/branded drugs. OptumRx alone managed ~$87B of specialty spend in 2025; any TrumpRx expansion of that surface area is direct revenue at risk. Weight: 9/10. (UNH 10-K FY2025; Buchanan Ingersoll on TrumpRx)
  • Defuse FTC antitrust posture — the Cigna/Express Scripts February 2026 FTC settlement on insulin (no admission of fault, no monetary penalty) is the template they want for the remaining administrative complaints against Caremark and OptumRx. Weight: 8/10. (Healthcare Dive, Q4 2025 recap)
  • Slow the state-level PBM-reform patchwork (>40 states have enacted some form of PBM regulation 2023–2026) into a preemptive federal floor they can live with. Weight: 7/10. (Mintz, Apr 22 2026)
  • Protect medical-loss-ratio (MLR) economics on the insurer side: lower drug list prices from MFN/IRA actually help the insurance arms (less premium pressure), which creates a real intra-firm tension at CVS/Cigna/UNH. Weight: 6/10. (inferred; visible in Cigna's willingness to drop rebates first)

Positions (public)

  • "PBMs share the Trump Administration's goal of reducing prescription drug costs… and have repeatedly called on drug companies to lower list prices, which is the most effective and lasting way to deliver a better deal." — PCMA on MFN executive order. (PCMA via AMCP, 2025)
  • After the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026 (signed Feb 3, 2026) delinked Part D PBM compensation from drug prices, PCMA: "Big Pharma Wins Big with Passage of PBM and Employer Mandates" — framing reform as a manufacturer giveaway. (PCMA press release, Jan 30 2026)
  • PCMA urged DOL to rescind its proposed PBM transparency rule as duplicative of the CAA. (PCMA, Mar 19 2026)
  • Cigna/Evernorth publicly committed to transitioning commercial clients off rebate retention to point-of-sale savings — a defensive pivot framed as customer-driven. (Healthcare Dive, Oct 2025)
  • CVS Health on Q1 2026 earnings call: Caremark is "executing against 2026 rebate guarantee commitments" with "no surprises" expected H2 — i.e., the existing book is intact through 2026. (CVS Q1 2026 call transcript, May 6 2026)
  • Big 3 sworn testimony (Joyner/Caremark, Kautzner/Express Scripts, Conway/OptumRx) before House Oversight: claim >99% pass-through of Medicare rebates and sub-5% operating margins. (House Oversight, Jul 23 2024)

BATNA

If excluded from the MFN/TrumpRx deal architecture entirely, the floor is: keep the Medicare Part D and commercial-employer book under the new CAA "bona fide service fee" model (effective for 2027 bids, with reporting from July 2028); ride out the specialty/mail-order volume (still ~$87B at OptumRx alone) as a clinical-management services business with thinner but more durable margins; pursue an FTC-Cigna-style no-fault settlement on the remaining insulin complaints. Vertical-integrated players (CVS, Cigna, UNH) can cross-subsidize PBM margin compression from insurance, retail, or provider segments — standalones (Navitus, MedImpact) cannot, so consolidation accelerates. (Ankura CAA 2026 primer)

Red lines

  • Federal extension of Part D delinking to the commercial / ERISA self-funded market — would collapse the cross-segment rebate-arbitrage model and force write-downs at the Health Services segment level. Boards would replace CEOs.
  • Mandatory rebate pass-through to point-of-sale with full per-drug disclosure — destroys the negotiating value of confidentiality, which is the whole instrument PBMs sell to manufacturers.
  • Antitrust-driven structural separation of PBM from insurer or retail pharmacy — i.e., breaking up CVS or UNH. PCMA has consistently fought any such proposal; capital markets would punish severely.
  • Mandatory inclusion of TrumpRx pricing in plan formularies at MFN levels — would route the most profitable specialty book around the PBM entirely.

Leverage

  • Formulary control over ~270M covered lives. A manufacturer that does not pay a competitive rebate can be moved to non-preferred tier or excluded outright — observed in the House Oversight finding of 300 instances of higher-cost-rebate steering. (House Oversight, Jul 2024)
  • Vertical integration with the largest insurers — UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, Cigna Healthcare — gives PBMs implicit veto over whether MFN-priced or TrumpRx products get covered as a benefit at all.
  • Specialty mail-order and limited-distribution networks — the only viable channel for many cold-chain biologics; manufacturers cannot easily bypass without building new logistics.
  • Medicare Part D bid leverage: PBMs run the bids for the largest plan sponsors; CMS depends on their participation to keep premiums stable into the 2026 midterms.

Constraints

  • Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026 (signed Feb 3, 2026) — mandatory Part D delinking, flat bona-fide-service-fee compensation, annual transparency reporting to HHS Secretary beginning July 1, 2028. (Ankura)
  • FTC 6(b) investigation — interim reports July 2024 and Jan 14 2025; insulin administrative complaint Sept 2024; Cigna settled Feb 2026, Caremark/OptumRx litigation ongoing. (FTC Jan 14 2025)
  • State PBM-reform statutes in a majority of states post-Rutledge v. PCMA (2020) — patchwork licensing, anti-steering, pass-through mandates.
  • ERISA fiduciary litigation — Johnson & Johnson, Wells Fargo, and others have been sued by plan participants alleging fiduciary breach in PBM contract selection (2024–2026 dockets).
  • MedPAC ongoing congressional mandate to report on PBM data and recommend further structural changes. (MedPAC Mar 2026 Ch. 13)

Timeline pressures

  • 2026 midterms (Nov 3, 2026) — drug-pricing salience is high; both parties want a PBM scalp, limiting PBM political room to push back overtly.
  • CMS GLOBE / GUARD MFN models (released Dec 2025) — potential 2026 implementation imposes additional mandatory rebates on high-cost single-source drugs, eroding the gross-to-net spread PBMs monetize. (Goodwin, Dec 2025)
  • Medicare Part D 2027 bid cycle — first cycle under CAA 2026 delinking; bids due summer 2026.
  • TrumpRx expansion — May 2026 White House fact sheet expanded the platform to "everyday medicines" beyond the original 43-drug specialty launch. Each expansion wave erodes PBM-managed volume. (White House, May 2026)
  • FTC final 6(b) report — still pending as of May 2026; release would re-energize legislative appetite.

Notes on intra-actor coalitions

This is not a unified actor — it is at minimum four sub-factions: - Vertically-integrated Big 3 (CVS, Cigna, UNH) — can absorb PBM-margin compression via insurance/retail/provider segments. Cigna has already broken ranks by announcing rebate-free commercial pricing (Oct 2025), reading the regulatory tea leaves earliest. - Standalone PBMs (MedImpact, Navitus, Capital Rx, Rightway, Cost Plus) — benefit from delinking and transparency rules that level the playing field; their public positions diverge sharply from PCMA's. - Commercial insurers without captive PBMs (most BCBS plans, Humana's residual book, regional payers) — care about total medical-loss-ratio and premium stability; lower list prices via MFN actively help them, so they are softer opponents of MFN than the integrated PBMs are. - Employer plan sponsors as PBM customers — increasingly demanding pass-through contracts and suing for ERISA breach; this is the customer-side erosion that motivates the Cigna pivot.

The mid-2026 implication: when negotiating with "PBMs," the White House and Pharma both know PCMA's public unity is brittle. Cigna's defection and the standalone PBM faction's tacit support of delinking are the cleavages that make a deal possible.

US Patient Advocacy Organizations

A fragmented chorus rather than a single actor — mass-membership seniors' lobby, disease-specific groups (many quietly pharma-funded), and an independent reformer wing — collectively setting the moral frame of the 2026 US pharma-pricing fight.

Role

"Patient advocacy" is not one seat at the table; it is at least three seats arguing with each other. AARP is the 800-pound gorilla on Medicare drug pricing — a 501(c)(4) with ~38 million members that took public credit for the IRA's negotiation provisions and now polices their implementation. Patients For Affordable Drugs (P4AD) is the small but media-effective reform wing — explicitly refuses pharma money, publishes "front group" exposés, and supplies the pro-negotiation patient-story pipeline to Capitol Hill. That funding is the central analytical fact of this actor: it bends the disease-group wing toward "access and innovation" framing and away from aggressive price-control framing, even when their members would benefit from the latter.

Observable behavior that makes them a distinct actor: they produce the patient stories congressional staff and CMS roundtables actually quote (CMS plans up to 15 patient-focused roundtables in Spring 2026 for IPAY 2028 drugs, Feb 5, 2026), they swing senior voters in midterms (AARP's voter-mobilization apparatus), and they are the only constituency that can credibly speak for "the patient" in op-eds and committee testimony.

EU Reference-Pricing Actors (EMA, G-BA/IQWiG, HAS, NICE, DG SANTE)

The background actors who don't sit at the US-Roche table, but whose prices and methodologies constitute the MFN benchmark — and who will be hit hardest by any spillover.

Role

This is a coalition of seat-adjacent actors. None of them negotiate directly with Roche on US pricing, but every one of them appears in the OECD-7/OECD-11 type basket that the Trump administration's Most-Favored-Nation (MFN) executive order of May 12, 2025 uses to anchor US Medicare/Medicaid drug prices (White House EO, 2025-05-12). The CMS "GENEROUS" model operationalises this by tying Medicaid net prices to the PPP-adjusted second-lowest net price across an eight-country reference basket — UK, France, Germany, Italy, Canada, Japan, Denmark, Switzerland (Sidley, 2025-12-10). Four of the five EU/UK reference-pricing actors profiled here are the price-setters in that basket: G-BA/IQWiG runs Germany's AMNOG benefit assessment and the GKV-Spitzenverband negotiates the Erstattungsbetrag (G-BA, ongoing); HAS issues SMR/ASMR transparency opinions that drive CEPS price negotiations in France (HAS Transparency Committee); NICE sets the UK threshold-based reimbursement frontier that is the global HTA reference standard despite Brexit. EMA (the regulator) and DG SANTE (the policymaker) are different — they don't set prices, but EMA's marketing authorisation gates EU launch and the new Joint Clinical Assessment, which went live January 12, 2025 for oncology and ATMPs under Regulation (EU) 2021/2282, harmonises clinical evidence inputs to the price-setters (European Commission JCA; DIA Global Forum, 2024-08).

Constituency

  • EMA: reports to the Commission, Council, and Parliament; constituency is EU member-state competent authorities and patients/HCPs. Losing constituency = member states bypassing EMA scientific opinions via national derogations.
  • G-BA: self-governance body under SGB V (§91), composed of GKV-SV, KBV, KZBV, DKG, plus patient representatives (non-voting). Constituency = statutory sickness funds (~90% of German insured). IQWiG is its scientific subcontractor. Losing constituency = Bundestag stripping benefit-assessment authority back to the BMG.
  • HAS: independent French public authority funded by social-security levy. Constituency = Assurance Maladie, Ministry of Health, Ministry of Economy (jointly oversee CEPS). Losing constituency = a Ministerial Orientation Letter overriding CT/CEESP opinions — the May 5, 2025 letter already pushed CEPS toward "health sovereignty" and renegotiation of the Master Agreement (Simon-Kucher 2026 LFSS analysis).
  • NICE: arms-length non-departmental public body of the UK Department of Health and Social Care. Constituency = NHS England, devolved administrations, and patient stakeholders. Losing constituency = Treasury/DHSC overriding TA recommendations via political deal.
  • DG SANTE: Commission DG under Commissioner for Health and Animal Welfare; constituency = College of Commissioners, Council EPSCO formation, ENVI/SANT in Parliament. Losing constituency = Council vetoing health initiatives on subsidiarity grounds.

Interests (private)

  • Lower medicine costs for national health systems — the existential driver of every HTA body; binding budget constraints in DE/FR/UK have tightened 2024–2026. Weight: 10/10. (GKV-SV AMNOG portal)
  • Maintain access to innovative therapies — political cost of patient access campaigns (oncology, rare disease) is high. Weight: 8/10. (IQWiG on EU-HTA integration)
  • Preserve credibility of value-assessment methodology — being seen as the global "gold standard" is reputational capital; NICE and G-BA actively defend methodology against political interference. Weight: 9/10. (inferred from HSF Kramer trilogue analysis, 2026-04)
  • Avoid being weaponised in US trade negotiations — strong shared interest; ISPOR and Health Affairs commentary explicitly frame this as a sovereignty concern. Weight: 9/10. (Health Affairs European View, 2026)
  • Protect EU pharma sovereignty / industrial base — Critical Medicines Act and pharma legislation reform make this a Commission priority; HAS in particular has been instructed to factor "health sovereignty" into CEPS pricing. Weight: 7/10. (Consilium CMA press release, 2026-05-12)
  • Protect confidentiality of net prices / MEAs — Germany's Medical Research Act (in force 2025-01-01) enables confidential Erstattungsbetrag specifically to reduce international spillover; this directly clashes with CMS's GENEROUS model demand for ex-US net-price reporting. Weight: 9/10. (Inside EU Life Sciences, 2024-07-12; JS Tindt GENEROUS analysis)

BATNA

National pricing systems continue functioning regardless of any US-Roche deal. G-BA negotiates the Erstattungsbetrag, HAS issues opinions, NICE conducts TAs, EMA grants MAs. Concrete payoff floor: status quo plus the additional insulation already legislated — confidential German Erstattungsbeträge (sunset June 30, 2028), Critical Medicines Act provisions for strategic projects and joint procurement, and JCA harmonisation reducing per-country evidence-generation friction. EU/UK actors lose nothing immediately by walking away from any informal coordination with the US; the IRP scrubbing inside Germany already happened (Pharmaceutical Technology on IRP removal).

Red lines

  • No leakage of confidential net prices into US benchmarks. If CMS GENEROUS demands ex-US net-price reporting from manufacturers, EU payers lose the rebate mechanism that lets them buy access without publicly inflating list prices. G-BA explicitly designed the 2025 confidentiality option to prevent this; crossing the line means undoing the Medical Research Act compromise.
  • No deal that raises EU list or net prices to protect US benchmarks. Politically impossible — every EU health minister would be voted out for "subsidising US patients." HAS's CEESP mandate explicitly anchors negotiations in clinically-relevant comparators, not US references.
  • No erosion of EMA scientific independence in marketing authorisation. A US deal that conditioned EMA decisions on US pricing outcomes would trigger immediate EP/ENVI revolt.
  • No subsidiarity breach by the Commission. Article 168 TFEU reserves pricing/reimbursement to member states; DG SANTE cannot agree to anything that touches national pricing autonomy without Council consensus.

Leverage

  • Market size: ~440M people in EU + UK combined; mandatory market for any global launch.
  • Regulatory gating: EMA's centralised authorisation is mandatory for oncology, ATMPs, orphans — Roche cannot launch in EU without EMA. JCA outcomes feed all national HTAs simultaneously from 2025 onward.
  • HTA methodology export: NICE, G-BA, and HAS methods are copied by Canada (CDA-AMC), Australia (PBAC), Brazil (CONITEC), Japan (C2H). The MFN basket countries are the same countries whose HTA methods set global norms — leverage compounds.
  • Confidential net-price information asymmetry: EU payers know the net price; CMS and the US do not. This asymmetry is the keystone of the entire MFN debate.

Constraints

  • TFEU Article 168: pricing/reimbursement is national competence; Commission cannot harmonise prices.
  • National statutes: SGB V (Germany), Code de la sécurité sociale (France), NHS Act 2006 / Health and Care Act 2022 (UK) — each binds its respective HTA body.
  • JCA Regulation 2021/2282: mandates joint clinical assessment but explicitly preserves national authority on relative effectiveness conclusions and price.
  • EFPIA lobbying capacity: industry has succeeded in softening the pharma legislation revision (8+1+1+1 data exclusivity rather than the Commission's original 6+2; final agreed Dec 11, 2025) (Crowell pharma package alert).
  • Transparency vs confidentiality balance: published list prices remain mandatory in DE/FR; confidentiality only attaches to rebates and (now) German Erstattungsbeträge under MFG.

Biosimilars and Competitor Manufacturers

The countervailing supply-side actor: every biosimilar launch is a public, demonstrable price cut, and they sit in the negotiation as the "if you don't move, we will" benchmark to Roche's biologics franchise.

Snapshot: May 19, 2026.

Covered actors: Sandoz (independent since Oct 2023), Samsung Bioepis, Celltrion, Amgen biosimilars, Teva, Boehringer Ingelheim biosimilars, and the trade vehicles — Biosimilars Forum, Medicines for Europe, and the Association for Accessible Medicines (AAM) / Biosimilars Council.

Role

Biosimilars are the natural countervailing force to Roche's biologics pricing — they have already eroded Avastin, Herceptin, and Rituxan/MabThera since the 2019 US launches of Mvasi and Kanjinti, and they are now bearing down on Xolair (Celltrion's interchangeable Omlyclo cleared to launch September 1, 2026 under a Genentech settlement) and on the future Ocrevus franchise (Amgen's ABP 692 entered Phase 3 in February 2025; Celltrion's CT-P53 and a Samsung Bioepis ocrelizumab are also in the pipeline, with US patent expiry 28 March 2029). In the mid-2026 MFN/TrumpRx negotiation, they sit at the table not as a party Roche bargains with but as the empirical price floor that Roche, CMS, and the White House each cite to discipline brand pricing. Their authority is market-derived (ASP-discounted competition) and statutory (the BPCIA pathway and IRA "bona fide marketing" off-ramp). Observable distinct behavior: their trade associations testify against brand pharma and against PBM rebate steering, not against the Administration's MFN framework.

BATNA

No deal in the MFN/TrumpRx package still leaves biosimilars with a working growth path: - Continued state-level pharmacist substitution wins (49 states now have substitution statutes for interchangeables). - Continued IRA "bona fide marketing" delistings as launches occur — Stelara, Entresto, Xarelto are the proof case from CMS's 2025 announcement. - Continued private-label and contracting workarounds (Blue Shield of California's 90%-off Humira biosimilar deal with Fresenius Kabi via Evio is the template). - Pivot capacity and capital to ex-US launches (Europe, Canada, LATAM, Asia) where launch timelines are shorter and tender mechanics favor multi-source supply. Payoff floor: biosimilar segment continues to compound roughly 30–40% on the Amgen/Sandoz revenue base, plus Omlyclo, Opuviz, Afqlir, and the Stelara biosimilars maturing in 2026–27.

Red lines

  • Any MFN structure that protects brand margins by reducing competitive pressure on brand pricing while leaving biosimilar reimbursement (ASP+6, Part D placement) unchanged. This would let brands quietly hold formulary position while pretending to discount via DTC channels. Constituency reason: biosimilar share growth is the only metric their boards and shareholders track.
  • Congressional weakening of BPCIA interchangeability or 180-day exclusivity — this would gut the substitution economics. Constituency reason: it directly eliminates the legal predicate for state pharmacist substitution.
  • MFN scope creep onto biosimilars themselves — HHS has so far ringfenced biosimilars out; the trade groups will go nuclear if that changes.
  • CMS reading "bona fide marketing" so narrowly that a brand stays on the IRA negotiation list despite a biosimilar launch — collapses the off-ramp the IRA was supposed to provide.

Leverage

  • Empirical, observable price cuts: trastuzumab biosimilars at 52% ASP discount, bevacizumab at 49%, rituximab at 66% (CMS Q1 2025 ASP data via ASPE). When CMS, the White House, or Congress needs a number to cite against brand pharma, the biosimilars supply it.
  • $189bn ten-year savings figure (Biosimilars Council, 2024) — the talking point that anchors every Hill hearing.
  • Existing TrumpRx integration: Amgen, Genentech (Roche's US arm), Boehringer Ingelheim, Pfizer are already on the platform with biosimilar SKUs, which positions biosimilar manufacturers as the proof case that the DTC model "works."
  • IRA bona fide marketing leverage: the trade groups effectively co-write CMS's standard for what counts as a real launch.
  • Manufacturing footprint (Sandoz, Boehringer in particular) gives them a political story aligned with the Administration's reshoring rhetoric.

Constraints

  • BPCIA statutory architecture — 12-year reference exclusivity for the originator, mandatory patent dance, 180-day notice of commercial marketing.
  • FDA review timelines and the still-extant biosimilar/interchangeable bifurcation until S.1954/H.R.5526 passes.
  • Settlement-determined launch dates (Omlyclo: Sept 1, 2026; Opuviz: Jan 2027) — these are contractual ceilings.
  • PBM formulary discretion — even with FDA approval and interchangeability, a private-label biosimilar (Cordavis, Quallent) can crowd out independents.
  • Foreign-state actors (Celltrion, Samsung Bioepis are Korean; CinnaGen Iranian) face geopolitical exposure on MFN reference-price math and tariff/trade rhetoric.

Roche investors (institutional, ADR, index, sovereign, sell-side)

The price-signal actor: no seat at the table, but every Roche move in the US negotiation is graded in real time on the SIX and OTC tape, with the founding-family voting pool acting as a shock-absorber against short-term pressure.

Role

Roche's external shareholders — Swiss and European institutional holders of bearer shares (ROG, voting), holders of the newly-listed Participation Certificates (ROP, non-voting, the successor to the Genussscheine as of 17 March 2026), US ADR holders (RHHBY, OTC), passive index funds tracking the SMI and MSCI Europe, sovereign wealth funds (Norges Bank/GPFG, GPIF, ADIA), Swiss pension and life-insurance funds, and the sell-side analysts (UBS, J.P. They have no formal authority over the Genentech-MFN deal, the Section 232 tariff response, or 2027 price negotiations, but they price every signal within seconds and transmit a P&L verdict back to CEO Thomas Schinecker and chair Severin Schwan. Importantly, because the family-shareholder pool controls a family-held voting majority of voting shares (Bloomberg / Family Business Magazine, 25 March 2011), traditional shareholder-activist mechanics that operate at standalone US biopharma simply do not work here. What remains is share-price feedback, AGM votes on compensation/board, and ESG-stewardship engagement.

Active managers answer to their LPs and fund boards on relative pharma-sector performance; passive index funds (Vanguard, BlackRock, State Street) answer to their tracking error and stewardship teams; Swiss pension funds (Publica, AHV-Suva, BVK) and life insurers (Swiss Life, Zurich) answer to plan members and the Swiss FINMA solvency regime — Roche dividends are a material line in their CHF income; GPFG answers to the Norwegian Ministry of Finance and its ethics council; retail ADR holders are price-takers.
